Reading Arguments
Every source you encounter in AP Seminar is making an argument. Unit 2 teaches you to read those arguments analytically — identifying their structure, exposing their assumptions, recognizing their rhetorical strategies, and evaluating the quality of their reasoning.
THE THREE RHETORICAL APPEALS
ETHOS
Credibility & Character. The author's expertise, institutional affiliation, fairness, and trustworthiness. Establishes the right to speak on a topic.
LOGOS
Logic & Evidence. Data, statistics, logical reasoning, and factual evidence. The most valued appeal in academic writing and AP Seminar research.
PATHOS
Emotion & Values. Appeals to the audience's feelings, identity, and beliefs. Effective when appropriate; manipulative when used to bypass rational evaluation.
Exam tip: Skilled writers use all three appeals simultaneously. Don't stop at identifying one — ask how they work together. The smiling family in a drug ad (pathos) reduces scrutiny of the side effects list (logos). Dr. Chen's WHO title (ethos) makes us accept the uncited 30-40% statistic (logos) without questioning it.
ARGUMENT ANATOMY
Every argument contains: Claim — the debatable assertion being defended; Evidence — specific, accurate support; Reasoning/Warrant — the logical bridge explaining WHY the evidence proves the claim. The warrant is the most commonly missing element. The Qualifier limits scope ('in most cases,' 'tends to'). The Counterclaim acknowledges opposing views. The Rebuttal explains why the original claim holds despite the counterargument.
THE MOST COMMON LOGICAL FALLACIES
AD HOMINEM
Attacking the person rather than the argument. Identity, credentials, or character cannot determine whether an argument is correct.
STRAW MAN
Misrepresenting an opposing argument in a weakened form, then attacking that distortion rather than the actual position.
FALSE DICHOTOMY
Presenting only two options when a spectrum of choices exists. 'Either you're with us or against us.'
SLIPPERY SLOPE
Assuming one action will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without providing evidence for the causal chain.
CORRELATION ≠ CAUSATION
Assuming that because two things occur together, one causes the other. Requires ruling out reverse causation and confounding variables.
CIRCULAR REASONING
Using the conclusion as a premise. 'This law is wrong because it is unjust' — unjust and wrong mean the same thing.
Exam tip: Real arguments often commit multiple fallacies simultaneously. Don't stop at naming the first one you see — ask whether additional errors are operating. Ad hominem and straw man frequently appear together when a writer dismisses an opponent.

Argument Components Quick Reference
CLAIM
Debatable assertion. Takes a position. Not a fact, not a question.
EVIDENCE
Specific, accurate support: data, expert testimony, examples, primary sources.
REASONING/WARRANT
WHY the evidence proves the claim. The most often missing piece in student writing.
QUALIFIER
Limits the scope: 'often,' 'in most cases,' 'tends to.' Appropriate hedging strengthens, not weakens.
COUNTERCLAIM
Acknowledges an opposing view before rebutting or qualifying it. Shows intellectual honesty.
REBUTTAL
Explains why the original claim holds despite the counterargument. Not just a restatement.
