AP SEMINAR · UNIT 3
Evaluating Sources
Finding sources is easy. Evaluating them is the skill. Unit 3 gives you a rigorous framework for asking whether a source can be trusted, what it cannot tell you, whose interests it serves, and how to use it responsibly in an argument.
BIAS VS. LIMITATION
BIAS
A tendency to favor particular conclusions due to perspective, financial interest, or values. Response: acknowledge and corroborate independently.
Examples: industry-funded studies, partisan think tanks
LIMITATION
A structural gap in what a source can reveal based on methodology, scope, or time period. Response: scope your claim appropriately.
Examples: outdated study, non-representative sample, single country
Exam tip: Identify BOTH bias AND limitation. They require different responses: bias requires corroboration and transparent acknowledgment; limitation requires scoping your claim to what the methodology can actually establish.
SOAPSTONE QUICK GUIDE
SPEAKER
Who created this? What institutional role and interests does that create?
OCCASION
What specific moment or event prompted this text's creation?
AUDIENCE
Who was this for, and how does that shape its content?
PURPOSE
What is this trying to DO — inform, persuade, justify, manage controversy?
SUBJECT + TONE
What is it about? What attitude toward subject and audience?
EVIDENCE
What types of support does it use and how effectively?

Source Evaluation Checklist
☑ AUTHORSHIP
Who wrote this? Credentials? Institutional affiliation?
☑ PUBLICATION TYPE
Peer-reviewed? Think tank? News? Blog? Government?
☑ FUNDING
Who funded this? Do they have a financial interest in the outcome?
☑ METHODOLOGY
How was data collected? Sample? Does method match the claim?
☑ BIAS
Tendency toward certain conclusions? Acknowledge + corroborate.
☑ LIMITATION
What can this NOT tell us? Scope claim to what evidence establishes.
Source Type Reference
Primary — original documents, data, speeches from the period
Secondary — analyses, biographies interpreting primary material
Peer-reviewed — expert-evaluated; necessary but not sufficient
Think tank — policy research with institutional perspective
Meta-analysis — synthesizes studies; check inclusion criteria
Government report — authoritative but reflects administration priorities
Wikipedia — starting point only; cite the underlying sources
Internal documents — powerful primary evidence of private knowledge
