AP SEMINAR · UNIT 5
Individual Research & the IRR
The IRR is the culmination of the AP Seminar course — an original, sustained scholarly inquiry that is entirely your own. Unit 5 teaches you to design and execute that inquiry: choosing a question worth asking, collecting evidence rigorously, analyzing what the evidence means, and connecting your findings to something larger than the specific case you studied.
IRR STRUCTURE AT A GLANCE
INTRODUCTION
Research question, significance, overview of approach. The question should be complex, contestable, focused, and significant.
LITERATURE REVIEW
What's known, what's contested, where the gap is. Justifies why your specific inquiry is needed.
METHODOLOGY
How you collected and selected evidence. What types of sources you sought and why this approach fits the question.
ANALYSIS
Your original argument supported by evidence. NOT a source summary — your claims drive each paragraph.
CONCLUSION
Answer the research question. Acknowledge what remains uncertain. Explain broader significance.
IMPLICATIONS
What do these findings mean for related problems, policy, or future research? Extend the analysis outward.
WHAT SEPARATES STRONG FROM EXCELLENT IRRs
ElementStrong IRRExcellent IRR
ArgumentDefensible thesis, solid CER structureOriginal insight that emerges from synthesis -- something no source says but the student constructs
SourcesCredible sources evaluated for bias and limitationSources evaluated and integrated simultaneously as evidence for original claims
ContradictionsAcknowledged and notedUsed analytically to build a more precise, conditionalized understanding
LimitationsAcknowledged at conclusionWoven into analysis as context for scoping specific claims accurately
Core IRR self-check: Does every paragraph open with MY analytical claim? Do my sources serve my argument rather than drive it? Does my conclusion answer the specific research question I asked? Am I honest about what the evidence cannot establish?

Strong IRR Research Question Checklist
☑ COMPLEX
Requires weighing evidence and making analytical judgments, not just finding a factual answer.
☑ CONTESTABLE
Reasonable, informed people who have studied the evidence could reach different conclusions.
☑ FOCUSED
Specific enough to investigate thoroughly: specifies population, intervention, outcome, and context.
☑ SIGNIFICANT
Worth studying; connects to larger questions or problems beyond the specific case examined.
☑ RESEARCHABLE
Evidence actually exists to investigate this question at the level of rigor the IRR requires.
☑ NOT A THESIS
Asks a question; does not pre-suppose or state the answer before research is conducted.
Citation Quick Reference
Direct quotation: quotation marks + in-text citation + bibliography
Paraphrase: in-text citation + bibliography (no quotes, but still required)
Common knowledge: no citation needed (but when in doubt, cite)
Secondary citation: AVOID -- always find and cite the original primary source
Citation laundering: AVOID -- citing multiple secondary sources all citing the same primary
Missing in-text citation with bibliography: still plagiarism -- both required
