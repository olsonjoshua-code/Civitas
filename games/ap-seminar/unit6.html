Academic Discussion Contribution Quality Levels
LevelDescriptionExample
RestatingRepeating what someone else said'Right, so basically what you're saying is...'
AgreeingAffirming without adding analysis'I agree with that -- paid leave is clearly beneficial'
ContributingAdding a new point or evidence'Research from Germany shows...'
AdvancingBuilding on what was said AND opening a new line of inquiry'Building on what you said about costs -- German evidence shows implementation design matters; government-funded vs. employer-mandated produce different employer behavior'
ReframingRevealing the question itself is the wrong frame'We keep arguing whether paid leave is good or bad -- but maybe the more useful question is: for whom, under what design, at what cost?'
SPAR Debate Quick Guide
Opening: specific claim + specific evidence + reasoning
Unexpected point: address it directly with evidence, don't ignore or deflect
Factual error: provide counter-evidence with specificity, explain implications
Assigned position you disagree with: steelman it, find the best evidence
Losing the debate: still make the best available argument with evidence and reasoning
Scored on: argument quality (CER), not confidence, fluency, or volume

AP SEMINAR · UNIT 6
Team Collaboration & Presentation
The skills from Units 1-5 are individual. Unit 6 adds the collaborative dimension: how to combine different research perspectives into a unified argument that is more than the sum of its parts, how to argue spontaneously for any position, and how to advance a group's thinking through genuine intellectual exchange.
THREE AP SEMINAR TEAM COMPONENTS
TMP
Team Multimedia Presentation. Collaborative research on a shared question, divided by research area, synthesized into a unified argument, and presented together. Assessed for team synthesis and individual command.
SPAR
Spontaneous Argumentation. Assigned positions argued in real time. Assesses the ability to construct CER arguments without preparation -- applying argumentation skills flexibly to unfamiliar questions.
ACADEMIC DISCUSSION
Collaborative intellectual exchange. Assessed on quality of contribution, not quantity. Building on others, asking probing questions, advancing inquiry. The most valuable contribution moves the group's thinking forward.
WHAT DISTINGUISHES GENUINE TMP SYNTHESIS
Divided Research (Lower Score)Genuine Synthesis (Higher Score)
Each section is self-contained and could stand aloneEach section needs the others to make the argument complete
Sections are connected by transitions ('Now Member 2 will...')Sections are connected by the argument ('This establishes X, which means Y, which Member 2's research on Z explains')
Evaluator can identify what each member researched but not what the team arguesEvaluator learns something from the combined argument that no single section established
Counterevidence is absent or ignoredCounterevidence is engaged as a counterargument with a specific rebuttal
Key self-check: If the evaluator says 'I can tell what each member found, but I still don't know what the TEAM argues' -- the synthesis work is missing. The team's original analytical contribution must emerge from the combination of all members' research.

Strong IRR Research Question Checklist
☑ COMPLEX
Requires weighing evidence and making analytical judgments, not just finding a factual answer.
☑ CONTESTABLE
Reasonable, informed people who have studied the evidence could reach different conclusions.
☑ FOCUSED
Specific enough to investigate thoroughly: specifies population, intervention, outcome, and context.
☑ SIGNIFICANT
Worth studying; connects to larger questions or problems beyond the specific case examined.
☑ RESEARCHABLE
Evidence actually exists to investigate this question at the level of rigor the IRR requires.
☑ NOT A THESIS
Asks a question; does not pre-suppose or state the answer before research is conducted.
Citation Quick Reference
Direct quotation: quotation marks + in-text citation + bibliography
Paraphrase: in-text citation + bibliography (no quotes, but still required)
Common knowledge: no citation needed (but when in doubt, cite)
Secondary citation: AVOID -- always find and cite the original primary source
Citation laundering: AVOID -- citing multiple secondary sources all citing the same primary
Missing in-text citation with bibliography: still plagiarism -- both required
