Unit 4 Briefing — Transoceanic Interconnections
From 1450 to 1750, the world's oceans became highways. European states — first Portugal and Spain, then the Dutch, French, and British — used new ships and navigation tech to build the first truly global empires. Their voyages triggered the Columbian Exchange, which reshaped every continent biologically, demographically, and culturally. The reasoning skill is overwhelmingly Causation; Topic 4.8 brings Continuity and Change as a focus.
Topic 4.1 — Technological Innovations from 1450 to 1750

Why did long-distance ocean travel suddenly explode in the 15th century? The CED's answer is technology — a cluster of innovations, most of them borrowed from Asian and Islamic sources, that finally made transoceanic sailing practical. The reasoning skill is Causation: technology → exploration → empire.

The key innovations:

MAGNETIC COMPASS
Chinese invention. Allowed ships to determine direction without sight of stars. Spread west via the Silk Roads and Arab traders.
ASTROLABE
Refined by Muslim astronomers; improved by Portuguese for use at sea. Calculated latitude by measuring the angle of the sun or stars.
LATEEN SAIL
Triangular sail (Arab origin). Allowed ships to sail against the wind by tacking — essential for ocean voyages where winds are unpredictable.
STERNPOST RUDDER
Chinese innovation; gave precise steering control even on large ships in heavy seas. A foundation of the new ship designs.
CARAVEL
Portuguese-developed ship combining lateen and square sails. Small, fast, maneuverable. The original "exploration" vessel for Henry the Navigator's African voyages.
CARRACK / GALLEON
Larger three- or four-masted ships designed for long-distance trade and warfare. Could carry heavy cargo, cannons, and large crews. Workhorses of the Spanish treasure fleets.
FLUYT
Dutch cargo ship (late 16th c.). Designed for maximum capacity with minimum crew — the dominant Atlantic trade vessel. Made the Dutch the cheapest shipping operators in Europe.
CANNONS ON SHIPS
Mounting heavy gunpowder artillery on warships. Made European fleets dominant in any naval engagement — key to Portuguese seizure of Indian Ocean trade hubs.
Required Illustrative Examples (4.1): magnetic compass · astrolabe · lateen sail · sternpost rudder · caravel · carrack · galleon · fluyt · ship-mounted cannons · improved cartography · printed maps · diffusion from Asia/Islamic world
AP Exam tip: The CED makes a specific point that these technologies diffused from Chinese, Indian, and Arab origins — Europeans did not invent the compass, the astrolabe, or the lateen sail. They synthesized borrowed technologies into new ship designs. Be ready to argue that European exploration was built on global knowledge networks (Topic 2.5).
Topic 4.2 — Exploration: Causes and Events from 1450 to 1750

Topic 4.2 asks why European states started funding ocean voyages and what happened in the major expeditions. The CED groups causes into five well-known categories — sometimes summarized as "God, Gold, and Glory" but more accurate as:

• Economic motives — bypass Ottoman control of overland routes to Asia; access spices, silk, and gold directly.
• Religious motives — spread Christianity (especially Catholic) to non-Christian peoples; for Spain, continue the Reconquista abroad after 1492.
• Political/state motives — rivalry between European monarchies; royal sponsorship gave kings new revenue and prestige.
• Personal/individual motives — explorers and conquistadors sought wealth, titles, and fame.
• Technological capability — Topic 4.1's innovations made it possible.

The major expeditions the CED expects you to know:

PORTUGAL — leading the way
Henry the Navigator (1394–1460) sponsored voyages down West Africa. Bartolomeu Dias rounded the Cape of Good Hope (1488). Vasco da Gama reached India (1498), opening direct sea trade.
SPAIN — the Atlantic gamble
Christopher Columbus (1492) sailed west for Asia, found the Americas. Ferdinand Magellan's expedition (1519–1522) became the first to circumnavigate the globe — though Magellan died in the Philippines.
CONQUISTADORS
Hernán Cortés conquered the Aztec Empire (1519–21) using indigenous allies, smallpox, and gunpowder. Francisco Pizarro conquered the Inca Empire (1532–33).
NORTHERN EUROPEAN ARRIVALS
John Cabot (England, 1497) reached Newfoundland. Henry Hudson (Dutch and English, early 1600s) explored the river that bears his name. The Dutch, French, and English entered the field a century after Iberia.

The Treaty of Tordesillas (1494) is one of the most-tested events of the unit. With papal mediation, Spain and Portugal divided the non-European world along a meridian in the Atlantic: Spain got everything west (most of the Americas); Portugal got everything east (Brazil, Africa, India, the East Indies). This is why Brazil speaks Portuguese while the rest of Latin America speaks Spanish.

Required Illustrative Examples (4.2): Henry the Navigator · Vasco da Gama · Bartolomeu Dias · Christopher Columbus · Ferdinand Magellan · Hernán Cortés · Francisco Pizarro · Treaty of Tordesillas · Reconquista · "God, Gold, and Glory" · joint funding by monarchs and merchants
Topic 4.3 — The Columbian Exchange

The Columbian Exchange — a term coined by historian Alfred Crosby in 1972 — refers to the massive transfer of plants, animals, diseases, people, and cultures between the Old World (Afro-Eurasia) and the New World (Americas) following Columbus's 1492 voyage. The CED treats this as a turning point in world history. It is one of the single most-tested topics in the entire course.

1. Crops and animals. The exchange transformed diets and ecologies on every continent.

NEW WORLD → OLD WORLD
Maize (corn), potatoes, sweet potatoes, cassava (manioc), tomatoes, peppers, cacao, vanilla, peanuts, tobacco. The high-calorie staples — especially potatoes and maize — fueled massive population growth in Europe, Africa, and China after 1500.
OLD WORLD → NEW WORLD
Wheat, rice, sugarcane, coffee, bananas, citrus, grapes, olives. And animals that didn't exist in the Americas: horses (transformed Plains Indian cultures), cattle, pigs, sheep, chickens.

2. Disease — the catastrophe. The deadliest part of the exchange ran one direction. Old World diseases — especially smallpox, but also measles, influenza, typhus, malaria, yellow fever — encountered American populations with no prior immunity. The result was the largest demographic catastrophe in human history.

Estimates of indigenous American population loss range from 50% to 95% in the first century after contact. In Mexico, the population fell from about 25 million in 1492 to perhaps 1 million by 1600. Smallpox helped Cortés conquer the Aztecs (it killed Moctezuma's successor) and devastated Inca leadership before Pizarro arrived. Disease, not military force, was the decisive factor in European conquest.

Disease ran the other way too: syphilis appeared in Europe c. 1494, possibly carried back by Columbus's crew, and spread rapidly across Eurasia.

3. People — voluntary and forced migrations. The Columbian Exchange moved unprecedented numbers of human beings:

• European settlers — Spaniards, Portuguese, then Dutch, English, French — moved to the Americas. By 1750, perhaps 1.5 million Europeans had emigrated.
• Enslaved Africans — the Atlantic slave trade forcibly transported approximately 12.5 million Africans to the Americas between 1500 and 1866. About 2 million died on the Middle Passage (see Topic 4.5).
• Indigenous Americans — forced relocations, encomienda labor drafts, mit'a-style mining requisitions (the Spanish adapted the Inca mit'a for silver mining at Potosí).

4. Long-term consequences. The Columbian Exchange's effects play out across the rest of the course:

• Population growth in Afro-Eurasia. Potatoes alone dramatically increased the carrying capacity of European agriculture; maize did the same in China and Africa. World population grew from ~480 million in 1500 to ~770 million by 1750.
• The "great dying" in the Americas created labor shortages that drove the Atlantic slave trade.
• New cuisine cultures. Italian food without tomatoes, Indian curry without chili peppers, Irish food without potatoes — all unimaginable today, all post-1500.
• Environmental transformations. Eurasian livestock reshaped American ecosystems; New World crops changed the African landscape.

Required Illustrative Examples (4.3): Columbian Exchange · maize · potatoes · cassava · tomatoes · sugarcane · wheat · horses · cattle · pigs · smallpox · measles · influenza · syphilis · "great dying" · indigenous depopulation · Potosí · encomienda · mit'a (Spanish adaptation) · population growth in Afro-Eurasia
AP Exam tip: The Columbian Exchange shows up on every AP World exam. Have a clear, specific example ready for each direction (NW→OW: potatoes; OW→NW: smallpox), and be ready to explain a cause and effect (smallpox → indigenous depopulation → labor shortages → Atlantic slave trade → African demographic loss). This causal chain is the single most-tested narrative in the unit.
Topic 4.4 — Maritime Empires Established

Topic 4.4 covers how European maritime empires were built. Each major power took a different approach — the CED expects you to compare them.

PORTUGUESE EMPIRE
A trading post empire in the Indian Ocean. Seized strategic ports — Goa, Hormuz, Malacca, Macau — and used naval cannons to force Indian Ocean shippers to buy Portuguese passes (cartaz system). Did NOT try to conquer large territory. Also colonized Brazil for sugar.
SPANISH EMPIRE
A land-based settler empire centered on the Americas. Conquered the Aztec and Inca empires; created the Viceroyalty of New Spain (Mexico) and the Viceroyalty of Peru. Extracted silver from Potosí. Mass conversion to Catholicism.
DUTCH EMPIRE
Pioneered the joint-stock company: the Dutch East India Company (VOC, 1602) was the world's first multinational corporation. Took over Portuguese Asian trading posts; controlled the spice trade in Indonesia. Cape Colony in South Africa.
ENGLISH / BRITISH EMPIRE
British East India Company (1600) built trading posts in India that gradually became territorial control. Settler colonies in North America (Virginia 1607, Massachusetts 1620). Slave-economy colonies in the Caribbean.
FRENCH EMPIRE
Settler colonies in New France (Quebec, Mississippi). Caribbean sugar islands (Saint-Domingue/Haiti, Martinique). Trading posts in India. Smaller scale than English; lost most North American territory after Seven Years' War (1763).
RUSSIA OVERLAND
Russia's empire was technically land-based, but it expanded comparably during this period — across Siberia to the Pacific, driven by the fur trade. Worth comparing to the maritime empires.

Joint-stock companies were a transformative innovation. Investors pooled capital to fund a voyage; profits and losses were shared in proportion. This spread risk across many investors and made it possible to fund expeditions and colonies that no single merchant or king could afford. The Dutch VOC, British East India Company, Dutch West India Company, and British Royal African Company all operated like proto-states — with their own armies, navies, courts, and treaties. The CED treats them as a major innovation in how empires were funded and run.

Required Illustrative Examples (4.4): Portuguese trading-post empire · Cartaz system · Goa · Malacca · Macau · Spanish viceroyalty · Viceroyalty of New Spain · Viceroyalty of Peru · Treaty of Tordesillas · Dutch East India Company (VOC) · British East India Company · French and Indian (settler/trading) empires · joint-stock company · Cape Colony
Topic 4.5 — Maritime Empires Maintained and Developed

Topic 4.5 covers how these maritime empires were sustained — the labor systems, the trade systems, and the economic theory that justified them.

Mercantilism was the dominant economic doctrine of European empires (c. 1500–1750). Its core ideas:

• A nation's wealth = the gold and silver it accumulates.
• Trade is a zero-sum game: one country's gain is another's loss.
• Therefore, maximize exports, minimize imports; achieve a positive trade balance.
• Colonies exist to serve the mother country: provide raw materials, buy finished goods, never compete economically.
• Navigation Acts and similar laws restricted colonial trade to the mother country only.

The Atlantic Slave Trade — the largest forced migration in human history — was the labor backbone of the Atlantic system. The triangular pattern of trade:

EUROPE → AFRICA
Manufactured goods: textiles, metalware, alcohol, guns, beads. African coastal kingdoms exchanged enslaved people for these goods.
AFRICA → AMERICAS (Middle Passage)
Enslaved Africans — ~12.5 million transported, ~2 million died at sea. Conditions on slave ships were among the worst in human history. The Middle Passage is heavily tested.
AMERICAS → EUROPE
Plantation cash crops: sugar (the engine of the system), tobacco, cotton, indigo. And later: gold and silver from Spanish America.
EFFECTS ON AFRICA
Massive demographic loss; gender imbalance (mostly males taken); empowerment of slave-trading coastal kingdoms (Asante, Dahomey) at the expense of inland states. Created lasting structural underdevelopment.

Other coerced labor systems in the Americas:

• Encomienda — Spanish system granting conquistadors the right to indigenous labor and tribute in exchange for "protecting" and Christianizing them. Devastating; many Spanish abuses.
• Hacienda — large landed estates that succeeded the encomienda; used debt peonage — indigenous workers tied to landlords by inherited debt.
• Mit'a (Spanish version) — Spanish adaptation of the Inca labor draft, used to staff Potosí silver mines. Brutal; high mortality.
• Indentured servitude — mostly European or Asian laborers contracted to work for a fixed period (4–7 years) in exchange for passage.
• Chattel slavery — legal status of enslaved Africans and their descendants in the Americas. Hereditary, race-based, and lifelong.

Silver and the global economy. The Spanish discovery of Potosí (Bolivia, 1545) and Mexican silver mines pumped enormous quantities of silver into world circulation. Spanish silver flowed to Europe, then to China — where the Ming dynasty had switched to a silver-based tax system (the Single Whip Reform). By 1600, perhaps a third of all New World silver ended up in China. This created the world's first truly global economy and is one of the CED's flagship examples of how the Atlantic system tied into Asia.

Other global trade hubs: Manila Galleons connected Acapulco (Mexico) to Manila (Philippines), shipping silver west and Chinese silk east. The Spanish "real" became a global currency.

Required Illustrative Examples (4.5): Mercantilism · Navigation Acts · Atlantic Slave Trade · Middle Passage · triangular trade · plantation system · sugar economy · encomienda · hacienda · debt peonage · Spanish mit'a · Potosí · indentured servitude · chattel slavery · Manila Galleons · Single Whip Reform · silver as global currency
AP Exam tip: Topic 4.5 is the single most-tested labor topic on the AP World exam. Be ready to compare two labor systems — e.g., African chattel slavery vs. encomienda, or indentured servitude vs. the mit'a. The College Board specifically wants you to show how labor systems varied by region, time, and demographics.
Topic 4.6 — Internal and External Challenges to State Power

Empires were never simply imposed. Topic 4.6 covers the resistance and challenges that shaped imperial rule.

Indigenous resistance to European empires:

METACOM'S WAR (KING PHILIP'S WAR)
1675–78. Wampanoag chief Metacom (King Philip) led a coalition of Native peoples against English colonists in New England. Devastating to both sides; ended significant Native power in the region.
PUEBLO REVOLT
1680. Pueblo peoples in New Mexico, led by the religious leader Popé, expelled the Spanish from Santa Fe for 12 years. The most successful indigenous revolt of the colonial era.
MARATHA RESISTANCE
Hindu Maratha confederacy resisted Mughal rule, especially Aurangzeb. Eventually became a major regional power in India before British East India Company encroachment.

Slave resistance:

• Maroon societies — communities of escaped enslaved Africans who established autonomous settlements. Examples: Quilombos in Brazil (Palmares lasted ~100 years); maroons in Suriname, Jamaica, and the American South.
• Slave revolts — smaller-scale revolts on plantations across the Caribbean and Brazil; the largest revolts (like Haiti) come in Unit 5.
• Everyday resistance — work slowdowns, breaking tools, preserving African religious and cultural practices in syncretic forms (Vodou, Candomblé, Santería).

Religious challenges to states:

• The Protestant Reformation (Topic 3.3) shattered Catholic unity in Europe and triggered the Thirty Years' War.
• Sikh resistance to Mughal religious orthodoxy under Aurangzeb.
• Confucian scholars in Qing China sometimes resisted Manchu policies through writing and bureaucratic obstruction.

Peasant rebellions against tax pressure and resource extraction recurred everywhere:

• Cossack revolts in Russia (e.g., Pugachev's Rebellion 1773–75 — just past this period but the pattern was earlier).
• Peasant rebellions in late Ming China (which contributed to dynastic collapse and Qing conquest).
• The Glorious Revolution (England, 1688) — a different kind of "challenge to state power": replacement of an absolutist Catholic king by a Protestant constitutional monarchy. Established the idea that parliaments could limit monarchs.

Required Illustrative Examples (4.6): Metacom's War · Pueblo Revolt of 1680 · Maroon societies · Quilombo of Palmares · Sikh resistance · Maratha resistance · Cossack revolts · Glorious Revolution · religious dissent
Topic 4.7 — Changing Social Hierarchies: Class and Race from 1450–1750

Topic 4.7 examines how the new global economy and especially the racialized labor systems of the Atlantic created new social hierarchies. The CED's signature example is the Spanish American sistema de castas — one of the world's earliest formal racial classification systems.

The Spanish casta system in the Americas:

PENINSULARES
Spanish-born colonists; held all top political and church offices. The thinnest top layer of the hierarchy.
CREOLES
American-born people of Spanish ancestry. Wealthy and educated but blocked from top offices — a grievance that fed Latin American independence movements (Unit 5).
MESTIZOS
People of mixed European and Indigenous American ancestry. Middle status; eventually the largest group across most of Spanish America.
MULATTOES
People of mixed European and African ancestry. Status varied by colony; often involved in artisan and merchant work.
INDIGENOUS PEOPLES
Native populations — legally subject to tribute and labor obligations (encomienda, mit'a). The largest group early in the period; devastated by disease.
ENSLAVED AFRICANS
At the bottom of the legal hierarchy. Hereditary chattel slavery defined as a permanent racial status. The economic foundation of the plantation system.

Other social changes:

• The rise of a wealthy commercial bourgeoisie in European cities (especially Amsterdam, London) — merchants, bankers, joint-stock investors with new economic and eventually political power.
• Manchu/Han hierarchy in Qing China — Manchus reserved top military positions and required Han men to wear the queue.
• Ottoman hierarchies — the millet system organized non-Muslim subjects; Janissaries had distinct legal status.
• Russian serfdom intensified in this period — while Western Europe was abandoning serfdom, Russia legally bound its peasantry more tightly to the land. A major divergence.
• In French Saint-Domingue (Haiti), an extreme racialized hierarchy with white, mulatto, and enslaved Black populations — precisely the social structure that exploded in the Haitian Revolution (Unit 5).

Required Illustrative Examples (4.7): Casta system · peninsulares · creoles · mestizos · mulattoes · race-based slavery · commercial bourgeoisie · Russian serfdom (intensified) · Manchu/Han hierarchy · gender and labor changes · syncretic religious cultures (Vodou, Santería, Candomblé)
Topic 4.8 — Continuity and Change from 1450 to 1750

Topic 4.8 is the unit's skills topic. The reasoning process is Continuity and Change Over Time (CCOT); the suggested skill is Argumentation 6.B — support an argument with specific and relevant evidence. There's no new content; the CED asks you to identify what changed and what stayed the same in the 300-year period.

The most-tested CCOT axes:

CHANGES
European maritime dominance (new). Atlantic slave trade (new scale). Columbian Exchange (new). Joint-stock companies (new). Casta system (new). Mercantilism (new). Global silver economy (new).
CONTINUITIES
Coerced labor (continued in new forms — slavery, encomienda, serfdom). Patriarchal family structures (continued). Major religions (continued, though Reformation split Christianity). Land-based empires (Mughal, Qing, Ottoman, Safavid all still dominant).
REGIONAL VARIATION
Western Europe: serfdom ended. Russia: serfdom intensified. Americas: chattel slavery emerged. East Asia: similar economic structures continued. China: silver-based tax system was new.
TRANSITION POINT
By 1750, Europe is positioned to dominate the global economy (Industrial Revolution coming in Unit 5). Atlantic Revolutions and abolition begin. The world is no longer regional; it's global.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks: "Evaluate the extent to which the Atlantic World transformed in the period 1450–1750." Have two specific changes and one specific continuity ready — e.g., changes: Columbian Exchange + Atlantic slave trade; continuity: coerced labor in new forms. Three named, specific examples wins any LEQ on this period.
