Unit 6 Briefing — Consequences of Industrialization
From 1750 to 1900, industrialized European powers, the United States, and Japan used their new technological advantages to dominate the rest of the world. They carved up Africa, dominated Asia, and dictated trade terms to Latin America. They justified imperialism with new racial ideologies and the "civilizing mission." Indigenous peoples resisted with armed rebellions, intellectual critiques, and reform movements — some successful, most defeated. Meanwhile, industrialization triggered the largest mass migration in human history. The reasoning skill is overwhelmingly Causation; Topic 6.8 is the unit's causation/CCOT skills topic.
Topic 6.1 — Rationales for Imperialism

Topic 6.1 asks why industrialized powers expanded so aggressively in this period — and how they justified it. The reasoning skill is Causation.

The "New Imperialism" (c. 1870–1914) differed from earlier European expansion in scale, speed, and intensity. Whereas earlier empires (Unit 4) focused on coastal trading posts and the Americas, the New Imperialism penetrated deep into Africa, Asia, and the Pacific, asserting direct political control.

Five categories of motive recur on the AP exam:

ECONOMIC MOTIVES
Industrial economies needed raw materials (cotton, rubber, palm oil, ivory, copper, oil) and captive markets for finished goods. Colonies provided both. Joint-stock and chartered companies acted as imperial agents.
POLITICAL/STRATEGIC MOTIVES
Great-power rivalry. Britain vs. France, Britain vs. Russia ("the Great Game" in Central Asia), and after 1871 Germany pushing into the colonial competition. Naval bases, coaling stations, control of strategic chokepoints (Suez Canal).
SOCIAL DARWINISM
Misapplication of Darwin's biological theory to human societies. Argued that "fit" races/nations naturally dominated the "unfit." Provided pseudoscientific cover for racial hierarchy and conquest.
"CIVILIZING MISSION" / WHITE MAN'S BURDEN
The ideology that European rule "uplifted" non-European peoples through Christianity, Western education, and "civilization." Rudyard Kipling's "The White Man's Burden" (1899) is the CED's iconic example.
RELIGIOUS MOTIVES
Christian missionaries spread out across Africa and Asia. Often genuinely committed to humanitarian work (medicine, education) but also instruments of imperial expansion. Catholic and Protestant denominations both prominent.
NATIONALISM
Empire as a source of national prestige. Late industrializers like Germany and Italy felt they needed colonies to be "great powers." Public enthusiasm for empire ran high in many European countries.
Required Illustrative Examples (6.1): Social Darwinism · "civilizing mission" · "White Man's Burden" (Kipling) · Christian missionaries · scientific racism · economic motives (raw materials, markets) · political/strategic motives · the Great Game (Britain vs. Russia) · nationalism as imperial motive
AP Exam tip: The CED wants you to be able to distinguish the multiple rationales. A common SAQ asks: "Identify ONE economic motive AND ONE non-economic motive for European imperialism." Have a clear example for each: cotton from India for British mills (economic) vs. Social Darwinism (ideological).
Topic 6.2 — State Expansion: How Empires Were Built

Topic 6.2 covers where and how the new imperial expansion happened. The CED expects you to know the major theaters by region. Industrial technology (steamships, railroads, repeating rifles, machine guns, the telegraph, quinine for malaria) made European penetration of Africa and Asia physically possible for the first time.

The Scramble for Africa (1881–1914). In 1880, only ~10% of Africa was under European rule. By 1914, only Ethiopia and Liberia remained independent. This astonishingly fast partition is one of the most-tested events of the unit.

The Berlin Conference (1884–85), hosted by Bismarck, was a meeting of European powers (no Africans invited) that established the rules for African colonization — effectively dividing the continent on a map. This is the CED's signature event for Topic 6.2.

BRITISH AFRICA
Cape Colony, then expansion north under Cecil Rhodes; Egypt and Sudan controlled to protect the Suez Canal; Nigeria, Kenya, Uganda. "Cape to Cairo" ambition.
FRENCH AFRICA
Algeria (from 1830), West Africa (Senegal, Mali, Cote d'Ivoire), Equatorial Africa, Madagascar. The largest French overseas empire.
BELGIAN CONGO
King Leopold II of Belgium personally controlled the Congo Free State (from 1885) until international outrage over horrific atrocities (forced rubber labor, mutilations, millions of deaths) forced Belgium to take it over (1908). One of the worst atrocities of the imperial era.
GERMAN AFRICA
Tanganyika, Cameroon, Togo, Southwest Africa. Germany committed the Herero and Nama Genocide (1904–08) in modern Namibia — widely considered the first genocide of the 20th century.
ETHIOPIA — UNCONQUERED
Emperor Menelik II defeated invading Italian forces at the Battle of Adwa (1896) — the most famous African victory over a European power in this era. Ethiopia became a symbol of African resistance.
SOUTH AFRICA
British vs. Dutch-descended Boers (Afrikaners). The Anglo-Boer Wars (1880–81, 1899–1902) ended in British victory. Britain pioneered concentration camps for Boer civilians. Set up the racial hierarchy that became apartheid.

Imperialism in Asia — different patterns:

BRITISH INDIA — THE RAJ
After the Sepoy Mutiny / Indian Rebellion (1857), the British government took over from the East India Company and established direct rule (the British Raj, 1858–1947). Queen Victoria proclaimed Empress of India (1877). Cotton, tea, indigo extracted; railways built for imperial use.
QING CHINA — SEMI-COLONY
After the Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60), China was forced into "unequal treaties" — ceding Hong Kong, opening "treaty ports," granting extraterritoriality to foreigners. Spheres of influence carved out by Britain, France, Germany, Russia, Japan, U.S. China was never formally colonized but lost effective sovereignty.
FRENCH INDOCHINA
Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia (annexed 1862–93). Rice exports; rubber plantations. Sets up the 20th-century Vietnam wars.
DUTCH EAST INDIES
Indonesia — the cornerstone of the Dutch empire. Coffee, sugar, rubber. The Cultivation System forced Javanese farmers to grow export crops for the Dutch.
JAPAN AS IMPERIALIST
After Meiji industrialization (Topic 5.6), Japan joined the imperial powers. Defeated China (1894–95) taking Taiwan; defeated Russia (1904–05) — the first time an Asian power defeated a European one in modern history. Annexed Korea (1910). Set up the path to WWII in the Pacific.
U.S. EXPANSION
Manifest Destiny across North America (Mexican-American War, 1846–48). Annexed Hawaii (1898). After the Spanish-American War (1898): Puerto Rico, Guam, the Philippines (Philippine-American War, 1899–1902, was extremely violent). The U.S. became an overseas empire.
Required Illustrative Examples (6.2): Berlin Conference (1884–85) · Scramble for Africa · Cecil Rhodes · Belgian Congo / Leopold II · Battle of Adwa · Anglo-Boer Wars · British Raj · Sepoy Mutiny / Indian Rebellion of 1857 · Opium Wars · unequal treaties · spheres of influence · French Indochina · Dutch East Indies · Sino-Japanese War (1894–95) · Russo-Japanese War (1904–05) · Spanish-American War · Manifest Destiny · annexation of Hawaii

Topic 6.3 — Indigenous Responses to State Expansion

Topic 6.3 is critical to a complete picture of the imperial age. The CED makes clear: colonized peoples did not simply accept imperialism. They resisted in many forms — armed revolts, religious movements, intellectual critiques, and (eventually) organized nationalist movements that would culminate in 20th-century independence.

Armed resistance — major rebellions:

SEPOY MUTINY / INDIAN REBELLION (1857)
Indian sepoy soldiers in the British East India Company army revolted, joined by widespread civilian uprising. Sparked partly by religious offense (rifle cartridges greased with pig and cow fat). Brutally suppressed; led directly to British government taking over rule of India.
XHOSA CATTLE-KILLING (1856–57)
In response to British encroachment in South Africa, the Xhosa prophetess Nongqawuse predicted that killing all cattle and destroying crops would expel the British. Tragically, mass starvation followed, devastating the Xhosa.
MAHDIST WAR (1881–98)
In Sudan, Muhammad Ahmad declared himself the Mahdi (Islamic redeemer) and led a religious-political uprising against Egyptian-British rule. Defeated General Gordon at Khartoum (1885). Eventually defeated by British forces with machine guns at Omdurman (1898).
BOXER REBELLION (1899–1901)
Anti-Christian, anti-foreign uprising in northern China by the "Society of Righteous and Harmonious Fists." Targeted foreign missionaries and Chinese Christians. Crushed by an eight-nation coalition including U.S., Japan, Britain. Further humiliated the Qing.
GHOST DANCE (US, 1890)
Native American spiritual movement (Lakota, Paiute) prophesying the disappearance of whites and the return of the buffalo. Led to the Wounded Knee Massacre (Dec 1890) where U.S. troops killed ~250 Lakota. Effectively ended Native armed resistance in the U.S.
YAA ASANTEWAA WAR (1900)
Yaa Asantewaa, queen mother of Ejisu in the Asante Empire (modern Ghana), led an armed revolt against the British. Defeated, but became a powerful symbol of African women's leadership in resistance.

Reform and intellectual responses:

• Indian National Congress (founded 1885) — began as a moderate organization seeking greater Indian participation in colonial government; eventually became the vehicle of Indian independence (Gandhi joined in 1915).
• Tanzimat Reforms in the Ottoman Empire (1839–76) — tried to modernize legal codes, education, and military to compete with Europe.
• Self-Strengthening Movement in late Qing China (1861–95) — failed reform attempt to adopt Western technology.
• Meiji Restoration in Japan (Topic 5.6) — the most successful indigenous response to Western pressure: rapid state-led modernization that turned Japan into an imperial power itself.
• Sokoto Caliphate — West African Islamic state founded by Usman dan Fodio (1804); religious and political response to declining traditional kingdoms; eventually conquered by the British (1903).

The Cuban Independence Movement (1868–1898) is a CED-required example. José Martí — poet and revolutionary — led the final phase. Cuba's independence was short-circuited by the Spanish-American War (1898), which left Cuba effectively a U.S. protectorate — a foreshadowing of 20th-century U.S. economic imperialism in Latin America.

Topic 6.4 — Global Economic Development

Topic 6.4 examines how industrialization reshaped the global economy. The world became more integrated than ever before — but also more unequal. The basic pattern was an international division of labor:

• Industrial core (Britain, Western Europe, U.S., Japan) produced manufactured goods.
• Periphery (Africa, Asia, Latin America) produced raw materials and cash crops.
• The terms of trade systematically favored the core.

Cash crop economies were imposed across colonized regions. Farmers were pushed (often forced) to grow export crops for the world market instead of food for themselves:

RUBBER
For tires, industrial belts, electrical insulation. Belgian Congo (forced rubber labor with horrific atrocities), Brazilian Amazon, Malaya. Demand exploded with the bicycle and automobile.
PALM OIL & IVORY
West Africa. Palm oil for industrial machine lubrication and soap. Ivory for piano keys, billiard balls, ornaments — devastating elephant populations.
COTTON
Egypt, India, the U.S. South. Fed European textile mills. India had been the world's leading cotton textile producer; under British rule it became a raw cotton exporter — classic deindustrialization.
SUGAR, COFFEE, BANANAS
Latin America (Cuba, Brazil, Central America), the Caribbean. The United Fruit Company dominated banana production in Central America — gave us the term "banana republic."
TEA
India (Assam, Darjeeling), Ceylon (Sri Lanka), China. British demand drove plantation systems with Indian and Chinese labor.
METALS & MINERALS
Copper (Chile, Congo), tin (Malaya, Bolivia), gold and diamonds (South Africa). Often extracted by indigenous and migrant labor under brutal conditions.

Infrastructure — railways, ports, telegraph lines — was built primarily to extract from colonies, not to develop them. Indian railways linked cotton fields to ports for export, not city to city for Indians. Most major colonial railroad networks ran from interior resources to coastal ports. This shaped post-independence economies for the next century.

Refrigeration (commercially viable from 1870s+) was a quiet but transformative tech. It allowed Argentinian beef and Australian/New Zealand lamb to be shipped to Europe, integrating distant pastoral economies into the global food system.

Topic 6.5 — Economic Imperialism

Topic 6.5 asks: how did industrialized states dominate other economies without formal colonization? Latin America after independence is the central case. Politically free of Spain, Latin American countries remained economically dependent on Britain (and later the U.S.) for capital, manufactured goods, and markets.

Mechanisms of economic imperialism:

UNEQUAL TREATIES
Treaties forcing weaker states to accept unfavorable trade terms, low tariffs, and special privileges for foreign businesses. Treaty of Nanking (China, 1842), Treaty of Kanagawa (Japan, 1854), commercial treaties across Latin America.
EXTRATERRITORIALITY
Foreigners not subject to local law. Western citizens in China and Japan were tried only in their own consular courts. A direct affront to sovereignty; a major nationalist grievance.
SPHERES OF INFLUENCE
Geographic regions where one foreign power had exclusive economic privileges (railroad concessions, mining rights). China was carved up into spheres after 1895.
FOREIGN CONTROL OF FINANCE
Egyptian state debt was managed by British and French commissioners after 1876 — Egypt lost financial sovereignty before being formally occupied (1882). Ottoman Empire under similar foreign debt commissions.
GUNBOAT DIPLOMACY
Threat of military force to compel economic concessions. Commodore Perry's "black ships" forcing Japan open (1853). British Royal Navy enforcing trade terms in many regions.
CORPORATE DOMINANCE
Multinational corporations like United Fruit (Latin America) and HSBC (East Asia) shaped local politics, economies, and infrastructure even where their home governments did not formally rule.

Latin America after independence illustrates economic imperialism perfectly. After Bolívar and others won political independence (Unit 5), Latin American nations:

• Borrowed heavily from British banks to build railroads and infrastructure.
• Specialized in single export commodities (Argentine beef, Brazilian coffee, Chilean copper, Cuban sugar) — vulnerable to global price swings.
• Saw foreign companies (especially British and U.S.) dominate key industries.
• Lost political autonomy when they couldn't pay debts — British and U.S. forces intervened repeatedly.

This pattern of political independence with economic dependency shaped Latin America's 20th-century history — and gave us the modern concept of "neocolonialism."

Required Illustrative Examples (6.4–6.5): cash crop economies · raw material extraction · rubber · palm oil · ivory · cotton · sugar · tea · the United Fruit Company · "banana republics" · unequal treaties · Treaty of Nanking · extraterritoriality · spheres of influence · gunboat diplomacy · British financial control of Egypt · Latin American economic dependency · refrigeration · global trade infrastructure
Topic 6.6 — Causes of Migration in an Interconnected World

The 19th century saw the largest migrations in human history to that point. The CED uses a push/pull framework and distinguishes between voluntary and coerced migration.

Push factors — what drove people to leave home:

• Population pressure — from Columbian Exchange crops + medical advances, Europe's population doubled between 1800 and 1900.
• Famine — the Irish Potato Famine (1845–52) drove approximately a million people to emigrate, mostly to the U.S. The CED's iconic example.
• Religious and political persecution — Russian Jews fleeing pogroms; Armenians fleeing Ottoman persecution.
• Land scarcity — Italian, German, and Scandinavian peasants pushed off small farms.
• Industrialization displacing rural labor — especially in China and India.

Pull factors — what drew people to specific destinations:

• Industrial wages — jobs in U.S. and Western European cities.
• Frontier land — the U.S. Homestead Act (1862), Argentine pampas, Australian outback, Canadian prairies.
• Gold rushes — California (1848–55), Australia (1851), South Africa (1886), Klondike (1897). Drew migrants from around the world, especially China.
• Plantation labor demand — replacing slave labor after abolition (see below).

Coerced and semi-coerced migration — the CED gives this special attention because it overlaps with imperialism:

END OF ATLANTIC SLAVE TRADE
Britain abolished its slave trade in 1807, slavery in 1833. The U.S. abolished slavery in 1865 (13th Amendment). Brazil was last in 1888. But labor demand on plantations didn't disappear — it had to be filled.
INDIAN INDENTURED LABOR
Approximately 1.3 million Indians migrated as indentured workers to former slave colonies — Mauritius, Trinidad, Guyana, Fiji, South Africa, Caribbean sugar islands. Contracts of 5–10 years; conditions often abusive. Many never returned.
CHINESE INDENTURED LABOR
Chinese migrants worked on Cuban sugar plantations, Peruvian guano islands, the U.S. transcontinental railroad, and Southeast Asian tin mines. Often under contracts barely distinguishable from slavery.
AFRICAN INTERNAL FORCED LABOR
European colonies forced Africans into rubber gathering (Congo), railway construction, mining. The Belgian Congo's atrocities are the worst-known case.
Topic 6.7 — Effects of Migration

Topic 6.7 examines what these massive movements did — to receiving societies, to sending societies, and to global culture.

Effects on receiving societies:

• Ethnic enclaves — immigrants clustered in neighborhoods preserving language, religion, food, social networks. Chinatowns in San Francisco, Vancouver, Lima. Italian and Polish neighborhoods in U.S. cities. Indian communities in Durban (South Africa) and Trinidad.
• New ethnic and racial hierarchies — settler-colonial societies developed elaborate racial classifications and discrimination.
• Anti-immigrant backlash — the Chinese Exclusion Act (U.S., 1882) was the first major federal law restricting immigration by ethnic group. Australia adopted "White Australia" policies. Reflected fears about wage competition and racial nativism.
• Cultural exchange — new foods (Italian-American pasta, Indo-Caribbean curry), languages, music, religious practices. Diasporic networks linked sending and receiving societies.

Gender effects. Most migrants in this era were men — they went abroad to work, often planning to return. This created female-headed households in sending societies (rural China, southern Italy, parts of India), with women managing farms and family economies in their husbands' absence. When women migrated, they often worked as domestic servants, factory workers, or in the textile industry.

Effects on sending societies:

• Remittances — money sent home by migrants. Significant in Italy, China, Ireland, India.
• Brain drain — loss of educated and skilled labor.
• Demographic and gender imbalances — villages with few young men.
• Diasporic networks — later facilitated nationalist movements (Indian, Chinese, Irish nationalism all had strong overseas components).

Required Illustrative Examples (6.6–6.7): Irish Potato Famine · Italian emigration · Chinese diaspora · Indian indentured labor · Chinese indentured labor · ethnic enclaves · Chinatowns · Chinese Exclusion Act · "White Australia" policy · push/pull factors · remittances · gold rushes (California, Australia, South Africa) · railroad construction labor · plantation labor
Topic 6.8 — Causation in the Imperial Age

Topic 6.8 is the unit's skills topic. Reasoning process: Causation. Suggested skill: Argumentation 6.B — support an argument with specific and relevant evidence. The CED asks you to weigh the multiple causes and effects of imperialism.

Most-tested causation chains:

INDUSTRIALIZATION → IMPERIALISM
Industry needed raw materials and markets → pressure for colonial expansion → New Imperialism. The CED's #1 causation chain for this unit.
IMPERIALISM → MIGRATION
Colonial economic systems generated labor demand → Indian and Chinese indentured workers → new global ethnic communities and resistance to immigration.
IMPERIALISM → NATIONALISM
Colonial education and Western ideas → indigenous intellectuals (Indian National Congress, José Martí) → nationalist movements that would win independence in the 20th century.
IMPERIAL RIVALRY → WORLD WAR I
Late-19th century scramble for colonies + alliance system + nationalism → tensions that exploded in 1914. The transition to Unit 7.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks: "Evaluate the extent to which industrialization changed the global economy in the period 1750–1900." Have two specific examples of change (cotton: India deindustrializes / Britain industrializes; rubber: from rare commodity to global mass-extraction industry) and one continuity (coerced labor in new forms — Indian and Chinese indentured workers replacing African slaves). Three named, specific examples wins any LEQ on this period.
