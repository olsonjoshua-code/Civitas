Unit 9 Briefing — Globalization
The final unit covers your present. Since 1900 — especially since 1991 — technology, trade, migration, and ideas have moved across borders at unprecedented speed. The internet, multinational corporations, climate change, pandemics, global popular culture: all are products of globalization. The CED organizes this content thematically rather than chronologically, with 9 topics spanning technology, environment, economy, society, culture, and resistance. Topic 9.9 is the unit\'s explicit causation/CCOT skills topic. Welcome to the world you actually live in.
Topic 9.1 — Advances in Technology and Exchange

The 20th century saw faster technological change than any previous era. Every domain of human life was transformed.

TRANSPORTATION
Airplanes (1903 Wright brothers; commercial aviation post-WWII); shipping containers (1956) revolutionized global trade; high-speed rail (Japan\'s Shinkansen 1964); space travel.
COMMUNICATION
Radio (1920s), television (1950s), satellites (Sputnik 1957), personal computer (1980s), internet (1990s), smartphones (2007+). Information now travels instantly worldwide.
ENERGY
Petroleum (geopolitical centerpiece of the 20th century), nuclear power (from 1950s), renewable energy (solar, wind, increasingly affordable post-2000).
MEDICINE
Penicillin (Fleming, 1928 — mass-produced WWII); vaccines (smallpox eradicated 1980; polio nearly eliminated); antiretrovirals (HIV/AIDS treatment).
GREEN REVOLUTION
1940s–60s. Norman Borlaug developed high-yield wheat varieties. Combined with fertilizers and irrigation, dramatically increased food production in Mexico, India, Pakistan. Saved an estimated billion people from famine.
BIRTH CONTROL TECH
Oral contraceptive ("the Pill," approved 1960). Transformed women\'s lives, family size, women\'s workforce participation. Combined with broader reproductive rights movements.
Required Illustrative Examples (9.1): shipping container · personal computer · internet · cell phones · satellite communication · Green Revolution · Norman Borlaug · oral contraceptive (the Pill) · vaccines · antibiotics · jet airplanes
Topic 9.2 — Disease & Public Health

Globalization has both helped fight disease (vaccines, WHO) and accelerated its spread (jet travel, urbanization).

Major 20th and 21st-century pandemics:

• 1918–19 Spanish Flu — killed an estimated 50–100 million globally. Worse than WWI itself. Spread by WWI troop movements.
• HIV/AIDS — emerged 1980s. Devastating in sub-Saharan Africa; ~40 million dead globally. Antiretrovirals (1996) transformed treatment but inequality persists.
• SARS (2003), H1N1 (2009), Ebola (West Africa 2014–16).
• COVID-19 (2020+) — ~7+ million confirmed deaths globally; massive economic and social disruption.

Public health responses:

• World Health Organization (WHO) — UN agency (1948). Coordinated smallpox eradication (declared 1980), one of humanity\'s greatest public health achievements.
• Doctors Without Borders (Médecins Sans Frontières) — founded 1971. Delivers medical care in conflict zones and crises.
• Diseases of affluence — heart disease, diabetes, cancer increased as life expectancy rose globally. Replaced infectious diseases as leading killers in wealthy countries.
• Global health inequality — treatments available in wealthy countries often unavailable elsewhere.

Required Illustrative Examples (9.2): 1918 Spanish Flu · HIV/AIDS · COVID-19 · smallpox eradication · WHO · Médecins Sans Frontières · diseases of affluence (heart disease, cancer, diabetes) · Ebola · vaccines
Topic 9.3 — Technology and the Environment

Industrialization\'s environmental costs became impossible to ignore in the late 20th century.

CLIMATE CHANGE
Burning fossil fuels released CO₂ into the atmosphere; global temperatures rising. Scientific consensus by 1990s. The defining environmental challenge of the 21st century.
POLLUTION
Air pollution (especially in industrializing China and India), water contamination, plastic in oceans. Bhopal disaster (India, 1984) and Chernobyl (USSR, 1986) became iconic disasters.
DEFORESTATION
Amazon, Indonesian, Central African rainforests under pressure from logging, ranching, palm oil. Loss of biodiversity and carbon sinks.
RESOURCE DEPLETION
Overfishing, water shortages (Aral Sea collapsed by Soviet irrigation), groundwater depletion. Limits to growth becoming visible.
ENVIRONMENTAL MOVEMENT
Rachel Carson\'s Silent Spring (1962) launched modern environmentalism. First Earth Day (1970). National environmental protection agencies founded; international treaties began.
CLIMATE AGREEMENTS
Kyoto Protocol (1997), Paris Agreement (2015). International efforts to limit emissions. Implementation has been uneven; emissions continue to rise globally.
Required Illustrative Examples (9.3): climate change · global warming · deforestation · pollution · Aral Sea · Bhopal disaster · Chernobyl · ozone layer (Montreal Protocol) · Kyoto Protocol · Paris Agreement · environmental movements · Rachel Carson / Silent Spring · Earth Day
Topic 9.4 — Economics in the Global Age

The late 20th century saw a major economic shift: from Keynesian/state-led economies (mid-century norm) to neoliberalism (free markets, deregulation, privatization).

Key shifts:

• Neoliberalism — Ronald Reagan (US) and Margaret Thatcher (UK) led 1980s deregulation, tax cuts, privatization. Spread globally as the "Washington Consensus."
• Chinese economic reforms — Deng Xiaoping opened China to market forces from 1978. China became the world\'s factory by 2000s; lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty.
• Soviet collapse → market transitions — Russia and Eastern Europe converted from communism to capitalism (often chaotically) in the 1990s.
• Free-trade agreements — NAFTA (US, Canada, Mexico, 1994), European Union (1993), Mercosur (S. America), ASEAN (SE Asia).
• Multinational corporations — Apple, Google, Toyota, Samsung, Nestlé, Walmart. Operate across multiple countries; sometimes more economically powerful than small nations.

Global financial institutions:

WORLD TRADE ORGANIZATION (WTO)
Founded 1995. Regulates international trade; resolves disputes; promotes free trade. Replaced GATT (1947). 164 member states by 2025.
INTERNATIONAL MONETARY FUND (IMF)
Founded 1944 (Bretton Woods). Lends to countries in financial crisis with strict economic conditions ("structural adjustment"). Often controversial in developing countries.
WORLD BANK
Founded 1944. Provides loans for development projects in poorer countries. Like IMF, criticized for imposing market reforms as conditions.
2008 FINANCIAL CRISIS
U.S. housing-market collapse triggered global recession. Exposed risks of unregulated finance. Led to some banking reform but globalization continued.

Knowledge economy — in wealthy countries, services, information, and finance replaced manufacturing as the primary economic activity. Manufacturing shifted to lower-wage countries: first Japan/Korea/Taiwan (1960s–80s), then China (1990s–2010s), now Vietnam, Bangladesh, Mexico.

Rise of Asian economies — "Asian Tigers" (Hong Kong, Singapore, South Korea, Taiwan) industrialized rapidly in 1960s–90s. India\'s 1991 economic liberalization; tech sector boom in Bangalore. China became 2nd largest economy in 2010.

Required Illustrative Examples (9.4): neoliberalism · Reagan · Thatcher · Deng Xiaoping reforms · NAFTA · European Union · WTO · IMF · World Bank · multinational corporations (Apple, Toyota, Walmart) · Asian Tigers · 2008 financial crisis · knowledge economy · outsourcing
Topic 9.5 — Calls for Reform and Responses

Reform movements expanded in this period across multiple axes:

FEMINISM
Second-wave (1960s–80s) and third-wave (1990s+). Equal pay, reproductive rights, workplace equality, ending domestic violence. UN Decade for Women (1975–85).
CIVIL RIGHTS & ANTI-RACISM
U.S. Civil Rights Movement; anti-apartheid in South Africa; Black Lives Matter (2013+). Ongoing struggles against systemic racism.
LGBTQ+ RIGHTS
Stonewall (1969). Decriminalization of homosexuality across most of the West (and many countries elsewhere). Marriage equality in many countries from 2000s+.
INDIGENOUS RIGHTS
UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007). Land claims, language preservation, opposition to extractive industries.
DISABILITY RIGHTS
U.S. Americans with Disabilities Act (1990); UN Convention on Rights of Persons with Disabilities (2006).
YOUTH MOVEMENTS
Climate activism (Greta Thunberg, Fridays for Future), gun control activism, education access. Social media has accelerated youth political mobilization.
Topic 9.6 — Globalized Culture

Global cultural exchange accelerated dramatically. The traffic flows multiple directions:

Westernization / "Americanization" — U.S. brands and media (Hollywood, McDonald\'s, Coca-Cola, Disney, Apple) dominate global markets. English became the global lingua franca of business, science, and the internet. Sometimes resented as cultural imperialism.

Reverse flows — the "non-Western" cultures going global:

• Bollywood — Indian film industry produces more films than Hollywood; massive following in South Asia, Middle East, parts of Africa.
• K-pop — Korean pop music (BTS, Blackpink) became a global phenomenon in the 2010s.
• Anime & manga — Japanese cultural exports globally beloved.
• Soccer/football — the world\'s most popular sport; FIFA World Cup the biggest sporting event globally.
• Latin music — reggaeton, Latin pop have become global.
• Cuisine — sushi, Indian curry, Mexican tacos, Korean BBQ all globalized.

The internet and social media created instant global cultural networks. YouTube (2005), Facebook (2004), Twitter/X (2006), Instagram (2010), TikTok (2016) all reshaped how culture spreads.

Required Illustrative Examples (9.5–9.6): feminism · civil rights movements · LGBTQ+ rights · Indigenous rights · McDonald\'s · Coca-Cola · Disney · Hollywood · Bollywood · K-pop · soccer/football · English as global language · Greta Thunberg · social media
Topic 9.7 — Resistance to Globalization

Globalization has provoked diverse forms of resistance.

RELIGIOUS FUNDAMENTALISM
Movements asserting traditional religious values against secular globalization. Iranian Revolution (1979), evangelical Christianity, Hindu nationalism in India, Islamic revivalist movements globally.
TERRORISM
Al-Qaeda\'s 9/11 attacks (2001) on the World Trade Center and Pentagon killed ~3,000. Triggered U.S. War on Terror, invasions of Afghanistan and Iraq. Reshaped 21st-century geopolitics.
ANTI-GLOBALIZATION PROTESTS
Seattle WTO protests (1999), Genoa G8 protests (2001). Coalitions of labor unions, environmentalists, anti-poverty activists challenging neoliberal globalization.
RIGHT-WING NATIONALISM
Anti-immigration, anti-trade, "America First" / Brexit / European far right. Defines much 21st-century politics. Reaction against globalization\'s economic dislocations.
CULTURAL PRESERVATION
UNESCO World Heritage Sites (1972+); language revival movements (Welsh, Hawaiian, Maori); indigenous-led efforts to protect traditional knowledge and practices.
ALTERNATIVE ECONOMICS
Fair Trade movement; microfinance (Grameen Bank, Muhammad Yunus); local food/buy-local movements; cooperative enterprises.
Required Illustrative Examples (9.7): Iranian Revolution · Al-Qaeda · 9/11 attacks · War on Terror · Taliban · ISIS · religious fundamentalism · anti-globalization protests (Seattle WTO) · Brexit · right-wing populism · Fair Trade · microfinance · Grameen Bank
Topic 9.8 — Institutions in a Globalized World

Topic 9.8 covers the global institutions trying to manage globalized challenges.

UNITED NATIONS
Founded 1945. UN General Assembly, Security Council (5 permanent + 10 rotating), specialized agencies (WHO, UNICEF, UNESCO, UNHCR). Imperfect but indispensable.
REGIONAL ORGANIZATIONS
European Union (1993), African Union (2002), ASEAN (1967), Mercosur (1991). Provide regional cooperation and economic integration.
INTERNATIONAL CRIMINAL COURT
Founded 2002 by the Rome Statute. Prosecutes genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity. U.S., China, Russia not members.
NGOs
Non-governmental organizations — Amnesty International, Greenpeace, Red Cross, Oxfam. Provide advocacy and aid outside government channels.
CLIMATE COOPERATION
UN Framework Convention on Climate Change (1992), Kyoto Protocol (1997), Paris Agreement (2015). Annual COP (Conference of Parties) negotiations continue.
G7 / G20 / BRICS
Coordinating groups of major economies. G7 (Western powers), G20 (broader), BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa — emerging powers).
Required Illustrative Examples (9.8): United Nations · European Union · NAFTA · ASEAN · African Union · WTO · IMF · World Bank · International Criminal Court · Amnesty International · Greenpeace · Doctors Without Borders · NGOs
Topic 9.9 — Continuity and Change in a Globalized World

Topic 9.9 is the unit\'s — and the entire course\'s — final skills topic. Reasoning process: Continuity and Change Over Time. Suggested skill: Argumentation 6.B.

Most-tested CCOT axes:

CHANGES
Internet and instant global communication (new). Climate change as a global crisis (new). End of formal colonial empires. Rise of multinational corporations. Knowledge economy. Global popular culture.
CONTINUITIES
Global inequality (Global North / Global South). Imperialism in new forms (economic neocolonialism). Religious and ethnic conflict. Patriarchy continues in many forms. Migration patterns shaped by old colonial relationships.
REGIONAL VARIATION
East Asia (especially China) rising rapidly. Africa: economic growth alongside continuing challenges. Latin America: democratization but inequality persists. Middle East: continuing conflict.
CONNECTIONS BACK TO UNIT 1
Today\'s globalization echoes the trade networks of 1200–1450 (Unit 1–2): Silk Roads → container ships, Indian Ocean → financial markets. The world has been globalized before, just on different scales and at different speeds.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks: "Evaluate the extent to which technology changed the world from 1900 to the present." Have two specific changes (internet creating instant global communication; Green Revolution dramatically increasing food production) and one continuity (technological gaps maintaining global inequality — e.g., the digital divide) ready. Three named, specific examples wins any LEQ on this period.
