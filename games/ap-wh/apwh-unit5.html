Unit 5 Briefing — Revolutions
From 1750 to 1900, the world transformed in two simultaneous waves. Political revolutions (American, French, Haitian, Latin American) overthrew old regimes, inspired by Enlightenment ideas about natural rights, popular sovereignty, and the social contract. The Industrial Revolution, beginning in Britain, reshaped economies, technologies, and daily life across the globe. The reasoning skill is Causation for most topics; Continuity and Change for Topic 5.10.
Topic 5.1 — The Enlightenment

The Enlightenment was an 18th-century European intellectual movement that emphasized reason, individualism, natural rights, and scientific inquiry. It built on the earlier Scientific Revolution (Copernicus, Galileo, Newton) and applied scientific reasoning to politics, society, religion, and economics. Its core argument: human beings can use reason to understand and improve the world.

The CED expects you to know the following ideas more than the names of philosophers (though both help):

NATURAL RIGHTS
Rights granted to all people by nature/God, not by governments. Cannot be denied. John Locke: life, liberty, and property. The intellectual foundation of the American Declaration of Independence.
SOCIAL CONTRACT
Government legitimacy comes from consent of the governed. People create governments to protect rights; if government fails, people can replace it. Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau all argued versions.
POPULAR SOVEREIGNTY
The people are the ultimate source of political authority. Direct attack on the divine right of kings. Rousseau's "general will."
SEPARATION OF POWERS
Montesquieu: divide government powers among branches (executive, legislative, judicial) to prevent tyranny. Foundation of the U.S. Constitution.
RELIGIOUS TOLERATION
Voltaire attacked religious persecution and clerical power. Most Enlightenment thinkers were Deists (believed in a Creator but not in revealed religion).
ECONOMIC LIBERALISM
Adam Smith (Wealth of Nations, 1776) attacked mercantilism and argued for free markets. Foundation of capitalism.

Voices for expanded rights. The CED specifically lists female and abolitionist voices — the College Board wants the Enlightenment understood as a contested movement, not just white European men:

• Mary Wollstonecraft — A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792) argued women deserved equal education and rational citizenship. Foundational feminist text.
• Olympe de Gouges — Declaration of the Rights of Woman and of the Female Citizen (1791), responding to the French Declaration of the Rights of Man, which excluded women. Executed during the Reign of Terror.
• Abolitionist movements — Enlightenment natural rights logic was used by figures like William Wilberforce (Britain), Olaudah Equiano (formerly enslaved abolitionist), and the Quakers to argue against slavery. Britain abolished the slave trade in 1807, slavery itself in 1833.
• Religious revivals — the Enlightenment didn't kill religion. The Second Great Awakening in the U.S. and Methodism in Britain emphasized personal salvation and often supported abolition and women's rights.

Required Illustrative Examples (5.1): natural rights · social contract · popular sovereignty · separation of powers · religious toleration · economic liberalism · John Locke · Voltaire · Rousseau · Montesquieu · Adam Smith · Mary Wollstonecraft · Olympe de Gouges · William Wilberforce · Olaudah Equiano · Second Great Awakening · Deism
AP Exam tip: The CED is unusually specific that Enlightenment philosophers are not required by name in the Learning Objectives. What's required is the ideas and the political revolutions they inspired (Topic 5.2). Know natural rights, social contract, and popular sovereignty cold — even if you forget who wrote them.
Topic 5.2 — Nationalism and Revolutions

Topic 5.2 is one of the largest single content blocks in the CED. The College Board names specific revolutions and documents as Required Illustrative Examples. Master these four: American, French, Haitian, Latin American — plus nationalist unifications (Italy, Germany).

The American Revolution (1775–1783) — the first successful colonial revolt against a European power. Causes the CED expects:

• Enlightenment ideas (natural rights, popular sovereignty) widely read among educated colonists.
• Salutary neglect → tighter British control after the Seven Years' War (1756–1763). Britain needed to pay war debts; passed the Stamp Act (1765), Tea Act (1773), etc.
• "No taxation without representation" — colonists insisted Parliament couldn't tax them since they had no MPs.
• Boston Tea Party (1773), Lexington and Concord (1775).
• Thomas Paine's Common Sense (1776) made the case for republican independence.

The Declaration of Independence (1776), drafted by Thomas Jefferson, is the CED's signature document for this revolution. It explicitly invoked Locke: "all men are created equal... endowed... with certain unalienable Rights... Life, Liberty, and the pursuit of Happiness." France joined the war (1778); Britain surrendered at Yorktown (1781).

Outcome: the U.S. Constitution (1787) created a republic with separation of powers, a Bill of Rights (1791), and an elected presidency — though slavery continued and women, indigenous peoples, and Black Americans had no political rights.

The French Revolution (1789–1799) — the most influential of all modern revolutions. Causes:

• The Three Estates system: Clergy (1st), Nobility (2nd), Everyone Else (3rd Estate, ~97% of population). The first two paid no taxes; the third bore the entire fiscal burden.
• Massive royal debt from supporting the American Revolution and Versailles luxury under Louis XVI.
• Bread shortages — bad harvests (1788) doubled bread prices.
• Enlightenment ideas circulating widely; American Revolution as an inspiring model.

The Estates-General convened in 1789 for the first time since 1614. The Third Estate broke away as the National Assembly, took the Tennis Court Oath (June 1789), and the Parisian crowd stormed the Bastille (July 14, 1789).

The Assembly produced the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789) — the CED's required document for this revolution. "Men are born and remain free and equal in rights." Direct echo of the American Declaration.

Phases of the French Revolution:

CONSTITUTIONAL MONARCHY (1789–92)
National Assembly limits royal power; Louis XVI tries to flee (1791). Constitution of 1791.
RADICAL REPUBLIC (1792–94)
King executed (Jan 1793). The Reign of Terror under Robespierre — ~17,000 executed by guillotine. Olympe de Gouges executed.
DIRECTORY (1795–99)
More moderate government after Robespierre's fall (he was guillotined himself in 1794). Plagued by corruption and instability.
NAPOLEON (1799–1815)
General Napoleon Bonaparte seized power (1799). The Napoleonic Code (1804) codified equality before the law (for men). Spread Enlightenment legal reforms across Europe through conquest. Defeated at Waterloo (1815).
The Haitian Revolution (1791–1804)

The Haitian Revolution is the CED's most-tested revolution. Why? Because it was:

• The only successful slave revolt in modern history.
• The world's first independent Black republic.
• The first nation in the Americas to abolish slavery.
• A direct application of the French Declaration of the Rights of Man — logic the French government had refused to apply to its own enslaved colonies.

The colony of Saint-Domingue (the French western half of Hispaniola) was the wealthiest sugar colony in the world — producing roughly half of Europe's sugar and coffee. Its racialized social hierarchy was extreme: ~30,000 white colonists at top, ~30,000 mixed-race "free people of color" (some wealthy slaveholders themselves), and ~500,000 enslaved Africans at the bottom.

News of the French Revolution reached Saint-Domingue in 1789. Free people of color demanded the rights they were promised; whites refused. In 1791, enslaved Africans launched a massive uprising under Toussaint Louverture, a formerly enslaved man and brilliant military commander.

The revolution proceeded against staggering odds — against French planters, then against Spanish and British armies, then against Napoleon's invading expedition (1802–03). Toussaint was captured by the French and died in a French prison (1803). His successor Jean-Jacques Dessalines defeated Napoleon's forces and declared Haitian independence on January 1, 1804.

Effects:

• Crippled French colonial ambitions in the Americas — led directly to Napoleon selling the Louisiana Purchase (1803) to the U.S.
• Inspired enslaved peoples across the Americas — and terrified slaveholders. After Haiti, U.S. and Latin American slaveholders worked harder to prevent revolts.
• Haiti was diplomatically isolated by slaveholding powers; France forced Haiti to pay massive "reparations" to former slaveholders (debt finally paid off in 1947), crippling Haiti's economy for over a century.

Required Illustrative Examples (Haitian): Saint-Domingue · Toussaint Louverture · Jean-Jacques Dessalines · 1804 declaration of independence · first independent Black republic · effect on Latin American revolutions · Louisiana Purchase
Latin American Revolutions (1810–1825)

Latin America's wars of independence followed when Napoleon invaded Spain (1808), creating a power vacuum. Spanish-American creoles — American-born descendants of Spanish settlers, the wealthy class blocked from top positions by peninsulares — led the independence movements.

SIMÓN BOLÍVAR ("THE LIBERATOR")
Venezuelan creole; led independence in Venezuela, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Bolivia. Wrote the Letter from Jamaica (1815) — the CED's required Latin American document — arguing for independent republics inspired by Enlightenment ideas. Hoped (and failed) to unify Spanish America into one nation.
JOSÉ DE SAN MARTÍN
Argentine creole general; liberated Argentina, Chile, and Peru. Met Bolívar at Guayaquil (1822); turned over command and retired to Europe.
MIGUEL HIDALGO & JOSÉ MORELOS
Mexican priests who launched indigenous and mestizo uprisings (1810–1815). Both executed; revolution succeeded under more conservative leaders — Mexican independence (1821).
DOM PEDRO & BRAZILIAN INDEPENDENCE
Brazil's path was different. Portuguese royal family fled to Brazil during Napoleon's invasion. When pressed to return, Crown Prince Pedro declared Brazilian independence (1822) — as a monarchy, not a republic. Slavery continued in Brazil until 1888.

Nationalism — the idea that a "nation" (a people sharing language, culture, history) should have its own state — emerged powerfully in this period. It both built up new states and broke down old empires.

ITALIAN UNIFICATION (1815–71)
Giuseppe Garibaldi, Camillo Cavour, and King Victor Emmanuel II unified the Italian peninsula from many small states into the Kingdom of Italy.
GERMAN UNIFICATION (1864–71)
Prussian Chancellor Otto von Bismarck used three wars (against Denmark, Austria, France) to unify the German states under Prussian leadership. Created the German Empire (1871) — transformed European balance of power.
BALKAN NATIONALISMS
Greek independence from Ottomans (1830). Serbian, Bulgarian, and Romanian movements weakened the Ottoman Empire (the "sick man of Europe"). Set up the powder keg that became WWI.
PROPELLO REVOLUTIONS OF 1848
Wave of liberal/nationalist revolutions across Europe — Paris, Vienna, Berlin, Hungary, Italy. Almost all failed in the short term, but spread ideas that won eventually.
Required Illustrative Examples (5.2 documents): American Declaration of Independence · French Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen · Bolívar's Letter from Jamaica
Required Illustrative Examples (5.2 movements): American · French · Haitian · Latin American revolutions · Italian unification · German unification · Balkan independence · 1848 Revolutions · Mexican independence · Brazilian independence
Topic 5.3 — The Industrial Revolution Begins

The Industrial Revolution — the transition from hand-production economies to machine-driven, factory-based ones — began in Britain c. 1750. It is one of the most transformative developments in human history. The CED's central question is: why Britain, why first, and why then?

The CED's answer (causes):

• Coal — Britain had abundant, easily accessible coal — the fuel of industrialization.
• Iron — raw material for machines, tools, and railroads.
• Capital — profits from the Atlantic slave trade, sugar, and Indian textiles funded factories.
• Labor — the Enclosure Movement pushed peasants off common lands; they migrated to cities for factory work.
• Markets — British colonies (especially India) were captive markets for British manufactured goods.
• Property rights — British legal protection of patents and inventions encouraged innovation.
• Geography — rivers for water power, then transport; long coastline for shipping.

Key innovations of the early Industrial Revolution — mostly in textiles and steam:

• Spinning jenny (Hargreaves, 1764), water frame (Arkwright, 1769), spinning mule (Crompton, 1779) — mechanized cotton spinning.
• Power loom (Cartwright, 1785) — mechanized weaving.
• James Watt's steam engine (1769, improved 1770s) — the iconic invention. Allowed factories to be located anywhere, not just by rivers.
• The factory system itself — concentrating workers and machines in one location, replacing the older "putting-out" (cottage) system.

Cotton textiles were the leading sector. By 1830, British cotton mills produced cloth so cheaply that they destroyed Indian handloom weaving (which had been the world's leading textile industry for centuries) — a stunning reversal of the global economic order.

Topic 5.4 — Industrialization Spreads

From Britain, industrialization spread — in waves, with regional variation. The CED expects you to know which countries industrialized when, and why.

FIRST WAVE (1820s–60s)
Belgium, France, the United States, the German states — states with similar resources and political stability. Britain tried to prevent technology export but failed.
SECOND WAVE (1870s–90s)
Japan, Russia — states that industrialized through state-led "catch-up" programs (see Topic 5.6). Japan's Meiji Restoration is the model case.
DEINDUSTRIALIZATION
India, Egypt, Latin America — regions that lost their industrial sectors as they were absorbed into the global economy as raw-material suppliers. India's textile industry collapsed under British colonial pressure.
THE "DIVERGENCE"
By 1900, Western Europe + North America + Japan had industrialized; most of the world had not. This created the income gap between "developed" and "developing" countries that defines global politics today.
Topic 5.5 — Technology in the Industrial Age

The "Second Industrial Revolution" (c. 1870–1914) brought a new wave of innovations — mostly in heavy industry, transportation, and communications:

• Steam locomotive (1804, commercial 1825) and railroads — transformed land transport. By 1900 railroads connected every continent. The U.S. transcontinental railroad (1869); Trans-Siberian Railway (1891–1916).
• Steamships — Atlantic crossing time fell from 6–8 weeks (1800) to 6 days (1900).
• Bessemer process (1856) — cheap mass-produced steel. Foundation of skyscrapers, bridges, longer railroads.
• Telegraph (Samuel Morse, 1844) and transatlantic cable (1866) — instantaneous global communication.
• Telephone (Bell, 1876), electric light (Edison, 1879), internal combustion engine (1880s+).
• Chemical fertilizers — transformed agriculture; supported larger urban populations.

AP Exam tip: The CED specifically wants you to connect technology → expanded global trade → imperialism. Better ships, telegraphs, and railroads made it possible for European powers to dominate distant territories and integrate them into a single global economy — setting up the imperialism of Unit 6.
Topic 5.6 — Industrialization: Government's Role

The CED is specific that industrialization outside Britain often required active state intervention — the opposite of laissez-faire. Two case studies are required:

JAPAN — THE MEIJI RESTORATION (1868)
After being forced open by Commodore Perry (1853), Japan overthrew the Tokugawa Shogunate and "restored" the Meiji Emperor as a symbol of modernization. The state built railroads, founded factories, sent students to Europe and America, abolished the samurai class, adopted Western military methods. Japan industrialized within a generation — the most rapid industrialization in world history at that time.
RUSSIA — TSARIST INDUSTRIALIZATION
Russia abolished serfdom (1861) and pursued state-led industrialization under Witte (1890s). Trans-Siberian Railway. But political reform lagged — setting up the 1905 Revolution and (eventually) the Russian Revolution of 1917.
EGYPT — MUHAMMAD ALI
Ottoman Egyptian governor (r. 1805–48). Tried to industrialize Egypt — cotton plantations, modern army, foundries. The CED uses him as a key example of state-led industrialization that ultimately failed due to European pressure.
QING CHINA — SELF-STRENGTHENING
After the Opium Wars and Taiping Rebellion, Qing officials launched the Self-Strengthening Movement (1861–95) to adopt Western technology while preserving Confucian values. Far less successful than Meiji Japan; defeated by Japan in 1895.
Topic 5.7 — Economic Developments and Innovations

Industrialization required — and produced — new economic institutions and ideologies.

Capitalism emerged as the dominant economic system. Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations (1776) had already attacked mercantilism in favor of free markets. By the 19th century, the new institutions were:

• Stock markets — London Stock Exchange, New York Stock Exchange. Allowed mass investment in industry.
• Limited liability corporations — investors could lose only what they invested. Encouraged risk-taking.
• Banks and finance capitalism — J.P. Morgan, Rothschilds, etc. Provided massive capital for railroads, factories, infrastructure.
• Monopolies and trusts — Standard Oil (Rockefeller), U.S. Steel (Carnegie), German cartels. Concentrated industrial power in fewer hands.
• Multinational corporations — United Fruit Company (Latin America), HSBC (Asia). The CED specifically wants you to know these.
• Insurance — Lloyd's of London. Spread risk across investors.

Transnational businesses like Unilever, HSBC, and the United Fruit Company expanded across multiple countries — predecessors to today's multinationals. They often shaped politics in weaker countries (the United Fruit Company's influence in Central America gave us the term "banana republic").

Gold standard — most major economies tied their currencies to gold by the late 19th century, creating a single global financial system. (This collapsed during World War I and the Depression.)

Required Illustrative Examples (5.7): stock market · limited liability corporation · monopoly · trust · Standard Oil · transnational/multinational corporations · United Fruit Company · HSBC · Unilever · finance capitalism · gold standard
Topic 5.8 — Reactions to the Industrial Economy

Industrial capitalism produced massive wealth and massive misery. The reactions to it gave us most of the political ideologies of the modern world.

Socialism & Marxism. Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels published The Communist Manifesto (1848) and Das Kapital (1867). Their argument:

• History is driven by class struggle — in industrial society, between the bourgeoisie (owners) and the proletariat (workers).
• Capitalism inevitably produces growing inequality, alienation, and crises.
• Workers will eventually overthrow capitalism in a proletarian revolution, leading to a classless, stateless society (communism).
• Utopian socialists like Robert Owen (Britain) had earlier proposed model factory communities. Marx scorned them as unrealistic.
• Marxism became the foundation of socialist parties across Europe and inspired the Russian Revolution (1917).

Labor Unions and Reform.

• Luddites (Britain, 1810s) — textile workers who smashed machines. The CED's iconic example of early industrial resistance.
• Labor unions emerged in Britain (Trade Union Act 1871), the U.S. (American Federation of Labor 1886), and across Europe.
• Strikes — major events like the 1877 U.S. railroad strike, the 1888 London matchgirls' strike, the 1894 Pullman strike.
• Reform movements — British Factory Acts (1833+) limiting child labor, regulating workplace safety, capping work hours. Slow but real.

Other reactions:

• Anarchism — opposed all hierarchy, including the state and capitalism. Mikhail Bakunin (Russia), Pyotr Kropotkin.
• Romantic conservatism — idealized pre-industrial life; reaction against rationalism and industry.
• Religious reactions — the Catholic Church's Rerum Novarum (1891) condemned both unregulated capitalism and socialism.
• Tanzimat reforms in the Ottoman Empire (1839–76) — tried to modernize society and reduce class privilege; partially successful.
• Hong Xiuquan and the Taiping Rebellion (China, 1850–64) — massive social-religious revolt against Qing rule. Killed 20–30 million people. The CED treats it as both a reaction to Qing weakness and indirectly to industrialization-driven inequality.

Topic 5.9 — Society and the Industrial Age

Industrialization transformed everyday life. New social patterns the CED expects you to know:

URBANIZATION
Massive migration from countryside to cities. Manchester grew from 25,000 (1750) to 300,000 (1850). Cities expanded faster than infrastructure: slums, pollution, disease, crime. Eventual reforms: sewers, public health, water systems.
NEW CLASS STRUCTURE
Industrial bourgeoisie (factory owners) — new economic and political power. Middle class (managers, professionals, shopkeepers) — expanded dramatically. Industrial working class (proletariat) — the largest single class for the first time in human history.
WOMEN & FAMILY
"Cult of domesticity" for middle-class women — idealized as homemakers. Working-class women labored in factories under brutal conditions. Family structures changed as work moved out of the home.
CHILD LABOR
Children as young as 6 worked in factories and mines. Reformers gradually pushed for laws restricting child labor (UK Factory Act of 1833 was first).
SUFFRAGE MOVEMENTS
Universal male suffrage gradually expanded across Europe. Women's suffrage movements grew — Seneca Falls Convention (US, 1848); UK suffragettes (Emmeline Pankhurst). Universal suffrage came mostly after 1900.
EDUCATION & LITERACY
States expanded public education to create skilled workers and patriotic citizens. Mass-circulation newspapers, mass political parties, mass culture — all enabled by mass literacy.
Required Illustrative Examples (5.8–5.9): Karl Marx · Friedrich Engels · Communist Manifesto · proletariat · bourgeoisie · Robert Owen · Luddites · labor unions · British Factory Acts · Seneca Falls Convention · Tanzimat reforms · Self-Strengthening Movement · Taiping Rebellion · urbanization · cult of domesticity
Topic 5.10 — Continuity and Change in the Industrial Age

Topic 5.10 is the unit's skills topic. Reasoning process: Continuity and Change Over Time. Suggested skill: Argumentation 6.B — support an argument with specific and relevant evidence. The CED asks you to identify what changed and what stayed the same in the period 1750–1900.

Most-tested CCOT examples:

CHANGES
Industrial economy (new). Mass urbanization (new). Atlantic Revolutions overthrew old monarchies. New ideologies (liberalism, socialism, nationalism). Global divergence between industrial and non-industrial regions. Abolition of slavery in most of the world.
CONTINUITIES
Patriarchal family structures (women still excluded from full citizenship). Imperialism continued and intensified (Unit 6). Some labor systems remained coercive (sharecropping, debt peonage, indentured servitude).
REGIONAL VARIATION
Western Europe + N. America + Japan industrialized rapidly. India, China, Africa fell behind. Latin America gained political independence but remained economically dependent on Europe and the U.S. (foreshadowing Topic 6.5 economic imperialism).
TRANSITION POINT
By 1900, Europe + USA + Japan dominated the global economy and were carving up the rest of the world (Unit 6). The world had become genuinely global — tied by railroads, steamships, telegraphs, and mass migrations.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks: "Evaluate the extent to which industrial development between 1750 and 1900 changed economic systems." Have two specific changes (factory system, joint-stock corporations) and one continuity (coerced labor in new forms — sharecropping, indentured servitude) ready. Three named, specific examples wins any LEQ on this period.
