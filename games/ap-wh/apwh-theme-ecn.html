Theme Briefing — Economic Systems (ECN)
A cross-period synthesis of how trade, labor, money, and economic ideology evolved from 1200 to the present. The world economy went from regional trade networks (Silk Roads, Indian Ocean, trans-Saharan) to truly global capitalism — with massive consequences for who got rich, who got poor, and who got enslaved. Use this briefing to prepare for thematic LEQs and any AP question asking you to compare economic structures across periods. The dominant skills are CCOT and Comparison.
The Big Story: How the World Economy Changed (and Didn\'t) From 1200 to Present

Across 800 years, the world economy transformed dramatically. The CED expects you to identify continuities and changes:

CONTINUITY 1: COERCED LABOR
Slavery, serfdom, encomienda, indentured servitude, sweatshops — coerced labor never fully disappeared. The forms changed; the underlying reality of forced or near-forced work persisted.
CONTINUITY 2: TRADE INEQUALITY
Wealthy regions exchanged manufactured goods for raw materials from poorer regions in 1200 (Cairo for African gold) and in 2000 (Apple for Chinese-assembled iPhones). The geography shifted; the pattern persisted.
CONTINUITY 3: DIASPORIC MERCHANTS
Ethnic merchant communities (Sogdians, Genoese, Gujaratis, Hokkien Chinese, modern multinational corporations\' diasporas) lubricated long-distance trade in every era.
CONTINUITY 4: STATE INTERVENTION
From Yuan China taxing Silk Road traders to modern WTO/IMF/tariffs, states have always regulated economic activity. Pure laissez-faire is the exception, not the rule.

And the changes — what fundamentally transformed:

• From regional to global trade networks. 1200: Silk Roads, Indian Ocean, trans-Saharan trade. 1500+: a single integrated global economy via Atlantic + Pacific.
• From land empires to maritime + capitalist economies. The Atlantic system + joint-stock companies + plantation slavery built modern capitalism.
• From mercantilism to industrial capitalism to neoliberalism. Each ideology dominated for ~150–200 years before yielding to the next.
• From bullion-based to fiat-based money. Spanish silver was global currency in 1600; the U.S. dollar (with no gold backing since 1971) is global currency today.
• From single-commodity dependency to financial interdependence. Modern global supply chains span dozens of countries for a single product.

Era 1 (1200–1450): The Pre-Modern Trade Networks

From Units 1–2: Three major trade networks dominated Eurasia, plus a fourth in Africa.

SILK ROADS
Overland Eurasian network. Silk and porcelain west, horses and silver east. Pax Mongolica enabled. Diasporic merchants (Sogdians, Persians) facilitated. Caravanserais provided lodging.
INDIAN OCEAN
Maritime network connecting East Africa, Arabia, India, SE Asia, China. Monsoon-powered. Spices, cotton textiles, ivory. Swahili merchants, Gujarati traders, Chinese junks (Zheng He).
TRANS-SAHARAN TRADE
Camel caravans across the Sahara. Salt south, gold north. Mansa Musa\'s 1324 hajj distributed so much Mali gold it caused inflation in Cairo. Timbuktu as Islamic learning + trade center.
LABOR SYSTEMS
Free peasants paying tax/tribute (most of the world). Serfdom in Europe and Russia. Slavery in Islamic world and pre-Atlantic Africa. Mit\'a labor draft in Inca empire. Corvée in many monarchies.

Money in this era was bullion-based: gold dinars (Islamic world), silver, cowrie shells (Africa, parts of Asia), Chinese paper money under the Yuan (an early experiment).

Era 2A (1450–1750): Land Empires

From Unit 3: Land empires extracted wealth through new tax systems:

• Ottoman iltizam (tax farming) — Ottoman government auctioned tax-collection rights to wealthy individuals.
• Mughal zamindar system — local landholders collected taxes, kept a share.
• Ming/Qing silver tax — Single Whip Reform required tax payment in silver, creating massive Chinese demand for Spanish-American silver.
• Russian poll tax on serfs.

The Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal courts engaged actively in luxury trade — carpets (Persian), textiles (Mughal), coffee (originally Yemeni). Wealth concentrated in Delhi, Isfahan, Istanbul, Beijing.

Era 2B (1450–1750): The Atlantic System & Global Silver

From Unit 4: This is the era when modern capitalism was born.

Mercantilism was the dominant economic ideology — national wealth = bullion accumulated; trade is zero-sum; colonies serve the mother country; positive trade balance is the goal. Navigation Acts and similar laws restricted colonial trade.

Joint-stock companies (Dutch VOC 1602, British East India Company 1600) were a transformative financial innovation. Spread risk across investors; made huge expeditions financially possible. Foundation of modern corporate capitalism.

The Columbian Exchange reshaped diets and economies: maize and potatoes fueled population growth; sugar and tobacco built plantation economies; horses transformed Plains Indian cultures.

The Atlantic slave trade — the largest forced migration in human history (~12.5 million Africans transported, ~2 million dead at sea) — was the labor backbone of the Atlantic system. Sugar drove demand. Triangular trade tied Europe, Africa, and the Americas.

Other coerced labor systems in the Americas: encomienda (Spanish indigenous labor), hacienda (debt peonage), Spanish mit\'a (forced mine labor at Potosí), indentured servitude, chattel slavery.

Silver and the global economy. Spanish silver from Potosí flowed to Europe, then to China — where the Single Whip Reform required silver tax payment. By 1600, perhaps a third of all New World silver ended up in China. The world\'s first truly global economy.

Manila Galleons connected Acapulco to Manila, shipping silver west and Chinese silk east.

Era 3 (1750–1900): Industrial Capitalism

From Unit 5: The Industrial Revolution began in Britain c. 1750 and reshaped the global economy. Cotton mills replaced cottage textile production; coal-fired steam engines powered factories; the factory system replaced cottage industry.

Why Britain first? Coal + iron + capital (slave-trade profits) + labor (enclosure-displaced peasants) + colonial markets + property rights + geography + political stability.

Industrialization spread in waves: Belgium, France, U.S., German states (1820s–60s); then Japan (Meiji) and Russia (1870s–90s) through state-led "catch-up" industrialization. Meanwhile, India and Egypt deindustrialized as British and French markets undermined them.

Adam Smith\'s Wealth of Nations (1776) attacked mercantilism, advocating free markets. Foundation of liberal capitalism.

New economic institutions: stock markets (London, NYSE), limited liability corporations (investors lose only what they invest), monopolies and trusts (Standard Oil, U.S. Steel), multinational corporations (United Fruit Company in Latin America, HSBC in East Asia).

Marx and Engels\' Communist Manifesto (1848) argued capitalism produces growing inequality, alienation, and inevitable revolution. Foundation of socialism. Labor unions and reform movements (British Factory Acts, Tanzimat) tried to reform capitalism without abolishing it.

From Unit 6: Industrialized states imposed cash crop economies on colonized regions. Rubber (Belgian Congo, Brazil), palm oil and ivory (West Africa), cotton (Egypt, India, U.S. South), sugar/coffee/bananas (Latin America/Caribbean), tea (India, Ceylon). Colonial railways were built to extract from interiors to coastal ports for export.

Economic imperialism dominated even non-colonized regions: unequal treaties (China, Japan), extraterritoriality, spheres of influence, foreign control of finance (Egypt). Latin America gained political independence but remained economically dependent on Britain (and later the U.S.).

The global economy operated on the gold standard from the late 19th century — most major economies tied currencies to gold, creating a single global financial system.

Era 4 (1900–1945): Crisis, Depression, and Competing Models

From Unit 7: The Great Depression (1929–c. 1939) was the worst economic crisis in industrial history. ~25% unemployment in U.S. and Germany at peak. Trade collapsed under protectionist tariffs (Smoot-Hawley Tariff, 1930). Gold standard collapsed. Loss of faith in liberal capitalism was the central political consequence — opening the door to fascism and communism.

Four government responses across the political spectrum:

U.S. — NEW DEAL
FDR\'s program (1933+). Government employment programs (CCC, WPA), banking reform (FDIC), Social Security. Saved capitalism by reforming it. Massively expanded federal economic role.
USSR — FIVE-YEAR PLANS
Stalin\'s command-economy industrialization (1928+). Forced collectivization caused mass famine (Holodomor). USSR was the only major economy to grow during the Depression.
FASCIST ITALY — CORPORATIST STATE
Mussolini\'s regime organized the economy into state-controlled "corporations" representing industries. Pioneered the fascist economic model.
NAZI GERMANY — REARMAMENT
Hitler\'s regime used massive public works (Autobahn) and military rearmament to end unemployment. Funded by debt and seizures from Jewish citizens.

WWII required massive economic mobilization on all sides — "war economies" with rationing, price controls, women in factories ("Rosie the Riveter"), nationalized industries.

Era 5 (1945–1991): Bretton Woods, Cold War, Decolonization

From Unit 8: The post-WWII economic order was built at Bretton Woods (1944). Three new institutions:

• International Monetary Fund (IMF) — lends to countries in crisis with strict economic conditions.
• World Bank — provides loans for development projects in poorer countries.
• GATT (General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade, 1947; replaced by WTO 1995) — reduced trade barriers among capitalist countries.

The Marshall Plan (1948) provided $13 billion in U.S. aid to rebuild Western Europe, binding it economically to the U.S.

The Cold War was also an economic contest between two models:

• Capitalism in the West — market-based, private property, consumer goods, post-war boom (~1945–73).
• Centrally planned economies in the East — state ownership, Five-Year Plans, COMECON trade bloc, chronic shortages of consumer goods.

Decolonization created dozens of newly independent states. Most pursued state-led economic development — Nasser\'s Aswan Dam, Indira Gandhi\'s state industries, Nyerere\'s ujamaa in Tanzania. Mixed results; many ran into debt crises by the 1980s.

The capitalist post-war boom ended c. 1973 with oil shocks (OPEC) and stagflation. Set the stage for the neoliberal turn.

Era 6 (1980s–Present): Neoliberalism & Globalization

From Unit 9: The 1980s saw a major economic shift: from Keynesian/state-led economies to neoliberalism — free markets, deregulation, privatization, low taxes.

Key actors:

• Ronald Reagan (US) and Margaret Thatcher (UK) led 1980s deregulation. "There is no alternative" to free markets.
• Deng Xiaoping opened China to market forces from 1978. By 2010, China was world\'s 2nd-largest economy — "socialism with Chinese characteristics."
• Soviet collapse → market transitions — Russia and Eastern Europe converted from communism to capitalism (often chaotically) in the 1990s.
• Free-trade agreements — NAFTA (1994), European Union (1993), WTO (1995).
• Multinational corporations — Apple, Walmart, Toyota, Samsung. Global supply chains across dozens of countries.

Asian Tigers (Hong Kong, Singapore, S. Korea, Taiwan) industrialized rapidly through state-led, export-oriented strategies in 1960s–90s. India\'s 1991 economic liberalization. Tech sector boom in Bangalore.

The 2008 financial crisis exposed risks of unregulated finance — U.S. housing-market collapse triggered global recession. Led to some banking reform but globalization continued.

Knowledge economy — in wealthy countries, services, information, and finance replaced manufacturing as the primary economic activity. Manufacturing shifted to lower-wage countries.

Resistance to neoliberalism grew through the 2010s: anti-globalization protests (Seattle WTO, 1999), right-wing populism (Brexit, Trump), Fair Trade movement, microfinance (Grameen Bank). The 2020s have seen growing skepticism of the "Washington Consensus" globalization model.

The Big Questions for AP Synthesis

The AP exam often asks thematic LEQ questions about economics across periods. Here are the most-tested prompts and how to think about them:

TRADE NETWORKS
"Compare trade networks across two periods." Try: Silk Roads (1200) vs. Atlantic system (1700) vs. modern global supply chains. Same function, different geography, different scale, different beneficiaries.
LABOR SYSTEMS
"Compare labor systems across two periods." Try: serfdom (medieval) vs. Atlantic chattel slavery (1700) vs. Indian indentured labor (1850) vs. modern Bangladeshi garment workers (2020). The continuity of coercion through changing forms.
ECONOMIC IDEOLOGY
"Trace changes in dominant economic ideology." Mercantilism (1500–1750) → liberal capitalism (1750–1929) → Keynesian state capitalism (1933–73) → neoliberalism (1980–present). Each lasted ~150 years.
INEQUALITY & DEPENDENCY
"Evaluate continuity and change in global economic inequality." Latin America\'s 19th-c. dependency → Africa\'s post-colonial dependency → modern Global North/South divide. Same pattern, different geography.
How to use this Briefing: The ECN theme has the clearest arc of any AP World theme — from regional networks to global capitalism is a tractable narrative. Memorize one specific example per period (Silk Roads → VOC → cotton mill → Five-Year Plan → Marshall Plan → NAFTA) and you can write any LEQ on this material.

CCOT Skills This Theme Develops
CONTINUITY & CHANGE OVER TIME (CCOT)
The central skill. Continuities: coerced labor, trade inequality, diasporic merchants, state intervention. Changes: regional → global trade; mercantilism → industrial → neoliberalism; bullion → fiat money.
COMPARISON ACROSS PERIODS
Cross-period comparison — e.g., Silk Roads (1200) vs. Atlantic system (1700) vs. global supply chains (2020). Or: serfdom vs. chattel slavery vs. indentured labor vs. modern sweatshops.
CAUSATION CHAINS
Long causal chains: silver flow → Chinese economy → European trade deficit → Atlantic plantation slavery. Or: Industrial Revolution → imperialism → cash-crop economies → post-colonial dependency.
ARGUMENTATION (LEQ-focused)
Build a thesis claim about economic systems across periods, support it with three specific examples drawn from at least two different units, qualify with counterevidence, conclude with synthesis.
