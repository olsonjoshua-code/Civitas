Unit 8 Briefing — Cold War & Decolonization
After WWII, the world reorganized along two axes simultaneously: the U.S.–Soviet Cold War (1947–91) and the rapid decolonization of European empires across Asia and Africa. The two processes intertwined — both superpowers competed for influence in newly independent states, often through proxy wars. The reasoning skill is overwhelmingly Causation; Topic 8.9 makes it explicit.
Topic 8.1 — Setting the Stage

WWII fundamentally reshaped global power. Two facts above all:

• European empires were exhausted. Britain, France, the Netherlands, and Belgium emerged from WWII economically devastated and morally compromised (their own occupation by the Nazis undermined their claim to rule colonized peoples).
• Two superpowers emerged: the United States (industrially intact, atomic monopoly) and the Soviet Union (Red Army occupying Eastern Europe). They had been wartime allies, but their alliance was strategic, not ideological.

Yalta (Feb 1945) and Potsdam (July 1945) conferences set up postwar arrangements: Germany divided into occupation zones, the Soviets to declare war on Japan, the United Nations to be founded. But disagreements emerged over the future of Eastern Europe, where Stalin demanded "friendly governments" (i.e., communist puppet states) as a buffer against future invasion.

The United Nations (1945) replaced the failed League. Five permanent Security Council members (US, UK, USSR, France, China) with veto power. Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948).

Why decolonization accelerated after WWII:

• European powers exhausted; couldn't sustain colonies.
• Colonized peoples had fought in WWII for freedom — demanded their own.
• New ideologies of self-determination (UN Charter, Atlantic Charter).
• Both superpowers (U.S. for ideological reasons, USSR for strategic reasons) opposed European colonialism.
• Strong nationalist movements had emerged during the interwar period (Topic 7.5).

Required Illustrative Examples (8.1): Yalta · Potsdam · United Nations · Universal Declaration of Human Rights · Atlantic Charter · post-WWII reconstruction · superpower emergence
Topic 8.2 — The Cold War

The Cold War (c. 1947–1991) was the ideological and geopolitical rivalry between the United States (capitalist democracy) and the Soviet Union (communist authoritarianism). Direct U.S.-Soviet war never happened — both had nuclear weapons and the costs of direct conflict were unimaginable. Instead, they fought through proxies, arms races, espionage, and ideological competition.

Origins of the Cold War (1945–49):

IRON CURTAIN (1946)
Churchill's famous phrase describing the Soviet domination of Eastern Europe. Symbolized the division of Europe into communist and capitalist halves.
TRUMAN DOCTRINE (1947)
U.S. pledge to support nations resisting communist subversion. Initially aimed at Greece and Turkey. Foundation of "containment" as Cold War policy.
MARSHALL PLAN (1948)
$13 billion in U.S. economic aid to rebuild Western Europe. Bound it to the U.S. economically and politically. The Soviets refused to let Eastern European nations participate.
BERLIN BLOCKADE / AIRLIFT (1948–49)
Soviets blockaded West Berlin. U.S. and British air-supplied the city for 11 months. First major Cold War crisis. Led to formal NATO alliance.
NATO (1949)
North Atlantic Treaty Organization. U.S.-led military alliance of Western nations. Pledged that an attack on one was an attack on all.
WARSAW PACT (1955)
Soviet-led military alliance of Eastern European communist states. Counter to NATO. Disbanded in 1991 with Soviet collapse.

Major Cold War crises:

KOREAN WAR (1950–53)
North Korea (Soviet/Chinese-backed) invaded South Korea. UN forces (mostly U.S.) intervened. Ended in stalemate at the 38th parallel. ~3 million dead. Korea remains divided today.
CUBAN MISSILE CRISIS (1962)
Soviet nuclear missiles in Cuba (after Bay of Pigs invasion failed). Closest the world came to nuclear war. Resolved when U.S. removed missiles from Turkey and Soviets withdrew from Cuba.
BERLIN WALL (1961)
Soviets built a wall dividing East and West Berlin to stop refugees fleeing to the West. Iconic Cold War symbol. Stood until 1989.
VIETNAM WAR (1955–75)
Communist North Vietnam (Ho Chi Minh) vs. South Vietnam (U.S.-backed). U.S. lost ~58,000; ~2 million Vietnamese died. U.S. withdrew 1973; North Vietnam unified the country in 1975.
SOVIET-AFGHAN WAR (1979–89)
USSR invaded Afghanistan to support a friendly communist government. U.S. armed mujahideen (Islamic fighters). Soviets withdrew in defeat 1989. Major factor in Soviet collapse.
NUCLEAR ARMS RACE
USSR achieved atomic weapon (1949), hydrogen bomb (1953). Both sides built tens of thousands of warheads. Doctrine of Mutually Assured Destruction (MAD).
Required Illustrative Examples (8.2): Truman Doctrine · Marshall Plan · containment · Iron Curtain · Berlin Blockade · NATO · Warsaw Pact · Korean War · Cuban Missile Crisis · Berlin Wall · Vietnam War · Soviet-Afghan War · nuclear arms race · MAD · Sputnik
Topic 8.3 — Effects of the Cold War

The Cold War's effects went far beyond U.S.-Soviet relations.

• Nuclear proliferation — the U.K., France, China, India, Pakistan, Israel, North Korea acquired nuclear weapons.
• Proxy wars across the Global South — Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Mozambique, Nicaragua, El Salvador, Afghanistan. The Cold War was hot in the developing world.
• Massive military spending distorted both U.S. and Soviet economies. Eisenhower warned of the "military-industrial complex."
• Space Race — Soviet Sputnik (1957) shocked the U.S.; Yuri Gagarin (1961) was the first human in space; the U.S. Apollo 11 moon landing (1969) was the climax. Drove tech innovation.
• Cultural Cold War — jazz, rock, Hollywood, abstract art on the U.S. side; ballet, classical music, propaganda film on the Soviet side. CIA secretly funded Western cultural programs.
• Domestic surveillance — McCarthyism in the U.S. (1950s); KGB and Stasi surveillance in the Eastern Bloc.

Topic 8.4 — Spread of Communism After 1900

Topic 8.4 covers the spread of communist regimes globally. The CED's flagship case is China.

Chinese Communist Revolution (1949). The Chinese Civil War (1927–49) between Mao's CCP and Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalists ended with Communist victory. Mao Zedong proclaimed the People's Republic of China on October 1, 1949. Chiang's Nationalists retreated to Taiwan. Mao's policies:

LAND REFORM
Confiscated land from landlords; redistributed to peasants. Killed an estimated 1–5 million landlords in the process.
GREAT LEAP FORWARD (1958–62)
Mao\'s catastrophic forced industrialization and collectivization. Caused the worst famine in human history: ~30–45 million Chinese died.
CULTURAL REVOLUTION (1966–76)
Mao mobilized youth (Red Guards) against intellectuals and "capitalist roaders." Universities closed; ~1.5–2 million killed; millions persecuted. Devastated Chinese culture and education for a generation.
DENG XIAOPING REFORMS (1978+)
Mao\'s successor introduced market reforms ("socialism with Chinese characteristics"). Foundation of China\'s modern economic boom.

Other communist states emerged across Eastern Europe (Soviet satellites: Poland, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria), Asia (North Korea 1948, North Vietnam 1954, Cambodia under Khmer Rouge 1975, Laos 1975), Latin America (Cuba under Fidel Castro, 1959), and parts of Africa.

Cuban Revolution (1959). Fidel Castro and Che Guevara overthrew U.S.-backed dictator Batista. Cuba became the only Soviet ally in the Americas; led to Bay of Pigs (1961) and Cuban Missile Crisis (1962). Cuban communism survived the Soviet collapse.

Required Illustrative Examples (8.4): Chinese Communist Revolution · Mao Zedong · Great Leap Forward · Cultural Revolution · Deng Xiaoping · Tiananmen Square (1989) · Fidel Castro · Cuban Revolution · Che Guevara · Soviet satellite states · Khmer Rouge / Pol Pot
Topic 8.5 — Decolonization After 1900

Topic 8.5 covers the dismantling of European empires after WWII. Hundreds of millions gained independence within a single generation. The methods varied: peaceful negotiation, mass nonviolent resistance, armed struggle.

Asia — the first wave:

INDIA & PAKISTAN (1947)
Mahatma Gandhi\'s nonviolent resistance won independence from Britain. Partition divided British India into Hindu-majority India and Muslim-majority Pakistan; ~1 million killed in religious violence; ~15 million displaced. Gandhi assassinated 1948.
VIETNAM (1945–75)
Ho Chi Minh\'s Viet Minh fought French (defeated at Dien Bien Phu, 1954), then U.S. (Vietnam War). North Vietnam unified country 1975 after U.S. withdrawal.
INDONESIA (1949)
Sukarno led independence from the Dutch. Later co-founded the Non-Aligned Movement (Bandung Conference, 1955).
PHILIPPINES (1946)
Independence from the U.S., as promised before WWII. Among the first post-war independence cases.

Africa — the second wave (mostly 1957–75):

GHANA (1957)
Kwame Nkrumah led Ghana to become the first sub-Saharan African nation to gain independence from Britain. Inspired the rest of Africa. Pan-African leader.
ALGERIA (1962)
Bloody 8-year war of independence from France (1954–62). FLN (National Liberation Front) won. ~500,000 deaths. France lost its largest colony.
"YEAR OF AFRICA" (1960)
17 African nations gained independence in 1960 alone. By 1970, almost all of Africa was independent.
SOUTH AFRICA — APARTHEID
White-minority government instituted apartheid (legal racial segregation) from 1948. Long anti-apartheid struggle led by Nelson Mandela and the ANC. Mandela imprisoned 27 years; freed 1990; first multiracial elections 1994.

Middle East:

• Israel founded 1948 (UN Partition Plan); first Arab-Israeli War followed immediately. Continuing conflict over Palestine.
• Egypt — Gamal Abdel Nasser overthrew the British-backed monarchy (1952). Nationalized the Suez Canal (1956), triggering Suez Crisis. Pan-Arab nationalist leader.
• Iran — Mosaddegh nationalized oil (1951); CIA-backed coup (1953) restored the Shah. Set up the Iranian Revolution of 1979.

Required Illustrative Examples (8.5): Indian independence · Partition of India · Pakistan · Israel · Vietnamese independence (Dien Bien Phu) · Ghana / Kwame Nkrumah · Algerian War · "Year of Africa" · Indonesian independence (Sukarno) · Nelson Mandela / apartheid · Nasser / Egyptian independence
Topic 8.6 — Newly Independent States

Topic 8.6 examines the challenges newly independent states faced. Most struggled with:

• Borders inherited from colonial maps — cutting through ethnic and religious groups, creating internal conflicts.
• Single-commodity economies — vulnerable to global price swings.
• Cold War pressure — both superpowers tried to win them as allies.
• Coups and authoritarianism — many democracies collapsed into one-party rule or military dictatorships.

State-led economic development was the dominant strategy. CED-required examples:

NASSER IN EGYPT
Built Aswan High Dam; nationalized Suez Canal; pursued Arab socialism. Pan-Arab leader of the 1950s–60s.
INDIRA GANDHI IN INDIA
Nehru\'s daughter. Continued state-led industrialization. Imposed "Emergency" rule (1975–77). Assassinated 1984.
JULIUS NYERERE IN TANZANIA
Pursued ujamaa (African socialism), forced villagization. Reduced inequality but failed economically. Stepped down voluntarily — rare in postcolonial Africa.
SIRIMAVO BANDARANAIKE
Sri Lankan PM (1960). World\'s first female head of government. Pursued state-led development and Sinhalese nationalism.

The Non-Aligned Movement — key for Topic 8.6.

Founded at the Bandung Conference (1955) in Indonesia by Sukarno (Indonesia), Nehru (India), Nasser (Egypt), and Tito (Yugoslavia). Refused to side with either superpower. Argued the Third World had its own interests — primarily decolonization, anti-racism, and economic development. Major moral force in the UN.

Migration patterns: Decolonization triggered massive migration to former colonial powers — South Asians to Britain, Algerians to France, Filipinos to the U.S., Caribbeans to the UK and U.S. Created lasting cultural and demographic links between former colonies and metropoles.

Required Illustrative Examples (8.6): Non-Aligned Movement · Bandung Conference · Sukarno · Nasser · Nehru · Indira Gandhi · Tito · Julius Nyerere · Sirimavo Bandaranaike · partition states (Israel, Cambodia, Pakistan) · post-colonial migrations (South Asians to Britain, Algerians to France, Filipinos to U.S.)
Topic 8.7 — Global Resistance to Established Power Structures

Topic 8.7 covers movements against established orders — both colonial and domestic.

U.S. CIVIL RIGHTS MOVEMENT
Martin Luther King Jr. led nonviolent campaign against segregation. Civil Rights Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965). MLK assassinated 1968. Inspired by Gandhi; inspired global movements.
WOMEN\'S MOVEMENTS
Second-wave feminism (1960s–80s). Equal pay, reproductive rights, workplace equality. UN Decade for Women (1975–85).
ANTI-APARTHEID
Nelson Mandela and the ANC fought South Africa\'s racial regime. Global boycott movement pressured the regime. Apartheid ended 1994 with multiracial elections.
SOLIDARITY (POLAND)
Lech Wałęsa\'s independent trade union (1980+) challenged Polish communism. First successful labor movement in the Eastern Bloc. Helped trigger 1989 collapse.
TIANANMEN SQUARE (1989)
Chinese student protesters demanded democracy. Government killed hundreds (estimates vary widely) in the square. Crushed the Chinese democracy movement.
IRANIAN REVOLUTION (1979)
Ayatollah Khomeini overthrew the Shah, established an Islamic Republic. Combined religious revival, anti-imperialism, anti-monarchism. U.S.-Iran relations broken to this day.
Required Illustrative Examples (8.7): Martin Luther King Jr. · U.S. Civil Rights Movement · Nelson Mandela / ANC · anti-apartheid movement · Solidarity (Poland) · Tiananmen Square (1989) · Iranian Revolution / Ayatollah Khomeini · women\'s rights movements · Liberation Theology · Mothers of the Plaza de Mayo (Argentina)
Topic 8.8 — End of the Cold War

The Cold War ended faster than anyone expected. Multiple causes converged:

• Soviet economic stagnation — central planning failed to keep pace with capitalist innovation, especially in computing.
• Soviet-Afghan War failure — Soviets withdrew 1989 after a brutal decade.
• U.S. military buildup under Reagan — defense spending increased pressure on Soviet economy.
• Gorbachev\'s reforms — glasnost (openness) and perestroika (restructuring) tried to save Soviet system but accelerated its collapse.

1989: the year communism fell in Eastern Europe.

• June: Solidarity won partially-free Polish elections.
• Summer: Hungary opened its border with Austria; East Germans fled west.
• November 9: Berlin Wall fell — iconic image of Cold War\'s end.
• Velvet Revolution in Czechoslovakia; Romania overthrew Ceaușescu (executed Christmas Day).
• By year-end, every Eastern European communist regime had fallen.

Soviet collapse (1991).

• Baltic states (Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia) declared independence.
• Failed coup against Gorbachev (August 1991).
• Boris Yeltsin rose to power in Russia.
• December 1991: Soviet Union formally dissolved into 15 successor states. Cold War over.

Mikhail Gorbachev won the Nobel Peace Prize (1990) for his role. The U.S. emerged as the world\'s sole superpower — setting up Unit 9.

Required Illustrative Examples (8.8): Mikhail Gorbachev · glasnost · perestroika · Berlin Wall fall (1989) · Solidarity · Velvet Revolution · collapse of USSR (1991) · Boris Yeltsin · Reagan
Topic 8.9 — Causation in the Age of Cold War and Decolonization

Topic 8.9 is the unit\'s skills topic. Reasoning process: Causation. Suggested skill: Argumentation 6.B.

Most-tested causation chains:

WWII → COLD WAR
U.S.-Soviet wartime alliance (against fascism) dissolved into ideological rivalry; Soviet expansion in Eastern Europe; U.S. containment policy.
WWII → DECOLONIZATION
European exhaustion + colonial soldiers\' WWII service + UN self-determination rhetoric → rapid postwar decolonization.
COLD WAR ↔ DECOLONIZATION
Both processes intersected: superpowers competed for newly independent states; Non-Aligned Movement responded; proxy wars in the Global South.
COLD WAR → GLOBALIZATION
End of Cold War + neoliberal economic integration → the globalized world of Unit 9.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks: "Evaluate the extent to which decolonization changed political structures in Asia and Africa." Have two specific changes (new nation-states with new constitutions; state-led economic development) and one continuity (economic dependency on former colonial powers, now called "neocolonialism") ready.
