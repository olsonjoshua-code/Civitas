Unit 3 Briefing — Land-Based Empires
Four "gunpowder empires" dominated Eurasia from c. 1450–1750: the Manchu/Qing, Mughal, Ottoman, and Safavid. The CED also expects you to know secondary land empires like Songhai, Tokugawa Japan, and Romanov Russia. This unit tests three things heavily: methods of imperial expansion, methods of administration, and belief systems used to legitimize rule. Causation is the dominant reasoning skill.
Topic 3.1 — Empires Expand

Between roughly 1450 and 1750, a wave of new land-based empires emerged or expanded dramatically across Eurasia. The CED groups them as the "gunpowder empires" because they all relied on three things: (1) gunpowder weaponry (cannons, muskets, fortified artillery), (2) professional standing armies recruited and paid by the state, and (3) centralized bureaucracies capable of taxing and administering vast contiguous territory.

The four required empires are the Manchu/Qing (China), Mughal (India), Ottoman (Turkey/Middle East/N. Africa/Balkans), and Safavid (Persia). Each is briefed in detail below. The CED also expects you to know secondary states (Songhai, Tokugawa Japan, Romanov Russia) and key state rivalries (Safavid–Mughal conflict; Songhai's defeat by Morocco at the Battle of Tondibi in 1591).

Required Illustrative Examples (3.1): Manchu (Qing) Empire · Mughal Empire · Ottoman Empire · Safavid Empire · Songhai Empire · Battle of Tondibi · Tokugawa Shogunate · Romanov Russia · Mughal–Safavid conflict · Battle of Chaldiran (1514) · Battle of Lepanto (1571) · Suleiman the Magnificent · Akbar · Kangxi · Shah Abbas
The Ottoman Empire (c. 1299–1922)

The Ottomans were the most enduring and arguably most successful gunpowder empire. From a small Anatolian frontier state c. 1300, they expanded into the largest Muslim empire of the early modern era — controlling the Balkans, Anatolia, the Levant, Egypt, North Africa, and parts of Arabia and Mesopotamia at their height.

The defining moment was the conquest of Constantinople (1453) by Mehmed II "the Conqueror," using massive bronze cannons that battered down the city's ancient walls. This ended the Byzantine Empire and gave the Ottomans a strategic capital (renamed Istanbul) at the crossroads of Europe and Asia.

Their golden age was under Suleiman the Magnificent (r. 1520–1566): conquered Hungary at the Battle of Mohács (1526), besieged Vienna (1529), reformed Ottoman law, and patronized a stunning architectural and literary flowering. By his death the Ottomans were the dominant power in the Mediterranean.

Two distinctive Ottoman institutions are heavily tested:

JANISSARIES
Elite Ottoman infantry corps. Originally recruited as enslaved Christian boys via the devshirme system — converted to Islam, trained from childhood, loyal personally to the sultan. Among the world's first professional standing armies.
MILLET SYSTEM
Religious communities (Greek Orthodox, Armenian Christian, Jewish) granted self-rule over their own civil affairs in exchange for tax payment and loyalty. Famously tolerant for the era — though non-Muslims paid a jizya head tax.
AP Exam tip: The devshirme ("blood tax") is one of the most-tested terms. Be ready to explain it as a recruitment of bureaucratic and military elites — converting enslaved Christian boys into Janissaries and high-ranking administrators. It illustrates the CED's Topic 3.2 theme of professional bureaucracy.
The Safavid Empire (1501–1736)

The Safavids ruled Persia (modern Iran) for over two centuries. Founded by Shah Ismail in 1501, they made one decision that shaped Iranian identity to this day: they imposed Twelver Shi'a Islam as the state religion across a population that had been mostly Sunni. This created lasting religious tension with the Sunni Ottomans on their west and the Sunni Mughals on their east.

The defining military event was the Battle of Chaldiran (1514) — the Ottomans (with cannons and Janissary muskets) defeated the Safavid cavalry, demonstrating the dominance of gunpowder over traditional steppe warfare. The Safavids learned the lesson and adopted gunpowder weapons, but Ottoman–Safavid wars continued for centuries.

The Safavid golden age came under Shah Abbas the Great (r. 1588–1629), who:

• Created a professional standing army of ghulams — enslaved Christian boys (paralleling the Ottoman devshirme), converted to Islam, trained as elite soldiers.
• Built a magnificent capital at Isfahan — "Isfahan is half the world," the saying went — with the Royal Mosque, the Shah Mosque, and the world's second-largest urban square.
• Encouraged Persian carpet manufacturing and silk exports — major sources of state revenue.
• Negotiated trade with European powers (English, Dutch) to break Ottoman commercial monopolies.

Required Illustrative Examples (Safavid): Shah Ismail · Shah Abbas the Great · Twelver Shi'a Islam · ghulams · Battle of Chaldiran (1514) · Isfahan · Mughal–Safavid rivalry · Persian carpet trade · silk exports
The Mughal Empire (1526–1857)

The Mughals ruled most of the Indian subcontinent for over three centuries. Founded by Babur (a descendant of both Genghis Khan and Timur) at the Battle of Panipat (1526), where his small army with cannons defeated the much larger Delhi Sultanate forces.

The most important Mughal ruler — and the one the CED uses repeatedly — is Akbar the Great (r. 1556–1605). He governed a Hindu-majority population with a small Muslim ruling elite, and his approach is the AP exam's go-to example of religious tolerance as a tool of state legitimation:

• Abolished the jizya (the head tax on non-Muslims) — making Hindus equal taxpayers with Muslims.
• Married Hindu princesses; appointed Hindus to high government positions.
• Held interfaith dialogues at his court between Muslim, Hindu, Jain, Zoroastrian, and Christian (Jesuit) scholars.
• Founded a syncretic court religion called Din-i Ilahi ("Religion of God") — never widely adopted, but symbolically powerful.
• Used a zamindar tax-collection system — local landholders collected taxes for the state and kept a share.

The Mughal cultural legacy was enormous. Akbar's grandson Shah Jahan (r. 1628–1658) built the Taj Mahal as a tomb for his wife Mumtaz Mahal — arguably the most famous monument of Islamic architecture.

But Akbar's tolerant approach was reversed by his great-grandson Aurangzeb (r. 1658–1707), who reimposed the jizya, demolished some Hindu temples, and fought constant wars to expand Mughal territory. His policies fractured the empire's coalition with Hindu elites and triggered religious revolts (notably the rise of the Marathas, a Hindu power, and the militarization of the Sikhs). The empire entered a long decline after his death — just in time for British East India Company influence to begin.

Required Illustrative Examples (Mughal): Babur · Akbar the Great · Shah Jahan · Aurangzeb · Battle of Panipat · zamindar tax system · Din-i Ilahi · jizya (its abolition and reimposition) · Taj Mahal · Mughal architecture · Sikhism (founded in this era)
AP Exam tip: The contrast between Akbar (tolerance) and Aurangzeb (orthodoxy) is one of the most-tested compare/contrast points in the unit. The College Board uses it as a case study in how religious policy can both legitimize and undermine imperial rule.
The Manchu (Qing) Empire (1644–1912)

The Manchus were a non-Han group from the steppes northeast of China who conquered the Ming Dynasty in 1644 and founded the Qing Dynasty. They ruled all of China — plus expanded territory into Mongolia, Tibet, Xinjiang, and Taiwan — until 1912, making them one of the longest-lived dynasties in Chinese history.

The Manchus faced the same challenge the Mongols had: ruling a vast Han Chinese population as a small foreign elite. Their solution was different. Rather than ruling like outsiders, they adopted the Chinese imperial system wholesale:

• Kept the Confucian civil service examination, recruiting Han bureaucrats.
• Maintained traditional Chinese architecture, cuisine, court ritual, and Mandate of Heaven ideology.
• Reserved top military and government positions for Manchus, but staffed the bureaucracy with Han Chinese.
• Required Han men to wear the Manchu queue (long braided hair) — a visible symbol of submission.

Two emperors are required by the CED:

KANGXI (r. 1661–1722)
Stabilized and expanded the Qing. Tolerant of Christianity (Jesuits at his court). Patron of art and scholarship. The longest-reigning emperor in Chinese history at his time.
QIANLONG (r. 1735–1796)
Pushed the Qing to its greatest territorial extent. Patron of culture but also began the dynasty's slide into corruption and stagnation. Famously rebuffed Britain's Macartney Mission (1793) saying China had no need of European goods.
Required Illustrative Examples (Qing): Manchu conquest of Ming · Qing Dynasty · Kangxi · Qianlong · queue (forced Manchu hairstyle) · maintained civil service exam · imperial architecture · Macartney Mission
Topic 3.2 — Empires: Administration

Topic 3.2 is the CED's deep dive on how rulers actually ran these huge empires. The learning objective: explain how rulers used a variety of methods to legitimize and consolidate their power in land-based empires from 1450 to 1750. The reasoning skill is Causation.

Five categories of administrative method appear repeatedly on the exam:

1. PROFESSIONAL BUREAUCRACIES
Salaried officials replacing hereditary nobles. Ottoman devshirme → Janissaries; Qing civil service exam; Mughal mansabdari rank system; Tokugawa salaried samurai bureaucratized into officials.
2. NEW TAX SYSTEMS
Ottoman iltizam (tax farming); Mughal zamindar system (local lords collected); Ming/Qing silver tax; Russian poll tax on serfs. Most strained peasant producers, sometimes prompting rebellions.
3. RELIGIOUS LEGITIMATION
Ottoman Caliph claim; Safavid Twelver Shi'ism as state religion; Akbar's religious tolerance; Qing Mandate of Heaven; European divine right of kings.
4. ART & ARCHITECTURE
Public displays of imperial grandeur. Suleymaniye Mosque (Ottoman); Taj Mahal (Mughal); Imperial mosque of Isfahan (Safavid); Versailles (Bourbon France); Forbidden City expansions (Qing).
5. INSCRIPTIONS & PROPAGANDA
Imperial portraiture (Qing emperors), royal chronicles (Mughal Akbarnama), public processions, and minted coinage all communicated state power to subjects who could not read.

The European parallel is part of the CED here too. France under Louis XIV (r. 1643–1715) provides the absolutism case study: divine right of kings, professional army of half a million men, intendants (centralized administrators), state-sponsored manufacturing, and the spectacle of Versailles. Russia under Peter the Great (r. 1682–1725) ruthlessly Westernized state administration, military, and even noble dress — building St. Petersburg as Russia's "window on the West."

Required Illustrative Examples (3.2): Ottoman devshirme · Janissaries · Mughal zamindar · Ottoman iltizam (tax farming) · Qing civil service exam · Tokugawa salaried samurai · Versailles · Louis XIV / divine right · Peter the Great · St. Petersburg · Suleymaniye Mosque · Taj Mahal · Akbarnama
AP Exam tip: Topic 3.2 is the CED's most direct test of causation. The standard SAQ asks: "Identify ONE method rulers used to legitimize their authority and explain its effects." Have a specific example ready from each empire — devshirme (Ottoman), zamindar (Mughal), Mandate of Heaven (Qing), divine right (Bourbon France).
Topic 3.3 — Empires: Belief Systems

Topic 3.3 is about how religion shaped this period — both as a tool of imperial legitimation (above) and as a source of internal conflict and reform. Three major religious developments matter:

1. The Sunni–Shi'a divide hardened. The Safavid imposition of Twelver Shi'ism made Persia a Shi'a stronghold permanently — pitted against Sunni Ottomans (west) and Sunni Mughals (east). The Battle of Chaldiran (1514) marked this religious split as a geopolitical reality. Sufism remained important across the Sunni world as a populist, mystical alternative to orthodox practice.

2. The Protestant Reformation (Europe) shattered Latin Christianity. Martin Luther nailed his 95 Theses to a Wittenberg church door in 1517 — objecting to indulgences and Church corruption. His ideas (justification by faith alone, sola scriptura, the priesthood of all believers) spread fast thanks to the printing press (a Topic 3.1 enabling technology). John Calvin in Geneva developed predestination theology. Henry VIII created the Church of England in 1534 for political reasons.

The Catholic Counter-Reformation responded: the Council of Trent (1545–63) reformed Church practice while reaffirming traditional doctrine; the Jesuits (founded 1540) became a global missionary order. Religious wars devastated central Europe — the Thirty Years' War (1618–1648) killed an estimated 4–8 million people in the Holy Roman Empire and ended only with the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which established the principle that each ruler chose the religion of his realm.

3. New syncretic and reform movements emerged:

SIKHISM
Founded by Guru Nanak in northern India (c. 1500). Blended Hindu and Islamic elements: monotheistic, rejecting caste, emphasizing devotion. By the 17th century the Sikhs developed into a militarized community in response to Mughal persecution.
DIN-I ILAHI
Akbar's syncretic court religion (1582). Drew elements from Islam, Hinduism, Zoroastrianism, Jainism, and Christianity. Never widely adopted but symbolized Mughal religious experimentalism.
PROTESTANTISM
Lutheran, Calvinist, Anglican, Anabaptist branches all emerged in this era. The diffusion of these ideas was made possible by the printing press.
NEW BUDDHIST & CHINESE TRADITIONS
Wahhabism (Arabian peninsula, 18th century) called for return to "pure" Islam. Chinese Neo-Confucianism remained the orthodox state philosophy under the Qing.
Required Illustrative Examples (3.3): Protestant Reformation · Martin Luther · 95 Theses · John Calvin · Anglican Church / Henry VIII · Catholic Counter-Reformation · Council of Trent · Jesuits · Thirty Years' War · Peace of Westphalia (1648) · Sunni–Shi'a conflict · Sikhism · Guru Nanak · Din-i Ilahi · Wahhabism · Sufism
Topic 3.4 — Comparison in Land-Based Empires

Topic 3.4 is the unit's skills topic. The reasoning process is Comparison; the suggested skill is 6.B: Support an argument using specific and relevant evidence. No new content — the topic asks you to compare across the four required empires.

The most-tested comparisons:

METHODS OF EXPANSION
All four used gunpowder weapons + professional armies, but specifics differ. Ottoman Janissaries vs. Safavid ghulams vs. Mughal mansabdari cavalry vs. Manchu banner armies.
METHODS OF ADMINISTRATION
Centralization vs. delegation. Ottoman millet system (autonomy by religion); Mughal zamindar (autonomy by region); Qing civil service exam (uniform Han bureaucracy); Safavid centralized but smaller.
RELIGIOUS LEGITIMATION
Ottoman Caliph + millet tolerance; Safavid Shi'a state religion; Mughal swung from Akbar's tolerance to Aurangzeb's orthodoxy; Qing Mandate of Heaven + religious flexibility.
RULING ELITES vs. SUBJECTS
All four ruled diverse subject populations: Ottomans (Christians, Jews, Muslims); Safavids (Sunnis, Christians, Zoroastrians); Mughals (Muslims, Hindus, Sikhs); Qing (Han, Mongols, Tibetans). Each developed distinct strategies.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks: "Compare the methods used by rulers of two land-based empires to legitimize their power, c. 1450–1750." Have one specific institution or policy ready from each of the four empires — devshirme (Ottoman), zamindar (Mughal), Mandate of Heaven (Qing), Twelver Shi'ism (Safavid). Three named examples, one per empire, and you can write any prompt on this period.
