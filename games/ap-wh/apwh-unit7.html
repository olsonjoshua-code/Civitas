Unit 7 Briefing — Global Conflict
Two world wars and an interwar economic catastrophe defined the first half of the 20th century. Old empires collapsed; new ideologies (communism, fascism, totalitarianism) replaced liberal democracy in much of Europe and Asia. Total war mobilized entire populations. Mass atrocities — from the Armenian Genocide to the Holocaust to the Holodomor — killed tens of millions. The reasoning skill is overwhelmingly Causation; Topic 7.9 makes it explicit.
Topic 7.1 — Shifting Power After 1900

By 1900, the global political order was unstable. Three forces converged:

• Old multinational empires — Ottoman, Russian, Austro-Hungarian, Qing — were strained by nationalism, industrialization, and military defeats.
• New industrial powers — Germany, Japan, the U.S. — demanded a larger share of global influence.
• Revolutionary ideologies — socialism, anarchism, anti-colonial nationalism — threatened existing regimes.

The Russian Revolution (1917) is the most-tested event in Topic 7.1.

CAUSES
Tsarist autocracy + industrial modernization without political reform; humiliating defeat in Russo-Japanese War (1904–05); failed Revolution of 1905 ("Bloody Sunday"); WWI catastrophe (millions of casualties, food shortages, mutinies).
FEBRUARY REVOLUTION (1917)
Tsar Nicholas II abdicated. Provisional Government took over but kept Russia in WWI — a fatal mistake.
OCTOBER REVOLUTION (1917)
Vladimir Lenin and the Bolsheviks seized power, promising "Peace, Land, Bread." Established the world's first communist state.
RUSSIAN CIVIL WAR (1918–22)
Red Army (Bolsheviks) vs. White Army (anti-communists, monarchists, foreign powers). Bolsheviks won. Soviet Union formally established 1922. Foreign powers (including U.S., UK, Japan) intervened against the Bolsheviks — lasting source of Soviet distrust of the West.

The Mexican Revolution (1910–1920) is the CED's other major early-20th-century revolution. Causes: long dictatorship of Porfirio Díaz, vast inequality, foreign (U.S., British) economic dominance. Leaders: Francisco Madero, Emiliano Zapata ("Land and Liberty"), Pancho Villa. Resulted in the Mexican Constitution of 1917 (land reform, labor rights) and eventually the long rule of the PRI (Institutional Revolutionary Party) from 1929.

Collapse of empires:

• Qing China — fell in the Xinhai Revolution (1911) after losses to Japan and the Boxer Rebellion fallout. Sun Yat-sen led the Republic of China (1912), but the country fragmented into warlord-controlled regions.
• Ottoman Empire — "Sick man of Europe." Young Turks revolted (1908) trying to modernize; Ottoman defeat in WWI ended the empire. Mustafa Kemal (Atatürk) founded the modern Republic of Turkey (1923).
• Austro-Hungarian Empire — dissolved at end of WWI into Austria, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and parts of Poland and Italy.
• Russian Empire — replaced by USSR.

Topic 7.2 — Causes of World War I

The causes of WWI are commonly memorized with the acronym MAIN (or sometimes MANIA): Militarism, Alliances, Imperialism, Nationalism (and Assassination as the trigger).

MILITARISM
Massive arms buildups across Europe. The Anglo-German naval race — Britain's Dreadnought battleships vs. Germany's High Seas Fleet — was the most visible. Mass conscript armies on the continent.
ALLIANCES
Triple Alliance: Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy (originally). Triple Entente: France, Russia, Britain. Designed to deter war — instead they ensured a small dispute would pull in everyone.
IMPERIALISM
Colonial rivalries especially in Africa and the Balkans. Germany was a latecomer to empire and resented the British/French headstart. Multiple "Moroccan crises" before 1914.
NATIONALISM
Pan-Slavic nationalism in the Balkans threatened multinational Austria-Hungary. German nationalism. French desire to recover Alsace-Lorraine (lost in 1871).
THE ASSASSINATION
June 28, 1914: Serbian nationalist Gavrilo Princip assassinated Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, in Sarajevo. Austria-Hungary's ultimatum to Serbia triggered the alliance system.
JULY CRISIS
Five weeks of escalation: Austria-Hungary declares war on Serbia → Russia mobilizes → Germany declares war on Russia and France → Germany invades Belgium → Britain enters. By August 4, 1914, all major powers were at war.
Topic 7.3 — Conducting World War I

WWI was the first "total war" — entire economies and populations mobilized for industrial-scale slaughter. ~17 million killed, ~20 million wounded.

New military technology made WWI uniquely lethal:

• Machine guns — defensive dominance.
• Trench warfare on the Western Front — from Belgium to Switzerland. Stalemate for 4 years.
• Poison gas — chlorine, phosgene, mustard gas. First mass-deployed at Ypres (1915).
• Tanks — first used by Britain at the Somme (1916).
• Aircraft — reconnaissance, then bombers and fighters.
• Submarines (U-boats) — Germany's unrestricted submarine warfare sank merchant ships, ultimately drawing the U.S. into the war (1917).
• Battleships — the Battle of Jutland (1916) was the largest naval battle of the war.

Total war on the home front:

• War economies — governments rationed food, fixed prices, nationalized industries.
• Women in the workforce — replaced men in factories, on farms, in offices. Major catalyst for women's suffrage after the war (UK 1918, US 1920).
• Propaganda — posters, films, newspapers all enlisted to maintain morale and demonize the enemy.
• Colonial soldiers — ~4 million colonial troops fought for European powers (Indians, Africans, Vietnamese, Caribbeans). Many returned home with revolutionary ideas.

The end of WWI and the Treaty of Versailles (1919).

U.S. entry (1917), German exhaustion, and the Allied counteroffensive ended the war on November 11, 1918. The peace conference in Paris produced the Treaty of Versailles — a CED-required document.

Key terms imposed on Germany:

• "War guilt clause" (Article 231) blamed Germany for the war.
• Massive reparations ($33 billion).
• Lost territory: Alsace-Lorraine to France, German colonies as League of Nations mandates.
• Military restricted to 100,000 men; no air force, no submarines.
• Created the League of Nations (Wilson's idea, but the U.S. Senate rejected joining).

The harsh terms left Germany humiliated and resentful — setting up the rise of Hitler and WWII.

AP Exam tip: The Treaty of Versailles is one of the most-tested documents in the entire course. Be ready to argue it as a cause of WWII via the harsh terms imposed on Germany. The CED specifically wants this causal chain.
Topic 7.4 — Economy in the Interwar Period

The 1920s and 1930s brought economic chaos. The Great Depression (1929–c. 1939) was the worst global economic crisis in industrial history. It began with the U.S. Stock Market Crash (October 1929) and spread worldwide because of the gold standard and U.S. financial dominance.

Effects:

• Mass unemployment — ~25% in the U.S. and Germany at peak.
• Trade collapse — protectionist tariffs (U.S. Smoot-Hawley Tariff, 1930) made it worse.
• Bank failures wiped out savings.
• Loss of faith in liberal democracy and capitalism — the central political consequence.

Government responses ranged across the political spectrum:

U.S. — THE NEW DEAL
Franklin D. Roosevelt's program (1933+). Government employment programs, banking reform, Social Security. Expanded the federal government's economic role enormously while preserving democracy and capitalism.
USSR — FIVE-YEAR PLANS
Stalin's command-economy industrialization (1928+). Massive forced industrialization at human cost. Collectivization of agriculture led to mass famine including the Holodomor. USSR was the only major economy to grow during the Depression.
FASCIST ITALY — CORPORATIST STATE
Mussolini's regime (from 1922) organized the economy into state-controlled "corporations" representing industries. Pioneered the fascist economic model.
NAZI GERMANY — REARMAMENT
Hitler's regime (from 1933) used massive public works (Autobahn) and military rearmament to end unemployment. Funded by debt and seizures from Jewish citizens.
Topic 7.5 — Unresolved Tensions After WWI

Topic 7.5 covers the political tensions of the interwar period — especially the rise of fascism, the spread of communism, and anti-colonial nationalism.

Fascism emerged as a powerful new ideology:

• Extreme nationalism and ethnic identity, often racial.
• Glorification of the state, the leader, and military force.
• Rejection of liberal democracy AND of communism.
• Suppression of political opposition, free press, labor unions.

Italian Fascism — Benito Mussolini and his Blackshirts seized power in the March on Rome (1922). The first fascist state.

Nazi Germany — Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party rose to power in the failing Weimar Republic. Hitler became Chancellor (Jan 1933); used the Reichstag Fire as pretext to suspend civil liberties; established totalitarian dictatorship. Persecuted Jews, communists, Roma, disabled people, LGBTQ+ people, political opponents. Mein Kampf (1925) outlined his ideology of Aryan racial supremacy and Lebensraum (living space) in Eastern Europe.

The Spanish Civil War (1936–39) saw fascist Francisco Franco defeat the Republican government with German and Italian help — a preview of WWII.

Anti-colonial movements grew in this period:

• India — Mahatma Gandhi led non-violent resistance against British rule. Salt March (1930) was iconic. The Indian National Congress demanded full independence.
• China — The May Fourth Movement (1919) protested against the Treaty of Versailles giving German concessions in China to Japan rather than back to China. Sparked nationalism and the founding of the Chinese Communist Party (1921) under Mao Zedong. Chinese Civil War between Communists and the Nationalist (KMT) Party of Chiang Kai-shek began 1927.
• Africa & Vietnam — Pan-African Congresses; Ho Chi Minh petitioned at Versailles for Vietnamese self-determination (rejected).

Topic 7.6 — Causes of World War II

Causes of WWII flowed directly from the failures of the post-WWI settlement:

TREATY OF VERSAILLES BACKLASH
Germany's humiliation and reparations created economic and political conditions for Hitler's rise. The Treaty's "war guilt clause" became a Nazi rallying cry.
GREAT DEPRESSION
Economic collapse destroyed faith in liberal democracy. People turned to extreme ideologies. Hitler's promises to end unemployment and restore German greatness won mass support.
FAILURE OF LEAGUE OF NATIONS
The League couldn't stop Japanese invasion of Manchuria (1931), Italian invasion of Ethiopia (1935), or Hitler's remilitarization. Showed collective security wasn't working.
APPEASEMENT
Britain (Chamberlain) and France let Hitler annex Austria (Anschluss, 1938) and the Sudetenland (Munich Agreement, 1938) hoping to avoid war. Encouraged further aggression.
JAPANESE EXPANSIONISM
Japan invaded Manchuria (1931), then China proper (1937). The brutal Rape of Nanjing (1937–38) killed ~200,000 Chinese civilians. Set up the Pacific theater.
NAZI-SOVIET PACT (Aug 1939)
Hitler and Stalin secretly agreed to divide Poland and Eastern Europe. Removed the threat of two-front war. Hitler invaded Poland September 1, 1939.
Topic 7.7 — Conducting World War II

WWII was the deadliest conflict in human history — ~75 million dead, including ~50 million civilians.

European theater:

• Blitzkrieg ("lightning war") — Germany used tanks, aircraft, mechanized infantry to overrun Poland (1939), then France (1940) in just six weeks.
• Battle of Britain (1940) — first major German defeat. Britain's RAF (and Polish, Czech, Canadian pilots) won the air war.
• Operation Barbarossa (June 1941) — Germany invaded the USSR, breaking the Nazi-Soviet Pact. Massive initial gains; halted at Moscow (winter 1941).
• Stalingrad (1942–43) — turning point on the Eastern Front. Soviet victory at horrendous cost.
• D-Day (June 6, 1944) — Allied invasion of Normandy. Western Front opened.
• Berlin fell to the Soviets May 1945; Hitler killed himself; Germany surrendered.

Pacific theater:

• Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) — Japanese attack brought the U.S. into the war.
• Midway (1942) — turning point in the Pacific. U.S. naval victory.
• Island-hopping — U.S. campaign across the Pacific. Brutal battles at Iwo Jima, Okinawa.
• Atomic bombs on Hiroshima (Aug 6, 1945) and Nagasaki (Aug 9, 1945). Killed ~200,000 mostly civilians. Japan surrendered August 15, 1945.

Total war — even more total than WWI.

• Strategic bombing of cities (Coventry, Hamburg, Dresden, Tokyo) deliberately targeted civilians. Tokyo firebombing killed more people than either atomic bomb.
• Women's labor mobilized at unprecedented scale ("Rosie the Riveter" in U.S.).
• Colonial troops again played key roles — Indian, African, Caribbean, ANZAC forces.
• Resistance movements in occupied Europe (French Resistance, Polish Home Army, Yugoslav Partisans).
• Internment — the U.S. interned ~120,000 Japanese-Americans; UK and Canada also interned "enemy aliens."

Topic 7.8 — Mass Atrocities of the 20th Century

Topic 7.8 is the CED's required survey of state-sponsored mass killings. The 20th century saw genocide on industrial scales. This is necessary but heavy material; treat it with the gravity it deserves.

ARMENIAN GENOCIDE (1915–23)
During WWI, the Ottoman government deported and massacred ~1.5 million Armenians. Often called the first genocide of the 20th century. Turkey still officially disputes the term.
HOLODOMOR (1932–33)
Stalin-engineered famine in Ukraine during forced collectivization. Killed an estimated 3.5–7 million Ukrainians. Now widely recognized as genocide.
THE HOLOCAUST (1941–45)
Nazi Germany's systematic murder of approximately 6 million Jews — about two-thirds of European Jewry. Also targeted: ~200,000+ Roma, disabled people, LGBTQ+ people, Poles, Soviet POWs, political opponents. Industrial-scale killing centers (Auschwitz, Treblinka, Sobibor). The "Final Solution."
CAMBODIAN GENOCIDE (1975–79)
The Khmer Rouge under Pol Pot killed approximately 1.5–2 million Cambodians (~25% of population) in radical agrarian-communist purges. Targeted intellectuals, ethnic minorities, anyone associated with the previous regime.
RWANDAN GENOCIDE (1994)
Hutu extremists killed approximately 800,000 Tutsi and moderate Hutus over 100 days. Ethnic divisions hardened by Belgian colonial rule. International community failed to intervene.
BOSNIAN WAR / SREBRENICA (1995)
Bosnian Serb forces killed approximately 8,000 Bosniak (Muslim) men and boys at Srebrenica. Ruled genocide by international tribunals. Part of the broader Yugoslav wars.

Common themes across these atrocities — the CED expects you to identify patterns:

• State sponsorship — governments organize the killing.
• Dehumanizing ideology (racial, ethnic, class).
• Crisis or war as cover/trigger.
• Bureaucratic organization.
• International failure to intervene in time.

The post-WWII Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) and the Genocide Convention (1948) were direct responses to the Holocaust — attempts to make such atrocities both legally prohibited and politically actionable.

Topic 7.9 — Causation in Global Conflict

Topic 7.9 is the unit's skills topic. Reasoning process: Causation. Suggested skill: Argumentation 6.B — support an argument with specific and relevant evidence.

Most-tested causation chains:

IMPERIALISM & NATIONALISM → WWI
19th-c. imperial competition + nationalism + alliance system + assassination → WWI. The CED's #1 causation chain for this unit.
WWI → WWII
Treaty of Versailles + Great Depression + appeasement → rise of fascism → WWII. The most-tested chain on the AP exam.
TOTAL WAR → SOCIAL CHANGE
Both wars accelerated women's suffrage, decolonization (colonial soldiers returned with new political ideas), and the welfare state.
WWII → COLD WAR (Unit 8)
U.S.-Soviet alliance against fascism dissolves into ideological rivalry → Cold War (1947–91). Direct transition into Unit 8.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks: "Evaluate the extent to which the causes of World War II differed from the causes of World War I." Have two specific differences (WWII: rise of totalitarian ideology vs. WWI: alliance system; WWII: failure of appeasement vs. WWI: assassination/July Crisis) and one similarity (both rooted in unresolved national rivalries) ready.
