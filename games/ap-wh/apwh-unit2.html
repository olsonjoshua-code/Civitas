Unit 2 Briefing — Networks of Exchange
Four major trade networks tied the world together c. 1200–1450. Read each network's briefing before its round. Every fact below comes from the AP World History: Modern Course and Exam Description (CED). Pay close attention to causes and effects — this unit's reasoning process is overwhelmingly Causation.
Topic 2.1 — The Silk Roads

The Silk Roads were not a single road but a network of overland trade routes stretching from China across Central Asia to the Mediterranean. They had existed for centuries before 1200, but flourished spectacularly during this era thanks to two forces: the rise of more sophisticated trading practices and the unification of Eurasia under Mongol rule (see Topic 2.2).

The Silk Roads were the world's longest-running long-distance trade system — and luxury goods, not bulk commodities, were what made them work. Anything heavy or low-value couldn't justify the extreme cost of overland transport across deserts and mountains. So the cargo was high-value-per-pound:

FROM CHINA
Silk (the namesake), porcelain ("china"), tea, paper, gunpowder.
FROM CENTRAL ASIA
Horses (especially the prized "heavenly horses" of Ferghana), jade, gems.
FROM INDIA
Cotton textiles, spices, precious stones.
FROM PERSIA / ME
Glasswork, fine carpets, dates, advanced steel.
FROM EUROPE
Wool textiles, silver, slaves, amber. Europe ran a chronic trade deficit with the East.
EVERYWHERE
Religions, technologies, ideas, diseases — the unintended cargo.

Innovations that made expanded trade possible. The CED is specific about which technological and commercial innovations to know:

• Caravanserai — roadside inns built about every 25 miles (a day's travel) along the Silk Roads. They provided shelter, water, food for travelers and their pack animals, and were funded by Muslim charitable foundations or local rulers. Without them, long-distance overland trade was nearly impossible.
• Saddles for camels — specifically the camel saddle that allowed riders to carry heavy loads across deserts. Made the Silk Roads' western and Saharan extensions practical.
• The magnetic compass — Chinese invention that revolutionized navigation when adopted along sea routes.
• The astrolabe — refined by Muslim scholars; allowed navigators to calculate latitude using stars.
• Improved shipbuilding — larger, more seaworthy vessels (the Chinese junk; the Indian Ocean dhow with its triangular lateen sail).
• Bills of exchange (a kind of medieval check) — merchants no longer had to carry actual gold across long distances. Originated in the Islamic world.
• Banking houses and credit — large merchant families could finance trades thousands of miles away.
• Hanseatic League — northern European merchant confederation that did similar work for Baltic and North Sea trade.

Required Illustrative Examples (2.1): Silk Roads · caravanserai · camel saddle · compass · astrolabe · larger ship designs (Chinese junks, Indian dhows) · paper money · bills of exchange · banking houses · Hanseatic League · Kashgar · Samarkand
AP Exam tip: The CED's most-tested concept here is "how innovation enabled the expansion of trade." Don't just memorize the list of inventions — be ready to explain cause and effect. (Caravanserai → safer travel → longer trade journeys → more goods exchanged.) Causation is the focal reasoning skill.
Topic 2.2 — The Mongol Empire and the Making of the Modern World

The Mongol Empire — founded by Genghis Khan (Temujin, unified the Mongol tribes c. 1206) — became the largest contiguous land empire in human history, stretching from Korea to Hungary at its peak. The Mongols' impact on this period is nearly impossible to overstate, which is why the CED gives them their own topic.

The Mongols rose because of military innovations: superb mounted archery, brutal effectiveness against fortified cities (using Chinese siege technology and engineers), psychological warfare (offering surrender or annihilation), and an extraordinarily flexible command system that promoted by merit rather than birth.

After Genghis's death (1227), the empire was divided among his descendants into four khanates:

YUAN DYNASTY (China)
Founded by Kublai Khan (1271). The Mongols ruled China itself — the first time non-Han rulers held the entire Chinese state. Marco Polo visited and described its wealth. Later overthrown by the Han Chinese Ming Dynasty (1368).
GOLDEN HORDE (Russia)
Mongol-Turkic state ruling the Russian principalities for ~250 years. Russian princes had to travel to the Khan to be confirmed. The legacy: Russia's later authoritarian centralization is often traced to this experience.
ILKHANATE (Persia / Middle East)
Mongol state ruling Persia and Iraq. Famously sacked Baghdad (1258), ending the Abbasid Caliphate. Eventually adopted Islam.
CHAGATAI KHANATE (Central Asia)
Held the central steppe heartland. Less integrated with sedentary civilizations than the others. Source of Timur (Tamerlane), who in the late 14th century rebuilt a fearsome Turco-Mongol successor empire.

The Pax Mongolica ("Mongol Peace") was the period of relative stability across the unified Eurasian space the Mongols ruled. While the Mongol conquests themselves were catastrophically destructive (some cities lost 90% of their populations), once the empire was established, the Mongols actively encouraged trade and made it safer than it had been in centuries.

The CED expects you to know specific consequences:

• Eurasian trade revival — safe overland routes meant Silk Roads commerce reached unprecedented volume.
• Cultural and technological diffusion — gunpowder, paper, the compass, and printing all spread west more rapidly under Mongol rule.
• Diplomatic exchanges — Mongol courts welcomed envoys, scholars, and missionaries from Europe and the Islamic world.
• The spread of the Black Death — the same trade routes that spread silk also spread the bubonic plague (see Topic 2.6).
• Long-term political legacy — the Mongols' centralized administrative methods influenced Russian autocracy, the Ming Dynasty's restoration, and Mughal India.

Required Illustrative Examples (2.2): Genghis Khan · Kublai Khan · Pax Mongolica · Yuan Dynasty · Golden Horde · Ilkhanate · Chagatai Khanate · Marco Polo · Sack of Baghdad (1258) · Timur (Tamerlane)
AP Exam tip: The CED's signature claim about Topic 2.2 is in the topic title itself: the Mongols were "the making of the modern world." Be ready to argue both sides — the Mongols caused massive destruction and dramatically increased global connectivity. The most common question stem: "Which best explains the impact of the Mongol Empire on Afro-Eurasian trade?"
Topic 2.3 — Exchange in the Indian Ocean

The Indian Ocean trade network connected East Africa, the Middle East, India, Southeast Asia, and China — and was, by 1200, the world's richest and most active maritime trade system. Unlike the Silk Roads (luxury goods, high cost), Indian Ocean ships could carry far heavier cargo: bulk goods like timber, grain, rice, ivory, and iron moved alongside the luxuries.

What made it work was a single environmental feature: the monsoon winds. Every year, predictable seasonal winds blew from the southwest in summer (carrying ships from Africa to India) and from the northeast in winter (carrying them back). Mariners who learned the monsoon system could move enormous distances reliably. Stranded merchants — ones who missed the wind — would have to live for months in foreign port cities, which had a profound cultural side effect: they intermarried, learned local languages, converted, and helped found diasporic communities.

Diasporic merchant communities — the CED specifically wants you to know this concept. These were communities of foreign merchants living long-term in port cities, blending into local culture but maintaining their own networks back home:

• Arab and Persian Muslim merchants in East African ports — the foundation of Swahili culture (Bantu + Arabic = Swahili language).
• Muslim merchants in Indian ports like Calicut on the Malabar Coast.
• Chinese merchants in Southeast Asia, especially Malacca and Java.
• Hindu Tamil merchants in Southeast Asia.
Diasporic communities became the engine of religious and cultural diffusion: Muslim merchants spread Islam to Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, and East Africa primarily by trading and marrying locally — not by conquest.

Major Indian Ocean ports and the people who built them:

CALICUT (India)
Spice port on India's southwest coast. Hindu rulers but a multireligious commercial city. Visited by Ibn Battuta, Zheng He, and (in 1498) Vasco da Gama.
MALACCA (Malaysia)
Strategic port at the strait between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. Founded c. 1400 and quickly became wealthy. Converted to Islam — the model for the Islamization of maritime Southeast Asia.
SWAHILI CITY-STATES
Kilwa, Mombasa, Sofala, Mogadishu — East African Bantu-speaking ports linked to Arab and Persian merchants. Exported gold (from Great Zimbabwe), ivory, slaves; imported textiles, porcelain.
MALDIVES
Cowrie shells from the Maldives served as a currency across much of the Indian Ocean basin and into West Africa via trans-Saharan trade — a tiny island chain at the center of a global money system.

The Ming Voyages of Zheng He (1405–1433) were a stunning Chinese state intervention in Indian Ocean trade. The Yongle Emperor sent admiral Zheng He — a Muslim eunuch — on seven enormous expeditions, with treasure fleets of up to 300 ships and 27,000 sailors. These voyages reached East Africa and brought back giraffes, gold, and tribute from dozens of states.

Then, abruptly, the Ming court ended them. The new emperor decided overseas voyages were wasteful and a distraction from threats on the northern border. The fleets were dismantled; the records were destroyed. China withdrew from sea-based imperialism just as European states were beginning to develop it — a missed turning point in world history.

Required Illustrative Examples (2.3): Indian Ocean trade · monsoon winds · dhow · Swahili Coast · Kilwa · Calicut · Malacca · Gujarat · Zanj (East Africa) · diasporic merchant communities · Zheng He · Ibn Battuta · Margery Kempe · cowrie shells
Topic 2.4 — Trans-Saharan Trade Routes

The Trans-Saharan trade routes connected sub-Saharan West Africa to the Mediterranean world by camel caravan across the Sahara desert. Like the Silk Roads, these routes existed long before 1200 but expanded dramatically in this period due to two factors: the spread of Islam through North and West Africa, and improvements in camel-saddle technology that made bulk transport more efficient.

The trade goods. The classic exchange was gold for salt:

SOUTHWARD (Mediterranean → West Africa)
Salt (essential for survival in tropical climates), textiles, glassware, books, horses.
NORTHWARD (West Africa → Mediterranean)
Gold (West Africa was the medieval world's largest gold producer), enslaved people, ivory, kola nuts.

By 1300 West African gold was financing the Mediterranean economy — coins minted in Italian city-states often used West African gold without even knowing where it came from.

The empires that grew rich from this trade:

• Mali Empire — controlled the gold-salt trade routes through cities like Timbuktu, Gao, and Djenné. Made enormously wealthy under Mansa Musa (r. 1312–1337), whose famous 1324–25 hajj to Mecca distributed so much gold in Cairo that the value of gold there allegedly fell for years. Mali also drew Islamic scholars and architecture — Timbuktu's Sankoré Madrasah became a major center of learning.
• Songhai Empire — rose from c. 1450 to dominate West Africa into the 1500s. Continued the gold-salt trade and Islamic scholarship of Mali.
• Hausa Kingdoms — northern Nigerian city-states that connected to trans-Saharan trade as well.

The trans-Saharan trade also spread Islam through West Africa. Muslim merchants converted local rulers, who built mosques, hired Muslim scholars and bureaucrats, and integrated Islamic law into governance — a process visible in Mali's transformation under Mansa Musa.

Required Illustrative Examples (2.4): Trans-Saharan trade · Mali Empire · Mansa Musa · Sundiata · Timbuktu · Sankoré Madrasah · Ghana Empire (predecessor) · Songhai Empire · gold-salt trade · camel saddle · Tuareg
AP Exam tip: The CED here uses Mansa Musa repeatedly as an example of cultural diffusion through trade. His pilgrimage didn't just spread gold — it brought Egyptian and Arabian scholars, books, and architects back to Mali, transforming Timbuktu. When you see "trans-Saharan trade" on the test, the test is usually really asking about how trade spread Islam and Islamic learning.
Topic 2.5 — Cultural Consequences of Connectivity

Topic 2.5 is the "so what?" of all the trade networks. The CED groups the consequences of connectivity into cultural (this topic) and environmental (next topic). The reasoning skill here is Sourcing and Situation (skill 2.A) — identifying point of view, purpose, situation, audience — because the CED expects you to read the writings of travelers like Ibn Battuta and Marco Polo as windows onto these consequences.

Trade networks didn't just move goods. They moved everything that travels with goods: religions, technologies, art, language, political ideas, and ways of organizing knowledge.

Religion spread along trade routes:

ISLAM
Spread to East Africa (Swahili coast), West Africa (Mali, Hausa), maritime Southeast Asia (Malacca, Indonesia, Philippines), and South Asia (Delhi Sultanate). Almost entirely peaceful diffusion through trade and Sufi missionaries.
BUDDHISM
Spread further along the Silk Roads (already established in China by 1200). New schools developed: Chan/Zen in China and Japan, Pure Land for the masses.
CHRISTIANITY
Less successful in this era — lost ground to Islam in the Middle East and North Africa — but spread along Mongol-era routes; Nestorian Christians lived in Mongol courts. The Pope sent the first Catholic missionaries to China.
SYNCRETIC TRADITIONS
Connectivity didn't just spread religions; it blended them. Sikhism (founded by Guru Nanak in 15th-c. India) blended Hindu and Islamic elements. Sufism merged with Hindu Bhakti devotion. Islamic art absorbed Persian, Indian, and Chinese motifs.

Technology, knowledge, and art also moved. The CED specifically expects you to know:

• Gunpowder, paper, printing, the compass — all Chinese inventions that spread west via the Silk Roads and Mongol-era exchanges. Each one transformed the cultures that received them.
• Greek classical texts — preserved by Muslim scholars, then translated from Arabic into Latin in al-Andalus (Spain) and Sicily, helping spark the European Renaissance.
• Mathematics — Indian numerals reached Europe via Arab intermediaries (we still call them "Arabic numerals"). Algebra is an Arabic word.
• Persian and Chinese artistic motifs appeared in tilework, textiles, and porcelain across Eurasia.
• Travelers' accounts — Marco Polo (Italian merchant in Yuan China), Ibn Battuta (Moroccan jurist who traveled Mali, India, China, and most of Dar al-Islam), and Margery Kempe (English Christian pilgrim who traveled to the Holy Land) — left written accounts that historians still rely on.

Required Illustrative Examples (2.5): Marco Polo · Ibn Battuta · Margery Kempe · Sufism · Sikhism · Bhakti · spread of Islam · Greek classical texts · Mongol diplomatic exchanges · Persian and Chinese artistic influence · gunpowder diffusion · paper diffusion
AP Exam tip: Topic 2.5 is where the College Board loves to put document-based questions. The standard prompt: "Using the document, explain how trade networks affected religious or cultural development." Have one specific example from each network ready (Silk Roads, Indian Ocean, Trans-Saharan, Mongol). Don't write generalities — cite specific people, places, or technologies.
Topic 2.6 — Environmental Consequences of Connectivity

Trade networks moved more than ideas; they also moved biological cargo — sometimes deliberately (crops), sometimes accidentally (diseases, pests). The CED's environmental theme (ENV) becomes a major focus starting here.

Crop diffusion changed how the world ate and worked. Specific examples the CED highlights:

• Champa rice — a fast-ripening, drought-resistant strain spread from Vietnam to Song China, allowing two harvests per year and supporting massive population growth (over 100 million people in China by 1100).
• Bananas — spread from Southeast Asia to East Africa via Indian Ocean trade well before 1200, then continued spreading inland. Bananas allowed African populations to thrive in tropical forest zones previously unsuitable for cereal agriculture.
• Citrus fruits — spread westward from Southeast Asia to the Mediterranean via Muslim merchants. The Spanish "naranja" comes from Sanskrit via Arabic.
• Sugarcane — originally Southeast Asian; spread by Muslim merchants throughout the Mediterranean and (later) into the New World, where it became central to the Atlantic slave trade.
• Cotton — Indian cotton textiles were already a global trade good; cotton cultivation also spread.

Disease diffusion was often catastrophic. The single most consequential example is the Black Death (also called the Bubonic Plague) of the 14th century:

The plague originated in Central Asia and was carried westward along Silk Roads and Mongol trade routes by infected fleas on rats traveling with caravans. It reached Crimea c. 1346, then jumped onto Genoese trading ships heading to Italy. Within five years (1347–1351) it killed an estimated 30–50% of Europe's population and caused similar devastation across the Islamic world and parts of Asia.

Consequences were enormous and the CED expects you to know them:
• Labor shortages — surviving peasants gained bargaining power; serfdom began to dissolve in Western Europe.
• Religious questioning — the Catholic Church's authority shaken; flagellant movements; pogroms against Jewish communities.
• Economic restructuring — concentration of wealth in surviving urban centers helped fund the Italian Renaissance.
• Long-term political effects — weakened feudalism and accelerated the rise of centralized monarchies.

Required Illustrative Examples (2.6): Champa rice · bananas · citrus · sugarcane · cotton diffusion · the Black Death (Bubonic Plague) · disease diffusion via Silk Roads · environmental degradation · overgrazing along trade routes
Topic 2.7 — Comparison of Economic Exchange

Topic 2.7 is a skills topic — the focal skill is Argumentation 6.B: Support an argument using specific and relevant evidence. No new content here. Instead, the CED asks you to compare across the four trade networks: Silk Roads, Mongol-era trade, Indian Ocean, Trans-Saharan.

The most-tested comparisons:

SCALE OF GOODS
Silk Roads: luxury only (high cost per pound). Indian Ocean: luxury and bulk (cheaper sea transport). Trans-Saharan: mixed. The pattern: water = bulk, land = luxury.
DOMINANT FAITH SPREAD
Silk Roads: Buddhism (earlier), then Islam. Indian Ocean: Islam, with some Buddhism. Trans-Saharan: Islam. Mongol: Buddhism, Christianity, Islam all moved.
TYPE OF MERCHANT
Silk Roads: long-distance overland traders, often Muslim. Indian Ocean: diasporic merchant communities in port cities. Trans-Saharan: caravan masters and Tuareg intermediaries.
STATE INVOLVEMENT
Silk Roads: states protected when convenient (Mongol Pax). Indian Ocean: Zheng He's voyages were the major state-led intervention. Trans-Saharan: Mali state heavily involved as gold-trade taxer.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks you to "compare the effects of two trade networks on the cultures involved." Have one specific example of cultural diffusion ready from each network — Mansa Musa's hajj (Trans-Saharan), the Islamization of Malacca (Indian Ocean), Marco Polo's account of Yuan China (Silk Roads/Mongol). One example per network, named & specific, and you can write any prompt on this period.
