Unit 1 Briefing — The Global Tapestry
Six world regions, c. 1200–1450. Read each region's briefing before its round. Every fact below comes from the AP World History: Modern Course and Exam Description (CED) and is testable on the exam.
Topic 1.1 — Developments in East Asia, c. 1200 – c. 1450

Song Dynasty China (960–1279) was the wealthiest, most populous, and most technologically advanced civilization in the world during this period. The Song retained the centralized imperial bureaucracy and expanded the civil service examination system based on the Confucian classics — in theory, any educated male could rise to power through merit, though wealthy families had massive advantages.

The Song also revived and reformed Confucian thought into Neo-Confucianism, blending classical Confucian ethics with elements of Buddhism and Daoism. Neo-Confucianism reinforced strict patriarchal hierarchies — the most visible consequence was the spread of foot binding among elite women, a practice that spread downward through the social hierarchy over the following centuries.

Economic and technological achievements were staggering: champa rice (a fast-ripening, drought-resistant strain imported from Vietnam) allowed two harvests per year and supported a population of over 100 million. The Song printed paper money (jiaozi), produced porcelain and silk for export, and built the world's largest cities — Kaifeng and Hangzhou had over a million people each. Inventions or refinements during this era included gunpowder, the magnetic compass, movable-type printing, and advanced shipbuilding (the Chinese junk).

Required Illustrative Examples (1.1): Champa rice · grand canal expansion · proto-industrial production · paper money · imperial bureaucracy · civil service examination · Neo-Confucianism · filial piety · Chinese tributary system

Cultural diffusion across East Asia. Chinese influence radiated outward to neighboring societies, but each region adapted Chinese ideas to local needs:

JAPAN (Heian → Kamakura)
Borrowed Chinese writing, Buddhism, and Confucian governance — but developed its own feudal system with the shogun, daimyo, and samurai. Japan's emperor became a ceremonial figure while real power belonged to the shogun (military ruler).
KOREA (Goryeo)
Adopted Chinese governance and the civil service exam more directly than Japan, but with a critical difference — only aristocrats (yangban) could take the exam. Korea remained more rigidly hierarchical.
VIETNAM (Đại Việt)
Adopted Confucian governance and the writing system but resisted Chinese rule and retained greater rights for women than China — including property rights and choice in marriage.
RELIGION
Buddhism spread along trade routes and was adapted into local schools — Chan/Zen in China and Japan, Pure Land Buddhism for the masses, and Theravada in Southeast Asia.
AP Exam tip: The CED specifies that you can use either Heian Japan or Kamakura Japan as your example — the testable point is Japanese feudalism with the shogun/daimyo/samurai structure. Don't confuse this with European feudalism; the test sometimes asks for a comparison.
Topic 1.2 — Developments in Dar al-Islam, c. 1200 – c. 1450

Dar al-Islam ("the abode of Islam") refers to the entire region under Muslim political or cultural rule — a vast zone stretching from the Iberian Peninsula across North Africa and the Middle East to India and the East Indies. By 1200, the unified Abbasid Caliphate had fragmented into competing states, but Islamic civilization was at its intellectual and economic peak.

The major successor states of the Abbasid era included:

SELJUK TURKS
A Turkic Muslim group from Central Asia who took political control of the Abbasid heartland (Iraq, Iran). The Abbasid caliph remained as a religious figurehead while Seljuk sultans held real power.
MAMLUK SULTANATE (Egypt)
Founded by Turkic slave-soldiers (mamluks) who seized power in Egypt. Famously defeated the Mongols at Ain Jalut (1260) — one of the few military defeats the Mongols ever suffered.
DELHI SULTANATE (India)
A series of five Turkic-Afghan dynasties (1206–1526) ruling much of northern India. Imposed Islamic governance over a Hindu majority — the long-term consequence was a deeply syncretic Indo-Islamic culture.
OTTOMAN BEGINNINGS
Founded c. 1299 by Osman I. Still a small Anatolian frontier state in this period — but expanding rapidly by 1450, on the verge of capturing Constantinople (which they did in 1453, technically marking the end of this unit).

Intellectual & cultural achievements. Even as political unity fragmented, Muslim scholars produced an unprecedented synthesis of Greek, Persian, Indian, and Chinese knowledge:

• Nasir al-Din al-Tusi made advances in astronomy and trigonometry that would influence Copernicus.
• Ibn Khaldun wrote the Muqaddimah, founding the modern study of history and sociology.
• 'A'ishah al-Ba'uniyyah was one of the most prolific female Sufi writers of the period.
• Sufism, the mystical branch of Islam, became the major vehicle for the spread of Islam — Sufi missionaries adapted Islamic teachings to local cultures, easing conversion in West Africa, Anatolia, and South/Southeast Asia.
• Muslim scholars preserved and translated Greek philosophical and scientific works (Aristotle, Galen, Ptolemy) into Arabic — these texts later moved into Latin Christendom via Spain and Sicily and helped fuel the Renaissance.

Required Illustrative Examples (1.2): Mamluk Sultanate · Seljuk Empire · Delhi Sultanate · House of Wisdom · Sufism · al-Tusi · Ibn Khaldun · 'A'ishah al-Ba'uniyyah
AP Exam tip: "Diffusion" and "preservation/transfer" of knowledge are the two most-tested concepts here. The CED specifically wants you to know that Muslim scholars translated and built on Greek classical texts and that this knowledge later transferred to Europe via Spain (al-Andalus) and Sicily.
Topic 1.3 — Developments in South & Southeast Asia, c. 1200 – c. 1450

South Asia in this period was politically fragmented but culturally vibrant. The dominant power across northern India was the Delhi Sultanate (see Topic 1.2), but in the south, powerful Hindu kingdoms persisted:

• Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646) — a major Hindu state that consolidated southern India in resistance to the Delhi Sultanate. Its capital, Vijayanagara, was reportedly one of the largest cities in the world.
• Rajput kingdoms — a network of Hindu warrior-aristocrat states in northwestern India that maintained Hindu rule despite the Sultanate's pressure.
• Chola Dynasty (already past its peak by 1200 but still influential) — had built a powerful maritime empire across the Indian Ocean.

Religious developments. South Asia was a religious crossroads where Hinduism, Buddhism, and Islam interacted intensely:

The Bhakti Movement was a Hindu devotional reform movement that bypassed the Brahmin priesthood and rejected the rigid caste system in favor of direct personal devotion to a chosen deity (often Vishnu, Shiva, or the Goddess). Bhakti poetry, often written in vernacular languages rather than Sanskrit, made Hinduism accessible to common people and women.

Meanwhile, Sufi missionaries were spreading Islam into South and Southeast Asia using a similar logic: emphasizing personal devotion and adapting Islamic teachings to local traditions. The result was massive religious blending — many South Asian Muslims today trace their conversion to a Sufi pir (saint), and Bhakti and Sufi poetry share a remarkably similar emotional vocabulary.

Southeast Asia was where two of the world's biggest oceanic trade systems met, and its states reflect the wealth that produced. Two patterns emerged:

SEA-BASED STATES
Srivijaya (Sumatra-based, peaked earlier) and Majapahit (Java, 1293–1527) controlled maritime trade through the Strait of Malacca. Wealth came from taxing commerce, not farming. Buddhism and Hinduism dominated.
LAND-BASED STATES
Khmer Empire (Cambodia) — built Angkor Wat, originally Hindu and later Buddhist. Sukhothai in Thailand. These states relied on rice agriculture with sophisticated irrigation and waterworks.

By c. 1400, Islam was spreading rapidly in maritime Southeast Asia through Muslim merchants from India and the Middle East — especially in port cities. Indonesia and Malaysia today are predominantly Muslim because of this peaceful, trade-driven diffusion.

Required Illustrative Examples (1.3): Vijayanagara Empire · Rajput kingdoms · Bhakti Movement · Sufism · Srivijaya Empire · Majapahit Kingdom · Khmer Empire (Angkor) · Sinhala Dynasty
Topic 1.4 — State Building in the Americas, c. 1200 – c. 1450

The Americas in 1200 were home to large, complex, urbanized civilizations — fundamentally connected to no other continent (no horses, no wheel-driven transport, no Old World diseases) yet politically and architecturally as sophisticated as anything in Eurasia. The CED requires you to know four:

MEXICA / AZTEC EMPIRE
Founded their capital Tenochtitlan on Lake Texcoco in 1325 and built an empire of tributary city-states — conquered peoples paid tribute (food, textiles, sacrificial captives) but kept their own rulers. Practiced ritual human sacrifice tied to their cosmology of feeding the sun. Theocratic — political and religious authority were one.
INCA EMPIRE
Founded c. 1438 in Cuzco (Peru); ruled the Sapa Inca, considered divine. Used a mit'a labor tax (mandatory rotational labor) to build roads and infrastructure across the Andes. Had no writing system but kept records on quipu (knotted strings). Their road system rivaled the Roman.
MISSISSIPPIAN CULTURE
A series of mound-building societies in the Mississippi River valley. Cahokia (near modern St. Louis) was the largest pre-Columbian city north of Mexico — c. 20,000 people at its peak. Maize-based agriculture supported large populations and a complex social hierarchy headed by paramount chiefs.
CHACO & MESA VERDE
In the American Southwest, the Ancestral Puebloans built large multi-story stone dwellings (kivas and cliff dwellings) connected by an extensive road network from Chaco Canyon. Decline by c. 1300 likely due to prolonged drought.
Required Illustrative Examples (1.4): Mexica/Aztec · Inca Empire · Mississippian culture · Chaco · Mesa Verde · mit'a · quipu · Tenochtitlan · Cahokia
AP Exam tip: The Inca mit'a system is one of the most-tested terms — it's a labor tax (not a financial one), required by the state for public works. Don't confuse it with the encomienda, which is a Spanish colonial system that came after this period.
Topic 1.5 — State Building in Africa, c. 1200 – c. 1450

Africa in this period was home to a remarkable variety of state structures, from massive empires that controlled gold-salt trade routes to compact, politically distinctive kingdoms. The CED groups these into a few key cases:

MALI EMPIRE (West Africa)
Successor to Ghana, peaked under Mansa Musa (r. 1312–1337). His pilgrimage to Mecca in 1324–1325 was so lavish that he allegedly destabilized the Egyptian gold market by giving away so much gold. Built Timbuktu into a major center of Islamic learning. Wealth came from controlling trans-Saharan gold and salt trade.
SONGHAI
Successor to Mali; rose c. 1450 to dominate West Africa into the 1500s. Centered on the city of Gao on the Niger River. Continued the trans-Saharan trade and Islamic scholarship of Mali.
HAUSA KINGDOMS
A loose federation of seven city-states in what is now northern Nigeria. Decentralized — no single emperor, but a shared linguistic and cultural identity, all Muslim, all integrated into trans-Saharan trade.
ETHIOPIA (Solomonic)
A Christian kingdom in the Horn of Africa, an outlier surrounded by Muslim states. Rulers traced descent from Solomon and Sheba. Famous for the rock-hewn churches at Lalibela (12th–13th century).
GREAT ZIMBABWE
Stone-walled trading capital in southeastern Africa (1100–1450), controlling the gold trade to the Indian Ocean coast. Its massive stone walls (built without mortar) gave the modern country its name.
SWAHILI CITY-STATES
A network of independent commercial city-states along the East African coast — Kilwa, Mombasa, Mogadishu, Sofala. Linked Africa to the Indian Ocean trade. Produced Swahili — a Bantu language with major Arabic loan-words — reflecting deep Afro-Arab cultural fusion.
Required Illustrative Examples (1.5): Mali Empire · Mansa Musa · Timbuktu · Hausa Kingdoms · Ethiopia (Solomonic) · Great Zimbabwe · Swahili city-states · Kilwa · Sundiata
AP Exam tip: The CED contrasts Mali (centralized, gold-based, Muslim) with the Hausa Kingdoms (decentralized, also Muslim) and with Ethiopia (Christian outlier). Knowing one African state isn't enough — the testable point is the diversity of African political and religious systems.
Topic 1.6 — Developments in Europe, c. 1200 – c. 1450

Europe in 1200 was the relative backwater of the connected world — far less wealthy or technologically advanced than China, Dar al-Islam, or India. But over this period, two crucial transformations laid the groundwork for Europe's later global role: the centralization of monarchical power and the recovery of classical learning.

Feudalism and manorialism were the political and economic systems of medieval Europe:

• Feudalism was a hierarchy of personal obligations: the king granted land (fiefs) to lords in exchange for military service and loyalty; lords sub-granted to lesser knights; everyone owed loyalty upward. Real political power was decentralized — the king of France in 1200 directly controlled only a tiny area around Paris.

• Manorialism was the economic system: serfs (legally bound to the land but not slaves) farmed a noble's manor in exchange for protection. Most Europeans were serfs.

The Roman Catholic Church was the most powerful single institution in Europe — wealthier than most kings, the only universal authority, the source of literacy and education through monasteries. The pope claimed authority over kings (a contested claim that produced centuries of conflict). The Crusades (1095–1291) and the earlier Reconquista in Spain were religious-military projects coordinated by the Church.

The trend toward centralization. Across the period, monarchs gradually increased their authority at the expense of the nobility:

• The Magna Carta (1215) — English nobles forced King John to accept written limits on royal power. Long-term: the seed of constitutional government.
• Kings raised standing royal armies paid in cash — making them no longer dependent on noble levies.
• The Hundred Years' War (1337–1453) between England and France strengthened royal authority in both kingdoms by forging national identities.
• Cities and towns grew, producing a wealthy bourgeoisie (merchants, craftsmen) — a new social class outside the lord-vassal-serf system.

The Black Death (1346–1353) killed an estimated 30–50% of Europe's population — and was a turning point. Massive labor shortages gave surviving peasants bargaining power against lords; serfdom began to dissolve in Western Europe. Survivors questioned the Church's authority (it couldn't stop the plague). And the resulting urban concentration of wealth helped fund the early Italian Renaissance.

The Renaissance began in northern Italian city-states (Florence, Venice, Milan) in the 14th century. Scholars rediscovered classical Greek and Roman texts (often via Arabic translations from al-Andalus and Sicily — see Topic 1.2) and developed humanism, the philosophical focus on human capability and earthly life rather than purely religious concerns. This is the seed of the early modern era.

Required Illustrative Examples (1.6): Feudalism · manorialism · serfdom · Roman Catholic Church · Crusades · Hundred Years' War · Magna Carta · Black Death · Italian Renaissance · humanism · Hanseatic League
Topic 1.7 — Comparison in the Period, c. 1200 – c. 1450

Topic 1.7 is a skills topic: it covers no new content. Instead, it asks you to compare across regions using the developments you've learned. The CED flags Argumentation (skill 6.A): Make a historically defensible claim as the focal skill.

The most-tested comparisons across Unit 1 are:

STATE LEGITIMIZATION
How rulers justified power. Compare: Mandate of Heaven (China) · divine right + Mansa pilgrimage (Mali) · divine descent (Inca Sapa Inca) · Christian coronation by the Pope (Europe) · Caliphate (Islamic world).
RELIGION & STATE
Where do they overlap? In all six regions, religion and political legitimacy were tightly fused — Confucianism in China, Islam in Dar al-Islam, Hinduism/Buddhism in Vijayanagara/Khmer, indigenous + state cults in the Americas, Christianity in Europe.
LABOR SYSTEMS
Compare: serfdom (Europe) · the mit'a (Inca) · slavery (across many) · free peasant labor (Song China) · tributary labor (Aztec).
CENTRALIZED vs DECENTRALIZED
Highly centralized: Song China, Mali, Inca. Decentralized: feudal Europe, Hausa Kingdoms, fragmented Dar al-Islam, Japan (after Heian). The pattern matters more than the single example.
AP Exam tip: A common LEQ asks you to "compare the methods of government and state-building used by two regions, c. 1200–1450." Have one strong example ready from three different regions — that's enough to write any LEQ on this period.
