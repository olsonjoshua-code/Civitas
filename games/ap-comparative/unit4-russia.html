AP COMPARATIVE GOVERNMENT · RUSSIA
Competitive Authoritarianism and Democratic Backsliding
Russia is the AP Comparative course's primary example of competitive authoritarianism and democratic backsliding from a promising post-Soviet starting point.
Regime Classification: Competitive Authoritarianism
Levitsky and Way's competitive authoritarianism fits Russia: elections held, opposition parties technically exist, constitution guarantees rights — but the incumbent uses state resources, media control, and selective violence to make genuine competition impossible. NOT totalitarianism (private life permitted) and NOT democracy (election outcomes predetermined). Key mechanisms: barring candidates (Navalny excluded from 2018 election), state media dominance, foreign agent law, administrative pressure on voters.
AP Exam tip: Russia's elections are not simply fake — they serve real purposes: legitimize the regime, mobilize supporters, gauge regional elite loyalty. The point is they cannot produce alternation of power against the incumbent's wishes.
Constitutional Structure: Superpresidentialism
Russia's 1993 Constitution was drafted after Yeltsin shelled parliament with tanks — creating a superpresidential system. The president issues decrees with force of law without legislative approval, determines all major policy, appoints the PM and all judges. The Medvedev interlude (2008-2012) — Putin as PM while Medvedev was nominal president — illustrates how term limits can be honored in letter while gutted in spirit.
Russia vs. China: Different Authoritarianisms
RUSSIA
Personalist (organized around Putin); competitive elections; weaker state capacity; residual independent civil society; succession crisis vulnerability.
CHINA
Institutionalized (CCP as institution); no competitive elections; stronger state capacity; more comprehensive civil society control; clearer succession mechanisms.

Key Institutions
👤
President
Head of State · Superpresidential
Directly elected. Issues decrees with force of law. Appoints PM and all federal judges. 2020 amendments reset Putin's terms; eligible until 2036.
🏛️
Prime Minister
Head of Government
Appointed by president, confirmed by Duma. Manages day-to-day government. Power is entirely derivative of presidential will. Currently Mikhail Mishustin.
📜
State Duma
Lower House · 450 seats
Half elected FPTP, half by PR (5% threshold). United Russia holds dominant majority. Known as a "rubber stamp" for Kremlin policy.
⚔️
United Russia
The Party of Power
Putin's dominant party founded 2001. Holds approximately 75% of Duma seats. No clear ideology. A "party of bureaucrats" joined for career advancement, not conviction.
📚
Federation Council
Upper House · 170 senators
Two members from each of Russia's 85 federal subjects. Approves use of armed forces abroad and federal judges. Now largely a presidential instrument.
⚖️
Constitutional Court
19 judges · Life tenure
Reviews laws for constitutionality. Judges nominated by president. Has not ruled against key executive interests. Functions as a legitimating, not limiting, institution.

Putin's Consolidation of Power
1991
Soviet collapse: shock therapy; oligarchs emerge from privatization
1993
Constitutional crisis: Yeltsin dissolves Duma by force; superpresidential constitution adopted
1999
Apartment bombings; Putin becomes PM; Second Chechen War; Yeltsin resigns, appoints Putin acting president
2004
Beslan school siege; Putin abolishes direct gubernatorial elections; appoints regional leaders
2008
Putin becomes PM (Medvedev as nominal president: term limit workaround); Georgia war
2014
Annexation of Crimea; Eastern Ukraine war; Western sanctions; approval rating surges to 85%
2020
Constitutional amendments reset Putin's terms (eligible until 2036); Navalny poisoned with Novichok
2022
Full-scale invasion of Ukraine; sweeping censorship laws; information blackout; mass emigration
2024
Navalny dies in prison; Putin wins election with 87% as opposition candidates are barred
