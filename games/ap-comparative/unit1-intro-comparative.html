AP COMPARATIVE GOVERNMENT · UNIT 1
Political Systems, Regimes, and Governments
This unit introduces the foundational concepts of comparative politics: how political scientists study governments, how regimes are classified, how states gain and lose legitimacy, and how power is structured across the six course countries. Everything here appears on the AP Exam.
1.1, The Practice of Political Scientists
The comparative method is the core approach of this course. Political scientists study two or more countries to identify what causes different political outcomes. Rather than focusing on a single nation, comparison allows us to test hypotheses, for example, does economic development lead to democracy? Does ethnic diversity cause instability?
AP Exam tip: Questions often ask you to apply comparative method logic, "which of the following is an example of using the comparative method?" The key is that it involves multiple countries and seeks to explain political outcomes.
1.2, Defining Political Organizations
Four concepts you must distinguish precisely:
STATE
A permanent political unit with four requirements: defined territory, a permanent population, an effective government, and sovereignty. States persist even when governments change.
NATION
A group sharing a common identity, ethnic, linguistic, cultural, or historical. Nations do not require a state. The Kurdish people are a nation without a state; the UK is a multinational state.
GOVERNMENT
The current set of officials holding power within a state. Governments change (elections, coups); states endure. The UK has had dozens of governments, it remains one state.
SOVEREIGNTY
Supreme authority, both internal (over territory and people) and external (independence from other states). Weber defined the state as the entity with a monopoly on the legitimate use of force.
AP Exam tip: "State vs. government" is a classic distinction question. A military coup changes the government, specifically the state continues. Nigeria has had many governments; it has been one state since 1960.
1.3, Democracy vs. Authoritarianism
A regime is the set of fundamental rules for how power is gained, exercised, and transferred. There are three broad types:
DEMOCRACY
Competitive elections, civil liberties, government accountability. The UK and Mexico (since 2000) are the course's democratic examples. Freedom House scores: UK 93, Mexico 60.
AUTHORITARIANISM
Restricts political competition and civil liberties. Power concentrated without meaningful electoral accountability. Russia (16), China (9), and Iran (14) all fall here.
HYBRID / COMPETITIVE AUTHORITARIAN
Elections exist but are not genuinely competitive. Iran and Russia both qualify. Nigeria (44) is "Partly Free", elections occur, but corruption and violence undermine quality. Totalitarianism is the extreme end: the state controls all aspects of life, including private behavior (Soviet Union under Stalin).
AP Exam tip: Know the difference between authoritarian and totalitarian. Authoritarian = restricts political life. Totalitarian = controls all aspects of life. Distinguish Iran (hybrid/theocratic) from China (one-party authoritarian) from Russia (competitive authoritarian).
1.4, Democratization & Democratic Backsliding
Democratization is the process of transitioning toward democracy. Key factors associated with successful democratization include economic development (a large middle class demanding rights, Lipset's Modernization Theory), a strong civil society, and prior democratic experience. Mexico's 2000 election is the course's model case: a gradual, electoral transition ending 71 years of PRI one-party rule when Vicente Fox (PAN) won the presidency peacefully.
Democratic backsliding is the reverse, specifically the gradual erosion of democratic institutions by elected leaders using legal means: weakening courts, restricting media, manipulating elections. Russia and Hungary are the classic examples. This is not a sudden coup; it happens from within.
AP Exam tip: "Democratic backsliding" and "competitive authoritarianism" often appear together. Russia's trajectory post-2000 is the go-to example: formally democratic institutions, systematically undermined from within.
1.5, Sources of Power and Authority (Weber)
Max Weber identified three pure types of legitimate authority. You must know all three and be able to apply them to the six countries:
TRADITIONAL
Authority from custom, heredity, and long-standing norms. Example: UK monarchy. The Crown's authority rests on centuries of tradition, even though real political power has shifted to Parliament.
CHARISMATIC
Authority from the extraordinary personal qualities of a leader. Example: Ayatollah Khomeini in Iran's 1979 revolution; early Putin; Mao in China. Inherently unstable, depends on one person.
RATIONAL-LEGAL
Authority from established laws, rules, and institutions. The dominant form in modern states. Example: UK Parliament, Mexico's constitution. Authority attaches to offices, not individuals.
The CCP's primary claim is performance legitimacy (output legitimacy), not one of Weber's three types, but equally important for the exam: the CCP justifies rule through delivering economic growth, not elections or tradition.
AP Exam tip: Weber questions appear frequently. Know which type fits each country. The UK monarchy = traditional. UK Parliament = rational-legal. Iran's Supreme Leader = theocratic/charismatic. CCP = performance legitimacy.
1.6, Change in Power and Authority
Power can change hands in two ways. Constitutional change follows the established rules: elections, votes of no confidence, constitutional amendments. Extra-constitutional change bypasses the rules: coups, revolutions, forced resignations under duress.
Key cases: Mexico's 2000 election = constitutional. Nigeria's multiple military coups (1966, 1983, 1993) = extra-constitutional. Russia's 2020 constitutional amendments = formally constitutional but used to consolidate power, illustrating how legal mechanisms can be weaponized.
AP Exam tip: A UK vote of no confidence = constitutional (it's built into the Westminster system). A military coup = extra-constitutional. Russia's 2020 amendments = constitutional in form, authoritarian in effect.
1.7, Federal and Unitary Systems
FEDERAL SYSTEMS
Power divided constitutionally between central and subnational governments. Subnational powers are entrenched, specifically the center cannot simply abolish them. Examples: Mexico, Nigeria, Russia (formally).
UNITARY SYSTEMS
All sovereign power held by the center. Subnational authority is delegated and can be reclaimed. Examples: UK, China, Iran.
Two critical distinctions: UK devolution grants powers to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, but Parliament remains sovereign and could theoretically reclaim them through ordinary legislation. This makes the UK unitary, not federal. Russia is formally federal with 89 subjects, but Putin recentralized power by abolishing direct gubernatorial elections and installing presidential appointees, making Russian federalism a facade.
AP Exam tip: The UK devolution vs. Mexican federalism comparison is a classic. The key word is "parliamentary sovereignty", because Parliament can reclaim devolved powers, the UK is unitary. Mexican states have constitutionally guaranteed powers the federal government cannot simply revoke.
1.8–1.9, Political Legitimacy and How Regimes Sustain It
Legitimacy is the belief among citizens that the government has the right to rule, not just the power to do so. A government can be powerful but illegitimate (a new military junta). It can be legitimate but weak. There are two main types:
PROCEDURAL LEGITIMACY
Based on following the accepted rules, especially free and fair elections. UK and Mexico. When a government refuses to transfer power after an election, it destroys procedural legitimacy.
PERFORMANCE LEGITIMACY
Based on delivering results, economic growth, stability, security. China's CCP is the exam's primary example. Its claim: we have lifted 800 million from poverty; we deserve to rule.
Authoritarian regimes sustain legitimacy through: nationalist appeals (Russia: great power status), co-optation (rewarding elites with access and resources), controlled elections (Iran, Russia, simulate consent without genuine competition), and repression (when other tools fail). Iran's Guardian Council vets candidates to ensure only regime-acceptable figures can win, preserving the appearance of democracy while controlling outcomes.
AP Exam tip: "Legitimacy" questions are among the most common in this unit. Be ready to distinguish procedural from performance legitimacy, and to explain how specific countries claim each. CCP = performance. UK = procedural. Iran = a mix of theocratic and controlled procedural.
1.10, Political Stability
Political stability is a state's ability to maintain order, transfer power peacefully, and govern effectively over time. Huntington's key argument: what matters most is not whether a regime is democratic or authoritarian, but whether it has effective institutions. A weak democracy (Nigeria) can be less stable than a strong authoritarian state (China).
Factors driving instability in Nigeria: ethnic cleavages (Hausa-Fulani, Yoruba, Igbo), Christian-Muslim religious divisions, oil-dependent economy creating corruption, weak courts and security forces. Factors underpinning UK stability: centuries of institutional precedent, strong rule of law, professional civil service, embedded democratic norms, all without a codified constitution.
The Six Course Countries, Quick Reference
United Kingdom
PARLIAMENTARY DEMOCRACY · UNITARY · FREEDOM HOUSE: 93
Constitutional monarchy with an uncodified constitution. Prime Minister holds executive power through confidence of Parliament (Westminster model). Devolution to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, but Parliament remains sovereign. Stability rooted in centuries of convention and strong rule of law. Weber authority type: rational-legal (Parliament) + traditional (monarchy).
Mexico
PRESIDENTIAL DEMOCRACY · FEDERAL · FREEDOM HOUSE: 60
Transitioned to democracy in 2000 when Vicente Fox (PAN) defeated the PRI after 71 years of one-party rule. Federal system with 31 states and Mexico City. The PRI maintained power through patron-client networks, co-optation, electoral fraud, and media control, a classic soft authoritarian model. Freedom House score has declined (66 to 60) due to cartel violence and concerns about democratic backsliding.
Russia
COMPETITIVE AUTHORITARIAN · FEDERAL (FACADE) · FREEDOM HOUSE: 16
A "superpresidential" system where formal institutions exist but real power is concentrated in the executive. Formally federal with 89 subjects, but Putin recentralized by abolishing gubernatorial elections and installing loyalists. Elections are held but state media control and opposition harassment make genuine competition impossible, specifically the definition of competitive authoritarianism. 2020 constitutional amendments reset term limits through a formally legal process.
China
ONE-PARTY AUTHORITARIAN · UNITARY · FREEDOM HOUSE: 9
The Chinese Communist Party (CCP) holds all political power, there is no independent judiciary, free press, or competitive elections. Despite having "autonomous regions" (Tibet, Xinjiang), China is a unitary state with all real decisions made in Beijing. The CCP's primary legitimacy claim is performance-based: four decades of ~9% annual GDP growth lifted 800 million from poverty. This output legitimacy is the CCP's core justification for one-party rule.
Iran
THEOCRATIC HYBRID · UNITARY · FREEDOM HOUSE: 14
A dual state: elected institutions (president, Majles/parliament) coexist with unelected clerical ones (Supreme Leader, Guardian Council). The Supreme Leader (currently Khamenei) holds ultimate authority, unelected and unchallengeable. The Guardian Council vets all candidates, ensuring only regime-acceptable figures can compete. This creates controlled pluralism: elections happen, but genuine opposition is filtered out. Velayat-e Faqih (Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist) is the constitutional basis for clerical rule.
Nigeria
PRESIDENTIAL DEMOCRACY (PARTLY FREE) · FEDERAL · FREEDOM HOUSE: 44
Africa's most populous country and largest economy, yet consistently "Partly Free." Has multiparty elections and a federal system with 36 states, but endemic corruption, ethnic divisions (Hausa-Fulani/Yoruba/Igbo), Christian-Muslim cleavages, Boko Haram insurgency, and an oil-dependent economy undermine governance. Multiple military coups between independence (1960) and the return to democracy (1999). Huntington's framework applies directly: the problem is not Nigeria's regime type, but its weak state capacity.
Key Thinkers for the Exam
Max Weber
POLITICS AS A VOCATION, 1919
Defined the state as having a monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force. Identified three types of authority: traditional, charismatic, and rational-legal. Foundational for any question about legitimacy or authority.
Samuel Huntington
POLITICAL ORDER IN CHANGING SOCIETIES, 1968
Argued that effective government capacity matters more to stability than regime type. A strong authoritarian state can be more stable than a weak democracy. Nigeria illustrates his point; the UK challenges it by combining democracy with high capacity.
Seymour Martin Lipset
MODERNIZATION THEORY
Argued that economic development produces a middle class that demands political rights, making democratization more likely. Mexico's 2000 transition and China's continued authoritarianism despite growth both relate to this theory.
Steven Levitsky & Lucan Way
COMPETITIVE AUTHORITARIANISM
Defined "competitive authoritarian" regimes, systems that hold elections but systematically undermine their fairness. Russia is the course's primary example. The concept explains how authoritarian rule can persist within formally democratic institutions.
What to Expect on the AP Exam
MCQ: Expect questions comparing two countries on a concept (e.g., "which country best illustrates performance legitimacy?"), applying Weber's authority types, and distinguishing federal from unitary systems. Always read carefully, "formal structure" vs. "actual practice" is a common trap (Russia).
Conceptual Analysis (Q1): Define a term precisely, then apply it to a country with a specific mechanism. Vague answers earn 0. "The CCP maintains legitimacy through economic growth" is good; "they control the country" is not.
Comparative Analysis (Q3): Must identify a similarity AND a difference. Do not just describe each country separately, you must explicitly compare. "Both China and Russia claim performance legitimacy, but China's is based on economic growth while Russia's relies more on nationalist appeals."
Argument Essay (Q4): Make a defensible claim, support with evidence from at least two countries, explain your reasoning, and address the counterargument. The claim must go beyond restating the prompt, it needs to take a specific, arguable position.
