Sources of Political Legitimacy — Weber's Three Types
Why Do Citizens Accept Government Authority?
Weber's three types of authority appear in different combinations across the six countries. Most regimes blend multiple types — specifying which type applies is the difference between a strong and weak FRQ answer.
RATIONAL-LEGAL
Authority from law and procedure — the office, not the person. UK: parliamentary sovereignty, rule of law, courts can rule against executive (Miller 2019). Mexico: constitutional framework, INEC independent elections. Nigeria: constitutional separation of powers (imperfectly enforced). Strongest in consolidated democracies
TRADITIONAL
Authority from custom and inheritance. UK: monarchy as residual traditional authority — politically symbolic, no governing power. Nigeria: traditional emirate and chieftaincy structures maintain local legitimacy alongside formal constitutional government
CHARISMATIC
Authority from personal appeal. Russia: Putin's nationalist-charismatic legitimacy; the regime is organized around the person, not the institution. Iran: Khomeini's charismatic founding authority institutionalized through velayat-e faqih — charisma routinized into theocratic rule
PERFORMANCE
(Weber's fourth, added by modern scholars) Authority from results — economic growth, stability, public goods. China: CCP's primary legitimacy claim — poverty reduction, GDP growth (~9% annually 1980-2020), infrastructure. Contingent: erodes when performance declines. Russia: secondary pillar alongside nationalism (oil boom 2000-2014 generated genuine popularity)
📌 AP EXAM TIP
"The CCP has legitimacy" = 0 pts. "The CCP relies primarily on performance legitimacy — its right to rule is justified by economic growth and poverty reduction, not elections or constitutional procedure" = 1 pt. Always specify the TYPE and explain the mechanism. Theocratic legitimacy (Iran) is a fifth type: authority derived from divine mandate or religious law.

📌 AP EXAM TIP — HYBRID REGIMES
Russia and Iran are both hybrid — but different hybrids. Russia manipulates elections to prevent genuine competition (competitive authoritarianism). Iran filters candidates BEFORE elections via the Guardian Council (theocratic hybrid). Both hold elections; neither produces genuine alternation of power. On FRQs, always explain the specific mechanism, not just "the elections aren't fair."
Executive Systems Compared
UK
PARLIAMENTARY
PM drawn from and accountable to Parliament — fusion of powers. Vote of no confidence can remove government (1979 Callaghan, 2022 Johnson resigned under pressure). PM commands Commons majority but can be checked by both Commons and Lords. No fixed terms — elections called when PM chooses or confidence lost
MEX/NGA
PRESIDENTIAL
Directly elected executive; separation of powers; fixed terms. Mexico: 6-year sexenio, no reelection (no reelección). Nigeria: 4-year terms, 2-term max, 25%/24 states required. Both have genuine legislative branches that can act independently of executive (Mexico Congress passed bills over AMLO opposition in 1997-2000)
RUSSIA
SUPERPRES.
Formally semi-presidential (president + PM); actually superpresidential. President issues decrees with force of law, appoints/dismisses PM, appoints all judges. Medvedev interlude (2008-12): term limits honored in letter, gutted in spirit. 2020 amendments reset terms — eligible until 2036
IRAN
DUAL EXEC.
Supreme Leader (unelected, permanent, divine authority) + President (elected, limited). Supreme Leader commands military, appoints judiciary, controls media, can dismiss president. Elected president handles domestic/foreign policy but cannot challenge theocratic framework
CHINA
PARTY-STATE
CCP General Secretary holds supreme power; state institutions (NPC, Premier) are secondary. NPC (3,000 delegates, 2-week session) has never rejected a CCP proposal — rubber stamp. Xi removed 2-term limit through NPC vote (2018). No separation of party and state
📌 AP EXAM TIP — FORMAL VS. ACTUAL
The AP exam frequently tests the gap between formal constitutional structure and actual political practice. Russia is formally semi-presidential but actually superpresidential. China formally has separation of NPC/State Council/CCP but actually has party-state fusion. Iran formally has an elected president but actually has Supreme Leader supremacy. Always distinguish formal and actual on FRQs.
Common AP Exam Mistakes — Power & Democracy
⚠️
Saying a country "has legitimacy" or "lacks legitimacy" without specifying the type. Every AP Comparative country claims legitimacy through some combination of types. Always name which type and explain why it does or doesn't function.
⚠️
Conflating regime type and government type. Regime = the rules of the political game (democracy, authoritarianism). Government = the specific people currently in power (Starmer's government, Xi's administration). The regime can outlast many governments.
⚠️
Calling Russia's system "semi-presidential" as if that explains its power structure. Russia is formally semi-presidential but actually superpresidential. The formal classification matters less than the actual concentration of power in the presidency.
⚠️
Treating "hybrid regime" as a single category. Russia (competitive authoritarian), Iran (theocratic hybrid), and Nigeria (fragile democracy) are all "hybrid" in different ways with different mechanisms. On FRQs, always specify the type of hybrid and the specific institutional feature that makes it hybrid.
FRQ Connections — Power & Democracy
✍️
Conceptual Analysis: Define competitive authoritarianism — requires BOTH dimensions (elections held + cannot produce alternation). Define hybrid regime — must specify both the democratic element AND the authoritarian constraint that limits it. Always apply to specific countries with specific mechanisms.
✍️
Quantitative Analysis: Electoral data — disproportionality (votes vs. seats) is a standard question. Know UK FPTP bonus (Labour 34%→63%), Russia's manipulation on top of FPTP (49%→75%), and Iran's turnout collapse (79%→40%) after candidate filtering. The China absence from the table is itself significant data.
✍️
Comparative Analysis: Executive accountability — UK (PMQs, vote of no confidence, independent courts) vs. Russia (no genuine accountability mechanisms despite formal constitution). The key contrast: UK's formal mechanisms function because political actors accept them; Russia's formal mechanisms exist but are not enforced.
✍️
Argument Essay: Is democracy the only legitimate form of government? China (performance legitimacy, 75-year stability) vs. UK (democratic legitimacy surviving crises). The key argument: performance legitimacy is contingent (erodes when growth slows) while democratic legitimacy survives poor performance through accountability mechanisms.
