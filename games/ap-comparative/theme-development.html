AP COMPARATIVE GOVERNMENT · THEMATIC UNIT
Development, Inequality, and Economic Policy
This thematic unit compares economic development strategies, inequality, and the relationship between development and regime type across all six AP Comparative countries.
Development and Regime Type
Modernization theory (Lipset) predicts economic development leads to democratization — a growing middle class demands rights. China is the most important challenge: 40 years of approximately 9% growth with no democratization. The CCP has managed this by co-opting business elites, using nationalism as a substitute, and repressing civil society before it builds democratic capacity. Taiwan (Chinese culture, democratized in the 1990s as it developed) is the crucial counterevidence showing Chinese culture is not incompatible with democracy.
AP Exam tip: When asked about the development-democracy link, use the China-Mexico comparison: Mexico's economic development (NAFTA, middle class growth) contributed to democratization in 2000. China's development did not. The difference: China had the institutional capacity to co-opt the middle class; Mexico's PRI lost control as the economy opened.
The Resource Curse
Nigeria and Iran illustrate the resource curse: oil wealth enables governments to fund patronage without taxing citizens (reducing accountability), causes Dutch disease (currency appreciation killing other sectors), and creates zero-sum competition for resource rents along ethnic/factional lines. The "no taxation without representation" logic runs in reverse — states that don't need taxes face less democratic pressure. Norway (not an AP Comparative country) shows the resource curse is not inevitable if pre-existing democratic institutions are strong.
Development Strategies Compared
STATE-LED (China)
SEZs, industrial policy, state-owned enterprises, infrastructure investment. Rapid growth but without political liberalization.
MARKET-LED (UK, Mexico post-NAFTA)
Privatization, trade liberalization, foreign investment. Thatcher's revolution; Mexico's NAFTA integration. Creates growth but can increase inequality.
RESOURCE-DEPENDENT (Nigeria, Iran)
Oil revenues dominate; other sectors underdeveloped. Vulnerability to price shocks; corruption; patronage distribution without accountability.
POPULIST (Mexico 4T, Russia energy nationalism)
State control of strategic resources (Pemex); social transfers; skepticism of foreign investment. Political mobilization around economic nationalism.
