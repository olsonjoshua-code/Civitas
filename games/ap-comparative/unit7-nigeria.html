Nigeria — Democratic Consolidation, Social Cleavages & the Resource Curse
Nigeria is the AP Comparative course's primary example of a developing democracy managing extreme social cleavages — ethnic, religious, and regional divisions that reinforce each other — while building democratic institutions from a colonial legacy and 33 years of military rule. It is also the course's clearest resource curse case study.
🏛️ Federal Presidential Republic
🗺️ Federal (36 states + FCT)
📋 Constitution: 1999
🛢️ Oil: ~90% of exports, ~80% of govt revenue
The Core Challenge: Reinforcing Cleavages
250+ Ethnic Groups, Three Dominant — and They All Overlap
Nigeria's 250+ ethnic groups and three dominant ones — Hausa-Fulani (Muslim, northern), Yoruba (mixed Christian/Muslim, southwest), and Igbo (Christian, southeast) — create reinforcing cleavages: ethnicity, religion, and region align and compound each other. When your ethnic group loses the presidency, you also feel your religion and your region have lost. Political competition becomes zero-sum existential conflict rather than policy disagreement.
REINFORCING (Nigeria)
Ethnicity + religion + region align: being Hausa-Fulani means being Muslim AND northern. Political losses feel total — your entire identity group has lost. Zero-sum competition, ethnic outbidding
CROSS-CUTTING (contrast)
Divisions that don't align: a person's ethnicity and class pull in different directions, creating cross-pressures. Moderates conflict — you share interests with outgroup members on some dimensions
🔑 BIAFRAN CIVIL WAR (1967–1970)
After the 1966 coup and anti-Igbo pogroms, the southeast seceded as "Biafra." The federal government reconquered it — 1–3 million dead, many from deliberately induced famine. This remains the ultimate warning of what reinforcing cleavages can produce when constitutional management fails. Every Nigerian politician understands the Biafran precedent.
📌 AP EXAM TIP
Always distinguish reinforcing vs. cross-cutting cleavages using Nigeria as the reinforcing example. On FRQs: explain WHY reinforcing cleavages threaten stability — the mechanism is zero-sum competition (political losses feel total when ethnicity = religion = region all lose simultaneously) and ethnic outbidding (politicians compete to be most extreme ethnic champion).
Constitutional Mechanisms for Managing Cleavages
Institutional Engineering for Accommodation
Nigeria has built constitutional responses to its cleavages — imperfect but meaningful institutional designs to force accommodation across ethnic and regional lines.
25% IN 24
Presidential electoral requirement: To win the presidency, a candidate must win a plurality AND receive at least 25% of votes in at least 24 of 36 states. This forces cross-ethnic coalition building — no candidate can win by mobilizing only one ethnic group. One of the most distinctive electoral rules in comparative politics
FED. CHAR.
Federal character principle: Government appointments must reflect the federal character (geographic diversity) of Nigeria. Prevents any single ethnic group from monopolizing the federal bureaucracy. Creates ethnic and regional quotas in cabinet and civil service positions
36 STATES
36-state federal structure: Breaking the country into 36 states gave minority groups their own political units — reducing the dominance of the three major ethnic blocs. States have control over education, local governance, and patronage resources
ZONING
Zoning/rotation norm: Informal convention alternating the presidency between north and south. Not constitutional but politically powerful — breaking it caused controversy when Jonathan (south) ran after completing a northern president's term. Distributes power across regions over time
📌 AP EXAM TIP
The 25%/24 states rule is Nigeria's most distinctive and most tested constitutional mechanism. On FRQs, always explain WHY it manages cleavages — it prevents ethnic bloc voting from being sufficient, forcing candidates to appeal beyond their own group. This is the direct institutional response to zero-sum ethnic competition.
The Resource Curse & Governance Failures
Oil, Corruption, and the "National Cake"
Nigeria is Africa's largest oil producer, yet it remains one of the world's most impoverished large countries. Oil accounts for ~90% of export revenue and ~80% of government revenue — but this wealth has not translated into broad development. The resource curse operates through three interlocking mechanisms:
›
Corruption/patronage: Politicians distribute oil rents to ethnic clients rather than investing in public goods. "National cake" politics — controlling the state means controlling billions in oil revenue, making politics existentially high-stakes. Transparency International CPI: 24/100 (compared to Mexico 31, UK 73)
›
Reduced accountability: Government funded by oil, not taxes — so citizens have less leverage. "No taxation without representation" logic inverted: no taxation means no democratic pressure for accountability. HDI: 0.548 vs. Mexico 0.781 despite comparable oil sectors
›
Niger Delta paradox: The region producing most of Nigeria's oil bears the environmental cost (pollution, gas flaring) while receiving relatively little benefit — generating armed militancy (MEND), oil bunkering, and ongoing violence that disrupts production
›
Prebendalism: Richard Joseph's concept — public office treated as a "prebend," a source of income and benefits to be shared with co-ethnics. Officeholders expected by their communities to deliver resources. Makes anti-corruption efforts personally costly for politicians
Democratic Progress & Ongoing Challenges
Nigeria's Fourth Republic (1999–present) is its longest period of civilian rule — 25+ years without a military coup. But democratic consolidation remains incomplete.
2015 ★
Landmark democratic moment: Buhari (APC) defeated incumbent Jonathan (PDP) — first time an opposition candidate defeated a sitting president in Nigerian history. Jonathan conceded before results were finalized, praised internationally as a sign of democratic maturity. The INEC (Independent National Electoral Commission) performed credibly
BOKO H.
Boko Haram insurgency (2009–present) in the northeast — at peak controlled territory the size of Belgium, killed 20,000+, displaced 2 million+. Tests Weber's definition of state sovereignty (monopoly of legitimate force). Chibok schoolgirl abductions (2014) drew international attention. Now reduced but not eliminated
ENDSARS
2020 #EndSARS protests — youth-led, social media coordinated movement against police brutality (Special Anti-Robbery Squad). Largest protests in a generation. Lekki tollgate massacre showed state willingness to use lethal force. Showed growing civil society capacity but also limits of civilian oversight of security forces
SHARIA
12 northern states adopted full Sharia law after 1999 return to civilian rule — including criminal codes. Constitutionally contested (federal constitution guarantees freedom of religion) but tolerated as political accommodation of northern Muslim identity. Illustrates how federalism can manage religious-ethnic cleavages through local accommodation
Common AP Exam Mistakes — Nigeria
⚠️
Confusing reinforcing and cross-cutting cleavages. Nigeria = reinforcing (ethnicity + religion + region align). Cross-cutting cleavages MODERATE conflict because they create cross-pressures. This distinction is tested constantly — know the definition and the Nigeria example cold.
⚠️
Forgetting the 25%/24 states electoral rule. This is Nigeria's most distinctive constitutional mechanism and one of the most commonly tested. Always explain it as forcing cross-ethnic coalition building, not just as a threshold requirement.
⚠️
Saying Nigeria "failed to democratize." Nigeria has had competitive multiparty elections since 1999, a landmark opposition victory in 2015, and 25+ years without a military coup. It is an incomplete consolidation, not a failed democracy — language precision matters on FRQs.
⚠️
Vague resource curse descriptions. "Oil causes corruption" = 0 pts. "Oil revenues fund patronage networks without requiring taxation, eliminating the fiscal pressure for democratic accountability that historically drove state-building" = 1 pt. Always explain the mechanism.
Exam Strategies — Nigeria Questions
🎯
Nigeria as the cleavage model: For any question about social cleavages, ethnic diversity, or democratic challenges — Nigeria is your primary example. Its reinforcing cleavage structure is the textbook case of why identity politics threatens stability.
🎯
Nigeria vs. Mexico comparison: Both federal presidential democracies in transition. Key contrasts: Nigeria has more severe reinforcing cleavages; Mexico has a stronger track record of electoral credibility (IFE/INE); Nigeria's resource curse is more severe (90% oil vs. Mexico's diversified economy); Mexico has had more peaceful transfers. Use these contrasts for comparative FRQs.
🎯
Quantitative Analysis: Nigeria's data — oil dependency (30%→80%), CPI (24), HDI (0.548) — is your resource curse evidence. Know the comparison: Mexico 31 CPI / 0.781 HDI shows that diversified economy + stronger institutions = better development outcomes despite similar presidential federal structure.
🎯
Argument Essay anchor: For ethnic diversity and democratic stability, Nigeria is your evidence that reinforcing cleavages do threaten stability — but the 25%/24 states rule shows that institutional design can partially counteract this. Use Nigeria + UK (cross-cutting cleavages as contrast) for the strongest essay structure.
FRQ Connections — Nigeria Topics Most Likely to Appear
✍️
Conceptual Analysis: Explain reinforcing vs. cross-cutting cleavages using Nigeria — requires naming the three ethnic groups with their corresponding religious and regional identities AND explaining the zero-sum mechanism that makes reinforcing cleavages destabilizing.
✍️
Quantitative Analysis: Oil dependency data (30%→80%) + CPI (24) correlation. Always explain the mechanism: high oil dependency → government funded without taxation → reduced accountability pressure → high corruption. The data is evidence for the resource curse argument.
✍️
Comparative Analysis: Nigeria vs. Mexico on democratic consolidation. Both federal presidential democracies in transition from authoritarianism — but Mexico has stronger electoral institutions (IFE/INE), more peaceful transfers (6 since 2000 vs. 2 since 2015), and a more diversified economy reducing the resource curse.
✍️
Argument Essay: Ethnic diversity and democratic stability — Nigeria is evidence that reinforcing cleavages do threaten stability, but institutional design (25%/24 states rule) can mitigate this. Contrast with a cross-cutting cleavage case for the strongest argument about why cleavage structure matters more than diversity per se.

Key Institutions
🏛️
President
Head of State & Government
Directly elected — must win both a national plurality AND at least 25% in 24 of 36 states. Two 4-year terms maximum. Currently Bola Tinubu (APC, 2023). Commands armed forces. Appoints cabinet with Senate confirmation.
📜
National Assembly
Bicameral · Senate + HoR
Senate: 109 senators (3 per state + 1 FCT). House of Representatives: 360 members. Both approve budget. Can override presidential vetoes with 2/3 majority. Historically rubber-stamped executive but gaining independence.
⚖️
Supreme Court
Judicial Review
Highest court with judicial review power. Increasingly active: ruled against states imposing sharia on non-Muslims; adjudicates electoral disputes. Improving independence since 1999 but faces corruption concerns.
🗺️
36 States + FCT
Federal Structure
36 states with elected governors and assemblies. Revenue allocation distributes federal oil revenues. 12 northern states adopted sharia criminal law after 1999. Real decentralization since civilian rule began.
🛢
Oil Sector (NNPC)
Economic Foundation
Oil = approximately 90% of export revenue. Niger Delta produces most oil but receives relatively little benefit. Corruption and the "resource curse" mean oil wealth has not translated into broad development.
🗳️
INEC
Independent National Electoral Commission
Manages all federal and state elections. Credibility improved since 1999 — 2015 and 2023 transfers of power internationally recognized. But electoral violence and vote-buying remain persistent challenges.
