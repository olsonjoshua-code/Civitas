AP COMPARATIVE GOVERNMENT · THEMATIC UNIT
Citizens, Society, and the State
This thematic unit compares how citizens organize, what divides them, and how states respond to civil society across all six AP Comparative countries. Questions require you to compare multiple countries simultaneously.
Civil Society Across Regime Types
Civil society — the independent organizations between the state and the individual — is structurally stronger in democracies because democratic regimes need it to function (accountability, information, political organization), and are constitutionally prevented from destroying it. Authoritarian regimes see independent civil society as a threat to their monopoly on political organization and systematically suppress it.
STRONG (UK, Mexico)
Independent media, free unions, NGOs, competitive parties. Civil society holds government accountable.
CONSTRAINED (Iran, Nigeria)
Exists but limited by Basij enforcement (Iran), patronage co-optation (Nigeria), journalist murders.
SEVERELY RESTRICTED (Russia, China)
Foreign agent law (Russia); GONGOs and Great Firewall (China). State crowds out genuine civil society.
AP Exam tip: The Civil Society FRQ often asks you to compare HOW two countries limit civil society. Key contrast: Russia uses legal harassment (foreign agent law, registration burdens) to gradually close space; China uses proactive GONGO creation to replace civil society with state-controlled substitutes. Different methods, same goal.
Pluralism vs. Corporatism
Pluralism (UK, post-2000 Mexico): multiple independent groups compete freely for influence. No state monopoly on representation. Corporatism (PRI-era Mexico): state organizes peak associations (CTM for labor, CNC for peasants) that participate in policymaking in exchange for political loyalty. The shift from corporatism to pluralism is a key measure of Mexico's democratic transition — independent unions, business groups, and civil society organizations emerged as the PRI lost its monopoly.
Social Cleavages: Reinforcing vs. Cross-Cutting
Reinforcing cleavages (Nigeria): ethnicity, religion, and region all align — being Hausa-Fulani means being Muslim and northern. Makes political competition zero-sum. Cross-cutting cleavages moderate conflict by creating cross-pressures. The UK's Brexit cleavage cut across traditional class and party lines (educated Remainers existed in both Labour and Conservative parties) — a cross-cutting cleavage that created new political alignments without ethnic polarization. China suppresses cleavage expression through censorship; Russia channels it through nationalism.
