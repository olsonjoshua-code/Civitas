AP COMPARATIVE GOVERNMENT · CHINA
One-Party Authoritarian State and Performance Legitimacy
China is the AP Comparative course's primary example of a one-party authoritarian state, performance-based legitimacy, and the most important challenge to modernization theory's prediction that economic development leads to democratization.
Regime Type and Legitimacy
The CCP holds a constitutionally guaranteed “leading role.” There are no competitive national elections, no independent judiciary, and no free press. The CCP's primary legitimacy claim is performance legitimacy: justified by delivering economic growth and stability, not by elections. Lifting 800+ million from poverty and building the world's second-largest economy are the core arguments.
AP Exam tip: Performance legitimacy vs. procedural legitimacy is the key China concept. CCP legitimacy is output-based (what it delivers), not input-based (how leaders are chosen). This also means: if growth slows, what replaces it?
Deng's Reform and Opening
Deng's 1978 “Reform and Opening” broke Maoist rigidity: “seek truth from facts” — markets and planning are both tools, not ideological commitments. This created “Socialism with Chinese Characteristics.” Deng also institutionalized collective leadership: mandatory retirement ages, term limits, factional balance in the PSC — designed to prevent another Cultural Revolution. Xi Jinping's abolition of term limits (2018) and PSC dominance by loyalists represents a significant departure.
Tiananmen and the CCP's Red Line
The 1989 Tiananmen crackdown was driven by a Leninist logic of existential threat: the leadership saw democratic demands as opposition to one-party rule itself. CCP internal documents (the “Tiananmen Papers”) reveal this reasoning: “The students have made democracy their flag, knowing that no one can oppose democracy... We cannot give in.” This illustrates how authoritarian regimes calculate the suppression threshold: demands challenging the fundamental political order trigger maximum response.
China and Modernization Theory
Despite 40 years of approximately 9% annual growth, China has NOT democratized. The CCP manages the modernization-democracy link by: generating growth through state-led capitalism (no independent bourgeoisie); co-opting business elites rather than allowing independent associations; using nationalism as non-democratic legitimacy; and repressing civil society before it builds democratic capacity. Taiwan counterevidence: Taiwan (Chinese culture, Confucian tradition) democratized successfully in the 1990s and is rated Free (94/100) — directly challenging the claim that Chinese culture is incompatible with democracy.

CCP Power Structure
⭐
Politburo Standing Committee
7 members · Supreme decision-making
Innermost circle of CCP power. 7 members make all major national decisions. Xi Jinping chairs as General Secretary. Since 2022, all 7 are Xi loyalists.
🔴
Politburo
~25 members · Policy oversight
Second tier of leadership. Members oversee major policy domains. Meets monthly. Key battleground for elite competition. Most members aspire to the Standing Committee.
🏛️
National People's Congress
~3,000 delegates · "Rubber stamp"
Formally China's legislature. Meets annually for about 2 weeks. Endorses rather than debates party decisions. Has never rejected a government bill.
⚙️
State Council
Premier + Cabinet · Implementation
Chief executive body implementing party decisions. Premier Li Qiang manages day-to-day governance. Under Xi, power has shifted from State Council to Politburo Standing Committee.
🗡️
Central Military Commission
Commanded by Xi Jinping
Controls the People's Liberation Army. Xi chairs the CMC as General Secretary AND President. "The party commands the gun" — military loyalty is to the party, not the state.
📋
Central Committee
~200 members · Plenary sessions
Represents the broader party elite. Formally selects the Politburo, but in practice this is predetermined through elite bargaining. Third Plenums on economic policy are especially significant.

China's Modern Political History
1949
Mao Zedong proclaims People's Republic of China: "China has stood up"
1958-62
Great Leap Forward: collectivization causes famine; estimated 15-55 million deaths
1978
Deng Xiaoping's "Reform and Opening": SEZs, market liberalization, collective leadership norms
1989
Tiananmen Square: military crackdown kills hundreds to thousands; June 4 permanently censored
1997
Hong Kong returned from UK: "one country, two systems" promised until 2047
2012
Xi Jinping becomes General Secretary; anti-corruption campaign begins
2018
Presidential term limits abolished; Xi can serve indefinitely
2019-20
Hong Kong protests suppressed; National Security Law imposed ending "one country, two systems"
2022
20th Party Congress: Xi gets unprecedented third term; zero-COVID policy ends
