Mexico — Democratic Transition & Incomplete Consolidation
Mexico is the AP Comparative course's primary example of democratic transition through electoral means and an ongoing case study in incomplete consolidation. It shows how a hegemonic party system can be dismantled peacefully — and how new democracies can backslide even without a coup.
🏛️ Federal Presidential Republic
🗺️ Federal System (31 states + CDMX)
📋 Constitution: 1917
🗳️ Mixed Electoral System (FPTP + PR)
The PRI System: Hegemonic Party Rule (1929–2000)
Soft Authoritarianism Through Electoral Façade
The PRI (Institutional Revolutionary Party) ruled Mexico for 71 years — not through military force but through a sophisticated hegemonic party system: elections were held, opposition parties existed, but presidential outcomes were never genuinely in doubt. The PRI controlled society through three interlocking tools: corporatism, patron-client networks, and controlled succession.
›
Corporatism: State organized major groups into PRI-affiliated peak associations — CTM (labor), CNC (peasants), CNOP (popular sectors). These delivered bloc votes in exchange for state recognition, jobs, and resources. No independent organizing permitted outside PRI structures
›
Patron-client networks (clientelism): Material goods — jobs, housing, permits, infrastructure — exchanged for political loyalty down the hierarchy. Benefits as favors contingent on PRI support, not as rights
›
Dedazo: The "fingerpoke" — the outgoing president personally selected his successor, maintaining elite continuity while preserving the form of competitive elections. No internal party democracy
›
No reelección / Sexenio: One 6-year presidential term, no reelection. The PRI was the constant; individual presidents were temporary. This prevented personal dictatorship while the party remained dominant
📌 AP EXAM TIP
PRI hegemony ≠ one-party state. Opposition parties existed and occasionally won locally. It is a "hegemonic party system" — softer authoritarianism than China. On MCQs: "Which best describes Mexico under the PRI?" → hegemonic party system, not one-party state or democracy.
The Democratic Transition (1977–2000)
Gradual Electoral Reform as the Path to Democracy
Mexico democratized through the electoral route — gradual institutional reform over decades, not revolution or military coup. Each crisis pushed the PRI toward more genuine competition.
1985
Mexico City earthquake — government failure built independent civic organizations outside PRI control for the first time. Civil society capacity grew without state direction
1988
Cárdenas likely won — PRI committed massive fraud ("se cayó el sistema"). Delegitimized PRI rule and created pressure for genuine electoral reform
1994
Zapatista uprising (NAFTA day one) + peso crisis + Colosio assassination. Triple crisis exposed PRI's inability to manage modernity and created space for reform
1996 ★
IFE Reform — the decisive step. Electoral administration removed from the Interior Ministry (PRI-controlled) and given to a genuinely independent Federal Electoral Institute. Before this, the ruling party counted its own votes. This single institutional change made a credible PRI defeat possible
2000
Vicente Fox (PAN) wins — first non-PRI president in 71 years. PRI accepts defeat peacefully. Democratic transition completed through the ballot box
📌 AP EXAM TIP
The 1996 IFE reform is THE key institutional change. Core argument: independent electoral commissions are what make transitions credible, because they remove the ruling party's ability to count its own votes. On argument essays about democratic transition, the IFE is your Mexico evidence for why institutional reform is the decisive proximate cause.
AMLO, MORENA & Democratic Backsliding
Populism, the Fourth Transformation, and Institutional Erosion
AMLO (2018–2024) won with 53% on a platform of radical transformation. While delivering genuine social programs, his presidency raised concerns about institutional erosion — degrading the independent bodies that enable democracy without overtly seizing power.
›
INE attacks: Attempted to replace INE leadership with loyalists ("Plan B" reform); SCJN struck it down. Attacking the electoral institute echoes the PRI's original control of elections
›
2024 Judicial Reform: Introduced popular election of Supreme Court judges — presented as democratization, but critics argue it creates a judiciary loyal to the majority party rather than independent constitutional review
›
Militarization: Military took over customs, ports, infrastructure, and the National Guard — reversing civilian control norms and concentrating coercive capacity under executive direction
›
Counter-evidence: Elections remained competitive; Sheinbaum won 2024 with 59%; no arrests of opposition leaders; SCJN did block several reforms. Mexico is gradual institutional erosion, not sudden authoritarian reversal
📌 AP EXAM TIP
Democratic backsliding does not require a coup. Mexico shows how democracies erode gradually through legal means — weakening independent institutions, packing courts, using popular mandates to centralize power. The Freedom House score drop from ~65 (2018) to ~60 (2023) quantifies this shift. Same pattern as Hungary and Turkey.
Ongoing Structural Challenges
CARTEL VIOLENCE AND STATE SOVEREIGNTY
Where cartels control territory, extort businesses, assassinate politicians and journalists, and corrupt officials, they challenge the Weberian monopoly on legitimate violence. ~30,000+ murders annually. In some states cartel power exceeds state authority — a formally democratic state failing the basic test of territorial control. No president since Calderón has resolved this structural challenge.
NAFTA/USMCA AND ECONOMIC INTEGRATION
NAFTA (1994) → USMCA (2020) deeply integrated Mexico into North American supply chains. Mexico is now the US's largest trading partner. Economic integration pressured democratic transition and reshaped domestic politics. Crucially: USMCA labor provisions explicitly required independent unions — undermining the PRI-era corporatist model from the outside.
Common AP Exam Mistakes — Mexico
⚠️
Calling the PRI system a "one-party state." Mexico under the PRI had elections and opposition parties. It was a hegemonic party system — softer authoritarianism than China or the Soviet Union.
⚠️
Saying Mexico transitioned because of economic development alone. The decisive factor was the 1996 IFE reform creating an independent electoral body. Civil society and economic pressure created demand; the IFE provided the mechanism.
⚠️
Treating Mexico as either "fully consolidated" or "failed." It's an intermediate case — 20+ years of competitive elections, but declining Freedom House scores. Use precise language: "incomplete consolidation" or "partial backsliding."
⚠️
Forgetting the sexenio details: 6-year term, no presidential reelection. Get both the number and constraint right. The no reelección rule applies to the president, not to Congress.
Exam Strategies — Mexico Questions
🎯
Mexico as the transition model: For questions about democratic transitions, electoral reform, or incomplete consolidation — Mexico is your primary example. Its gradual electoral route contrasts with sudden collapses (Nigeria's military exits) and failed transitions (Russia).
🎯
Mexico vs. Russia comparison: Both had dominant-party systems collapse in the 1990s — but Mexico produced genuine multiparty competition while Russia produced competitive authoritarianism. Key contrast: Mexico's IFE was independent; Russia's electoral administration stayed under executive control.
🎯
Quantitative Analysis: Mexico's Freedom House trajectory (20→66→60) is your evidence for gradual democratization followed by partial backsliding. The 2000 inflection point (score jump from ~45 to ~55) marks the critical juncture of the Fox victory.
🎯
Argument Essay: For any question about electoral institutions and democratic transition, Mexico is your strongest evidence that independent electoral administration is the decisive mechanism. Pair with Russia (which lacked it) for the strongest comparative argument.
FRQ Connections — Mexico Topics Most Likely to Appear
✍️
Conceptual Analysis: Define corporatism and explain how PRI used it — requires specific mechanisms (CTM, CNC, exchange of recognition for loyalty) not just "the state organized groups."
✍️
Quantitative Analysis: Mexico's Freedom House trend data shows gradual democratization (1970→2000) followed by partial backsliding (2018→2023). Know the specific scores and what caused each inflection point.
✍️
Comparative Analysis: Mexico vs. Russia on democratic transition outcomes. The key contrast is institutional: Mexico built a genuinely independent INE; Russia's electoral administration remained under Kremlin influence. Both held elections — only Mexico's were credible.
✍️
Argument Essay: For electoral institutions and democratic transition, Mexico is your primary evidence that independent electoral bodies are the decisive proximate cause. The IFE reform (not economic development or civil society alone) enabled the 2000 transition.

Key Institutions
🏛️
President
Head of State and Government
Directly elected for a single 6-year term (sexenio) with no reelection. Commands armed forces, appoints cabinet. Currently Claudia Sheinbaum (MORENA, 2024) — Mexico's first female president.
📜
Congress of the Union
Bicameral Legislature
Chamber of Deputies (500 seats): 300 by FPTP, 200 by proportional representation. Senate (128 seats): 3 per state plus 32 PR seats. Mixed system prevents PRI-style monopolization.
⚖️
Supreme Court (SCJN)
Suprema Corte de Justicia
Increasingly assertive since 2000. The 2024 judicial reform changed selection to popular election, threatening independence. Historically weak under PRI due to executive dominance.
🗳️
INE (Electoral Institute)
Instituto Nacional Electoral
Independent body overseeing all federal elections. Created in 1996 as IFE — the key institution enabling Mexico's 2000 democratic transition. AMLO controversially tried to weaken it in 2022.
🏛️
PRI
The "Perfect Dictatorship"
Dominated Mexican politics 1929-2000 through corporatism, patron-client networks, and electoral fraud. Lost presidency in 2000. Now a minor party.
🌹
MORENA
Movimiento Regeneracion Nacional
Founded by AMLO in 2014. Won 2018 landslide. Left-populist party continuing the "Fourth Transformation" agenda under Claudia Sheinbaum (2024).
