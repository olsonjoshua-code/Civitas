United Kingdom — Parliamentary Democracy & the Westminster Model
The UK is the AP Comparative course's primary example of a consolidated liberal democracy, parliamentary government, and gradual constitutional evolution without a single codified document. Understanding the UK means understanding parliamentary sovereignty, fusion of powers, and the tension between democratic tradition and modern rights protection.
🏛️ Constitutional Monarchy / Parliamentary Democracy
🗺️ Unitary State (with devolution)
📋 Uncodified Constitution
🗳️ FPTP Electoral System
Constitutional Structure & Parliamentary Sovereignty
The Uncodified Constitution and the Supremacy of Parliament
The UK has no single written constitutional document. Its constitution is spread across Acts of Parliament (Magna Carta 1215, Bill of Rights 1689, Parliament Acts 1911/1949), common law, and constitutional conventions — unwritten but binding political norms. Parliamentary sovereignty is the foundational principle: Parliament can pass any law it chooses, and no court can void it.
›
Supreme Court limits: Can declare legislation "incompatible" with the Human Rights Act — but cannot strike it down. Parliament must then decide whether to amend. Contrast with US where SCOTUS can invalidate laws
›
Constitutional conventions: Unwritten rules with real binding force — e.g., the monarch must appoint the leader of the largest Commons party as PM; Cabinet ministers must publicly support collective decisions
›
Miller case (2019): Supreme Court ruled 11-0 that Boris Johnson's prorogation of Parliament was unlawful — Parliament's sovereignty cannot be circumvented by executive action even using prerogative powers
›
Human Rights Act 1998: Incorporated the European Convention on Human Rights into UK law — the closest thing to a bill of rights, but technically repealable by Parliament like any other statute
📌 AP EXAM TIP
The most tested UK distinction: parliamentary sovereignty vs. judicial review. UK courts can declare incompatibility — they cannot invalidate. On MCQs, "which best describes UK judicial power?" → declare incompatibility, not strike down. On FRQs, this is the key mechanism that distinguishes the UK from the US and Germany.
Fusion of Powers, PM Dominance & Accountability
The Westminster Model: Responsible Government
Unlike the US separation of powers, the UK has a fusion of powers: the Prime Minister and Cabinet ARE Members of Parliament, drawn from and accountable to it. The PM must maintain the confidence of the Commons majority. When the PM has a large majority, party discipline means Parliament rarely checks the executive — Lord Hailsham's "elected dictatorship" critique.
Prime Minister's Questions (PMQs)
Every Wednesday, PM faces questions from MPs including the Leader of the Opposition. Primary accountability mechanism — but with a large majority, party discipline limits its effectiveness
Vote of No Confidence
If Commons passes a no-confidence motion, PM must resign or call an election within 14 days. Used in 2019 against May/Johnson. The legislature can remove the executive — cannot in US system
Collective Cabinet Responsibility
All Cabinet ministers must publicly support government decisions or resign. Creates unified executive front — but can suppress genuine deliberation within government
Select Committees
Cross-party Commons committees scrutinizing each department. Can call ministers and officials to testify. Growing in importance as a check — but recommendations are non-binding
📌 AP EXAM TIP
PMQs is your go-to UK accountability example on FRQs — but always note its limits. With a large majority, party whipping makes it theatrical. The deeper accountability is the vote of no confidence and internal party mechanisms (MPs can remove their own leader — May 2019, Johnson 2022). These are more powerful than PMQs in practice.
FPTP Electoral System & Party Politics
First-Past-the-Post: Disproportionality and Stability
FPTP in 650 single-member constituencies produces strong single-party majorities from pluralities and severely underrepresents parties with geographically dispersed support. The 2024 election produced the most disproportional result in modern British history: Labour won 33.7% of votes but 63% of seats (411/650); Reform UK won 14.3% of votes but only 0.8% of seats (5/650) — a 13.5 point penalty for dispersed support.
›
Advantage — stable government: FPTP typically produces single-party majority governments that can govern decisively without coalition negotiations; clear accountability (voters know who to blame)
›
Disadvantage — wasted votes: Millions of votes for losing candidates or surplus majority votes count for nothing; geographic concentration rewarded over national support
›
Two-party dominance: Labour and Conservatives have alternated in power for a century, though SNP, Lib Dems, and now Reform show growing fragmentation at the vote level even if not in seats
›
Devolved systems differ: Scottish Parliament and Welsh Senedd use proportional representation (AMS), producing more fragmented multiparty legislatures and requiring coalition or minority governments
📌 AP EXAM TIP
On Quantitative Analysis FRQs, the 2024 UK election data is your best example of FPTP disproportionality. Labour 33.7% → 63% of seats = massive bonus. Reform 14.3% → 0.8% of seats = massive penalty. The mechanism: FPTP rewards geographic concentration of votes, not national popularity. Always state the specific percentages.
Devolution — Unitary State with Asymmetric Decentralization
Since 1997–1998, the UK devolved significant powers to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland — but remains a unitary state. Parliament created these bodies through ordinary legislation and could theoretically abolish them. This is the critical distinction from federalism: in federal systems (US, Germany), subnational powers are constitutionally entrenched; in the UK they are not.
SCOTLAND
Scottish Parliament (Holyrood, 1999) has primary legislative power over health, education, justice, taxes. SNP has dominated since 2007. 2014 independence referendum: 55% No. 2016: Scotland voted 62% Remain — creating ongoing tension over independence and Brexit.
N. IRELAND
Stormont Assembly (1998 Good Friday Agreement) uses consociational power-sharing: both unionist (pro-UK) and nationalist (pro-united Ireland) communities must participate in government. First Minister and Deputy First Minister from opposite traditions. Stormont has collapsed multiple times — most recently 2022–2024 over Brexit's Northern Ireland Protocol.
WALES
Welsh Senedd (1999) has expanded powers over health, education, and some economic policy. Uses proportional representation. More limited autonomy than Scotland — Wales historically more integrated with England.
📌 AP EXAM TIP
Devolution ≠ federalism. The exam regularly tests this. Key distinction: in federalism, subnational powers are constitutionally protected and the central government cannot unilaterally abolish them. In the UK, Westminster created the devolved bodies through statute and retains theoretical sovereignty. Northern Ireland's consociationalism (power-sharing across communal groups) is a separate concept worth knowing for comparative questions about conflict management.
Brexit and Social Cleavages
The 2016 Brexit referendum (52% Leave) exposed a deep new values cleavage cutting across traditional class and party lines — one of the most significant political realignments in modern British history. Brexit also demonstrated parliamentary sovereignty in action: Parliament, not the referendum result or the executive, controlled the process.
›
Age cleavage: 75% of 18–24s voted Remain; 64% of 65+ voted Leave — the sharpest generational divide in modern British electoral history
›
Education cleavage: University graduates voted heavily Remain; those with secondary education or less voted predominantly Leave — education replacing class as a key political predictor
›
Geographic cleavage: London, Scotland, and university cities voted Remain; English towns and rural areas voted Leave — a metropolitan vs. provincial divide
›
Parliamentary sovereignty confirmed: The 2017 Miller case required Parliament to authorize Article 50; Parliament then controlled the entire Brexit process despite executive preferences — a direct demonstration of the foundational constitutional principle
Common AP Exam Mistakes — UK
⚠️
Saying the UK Supreme Court can "strike down" laws. It cannot. It can only declare incompatibility. Parliament retains sovereignty to ignore that declaration (though it rarely does politically).
⚠️
Calling UK devolution "federalism." Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland have devolved powers but Westminster remains sovereign and created these bodies through ordinary statute law.
⚠️
Treating PMQs as the primary accountability mechanism without noting its limits. With a large majority, it is largely theatrical. Internal party mechanisms (removing the leader) are stronger in practice.
⚠️
Confusing the monarch's formal powers with real power. King Charles appoints the PM, gives Royal Assent, opens Parliament — but exercises all these only on ministerial advice. Real power lies with the elected government.
⚠️
Saying "the UK has no constitution." It has an uncodified constitution — constitutional rules exist, they're just not in one document. The distinction matters: "uncodified" not "unwritten" (much of it is written in statutes).
Exam Strategies — UK Questions
🎯
The UK as the democratic baseline: In comparatives, the UK is almost always the "free, democratic" anchor against which authoritarian regimes (Russia, China, Iran) are measured. Know what makes the UK democratic: competitive elections, civil liberties, rule of law, accountable government, peaceful power transfers.
🎯
Parliamentary vs. presidential: The UK is your primary parliamentary system example. For any question comparing parliamentary and presidential systems, use UK vs. US (or UK vs. Mexico's presidential system). Key contrasts: fusion vs. separation of powers; confidence requirement vs. fixed terms; parliamentary sovereignty vs. judicial review.
🎯
Specific examples score points: "The UK holds elections" = 0 pts. "In the 2024 UK election, Labour won 33.7% of votes but 63% of seats due to FPTP's bonus for geographic concentration" = 1 pt. Always attach dates, percentages, and specific mechanisms.
🎯
Argument essay anchor: The UK is your strongest democratic evidence source. For questions about legitimacy, accountability, civil liberties, or electoral systems — UK + one authoritarian country (China, Russia, Iran) builds the strongest comparative argument structure.
FRQ Connections — UK Topics Most Likely to Appear
✍️
Conceptual Analysis: Define parliamentary sovereignty OR parliamentary system — requires the specific mechanism (Parliament can pass any law; court can only declare incompatibility; PM must maintain confidence) not just a general description.
✍️
Quantitative Analysis: 2024 election FPTP data is your best example. Know: Labour 33.7% votes → 63% seats; Reform 14.3% votes → 0.8% seats. Explain the mechanism: FPTP rewards geographic concentration; dispersed national support earns few seats.
✍️
Comparative Analysis: UK vs. Russia on accountability is the most common comparative. Key: UK PM faces PMQs weekly AND can be removed by Parliament; Russia's president controls the Duma through United Russia and faces no effective accountability. Both have formal legislative mechanisms — only the UK's are real.
✍️
Argument Essay: UK works best as evidence for democratic legitimacy, rule of law, and electoral accountability. Brexit demonstrates both the strength of parliamentary sovereignty (Parliament controlled the process) and a limitation of direct democracy (a 52% referendum result caused years of political instability).

Key Institutions at a Glance
👑
The Monarch
Head of State
Ceremonial role. King Charles III. Formally appoints PM, gives Royal Assent, opens Parliament. Exercises power only on ministerial advice. “The King reigns but does not rule.”
🏛️
Prime Minister
Head of Government
Leader of the majority party. Heads the Cabinet. Controls government through party discipline. Currently Keir Starmer (Labour, elected 2024).
📜
House of Commons
Lower House · 650 MPs
The elected chamber. Source of democratic legitimacy. Passes legislation, scrutinizes government. Elected via FPTP in single-member constituencies.
📚
House of Lords
Upper House · ~800 peers
Unelected revising chamber. Life peers, hereditary peers, Lords Spiritual. Can delay but not block legislation (Parliament Acts 1911 and 1949).
⚖️
UK Supreme Court
Est. 2009
Replaced the Law Lords. Cannot strike down Acts of Parliament (parliamentary sovereignty). Can declare laws incompatible with Human Rights Act.
🗺️
Devolved Assemblies
Scotland · Wales · N. Ireland
Scottish Parliament, Welsh Senedd, Northern Ireland Assembly. Powers delegated by Parliament through legislation. Parliament retains sovereignty.
