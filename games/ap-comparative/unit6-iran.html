Iran — Theocratic Hybrid Regime & Islamic Governance
Iran is the AP Comparative course's only theocracy — a regime where religious authority formally supersedes electoral legitimacy. Understanding Iran means understanding how a system can hold genuine elections while ensuring those elections cannot produce outcomes the regime finds unacceptable.
🕌 Theocratic Republic (hybrid)
🗺️ Unitary State
📋 Constitution: 1979 (revised 1989)
🛢️ Oil-dependent; rentier state
Regime Type: Theocratic Hybrid
Velayat-e Faqih: The Guardianship of the Islamic Jurist
Iran's political system is built on Khomeini's doctrine of velayat-e faqih — guardianship of the Islamic jurist — which holds that a senior Shia cleric has the divine right and duty to govern until the return of the Hidden Imam. This gives the Supreme Leader ultimate authority over all branches of government, military forces, and judicial appointments. Elected institutions (president, Majles) have real policymaking power but cannot challenge the fundamental theocratic framework.
›
Supreme Leader (Faqih): Ali Khamenei since 1989. Commands armed forces, appoints judiciary, controls state media, can dismiss president. No term limit, no election. The apex of all state power — formal and informal
›
Democratic elements: Directly elected president (4-year terms, max 2), elected Majles (parliament), Assembly of Experts (elected clerics who theoretically supervise Supreme Leader), direct referendums. Real competition exists between approved factions
›
The fundamental constraint: Elections produce genuine competition — but only between regime-approved options. No candidate who genuinely challenges theocratic rule can appear on a ballot. The system is a hybrid because it has both real elections AND unelected veto institutions
📌 AP EXAM TIP
The most tested Iran distinction: FORMAL democracy (elected president, Majles, direct vote) vs. ACTUAL theocracy (Guardian Council filters who can run; Supreme Leader holds veto over all outcomes). On FRQs, always explain both layers. "Iran holds elections" alone is incomplete — you must explain the Guardian Council constraint that limits what those elections can produce.
Key Institutions: The Architecture of Theocratic Control
Guardian Council ★
12 members — 6 Islamic jurists appointed by Supreme Leader + 6 lawyers from judiciary (also controlled by SL). Two powers: (1) vets all candidates, disqualifying thousands before each election; (2) can reject any legislation as un-Islamic. The chokepoint of theocratic control
Majles (Parliament)
290 elected members. Passes legislation, approves cabinet, questions ministers. Genuine debates and political competition — but Guardian Council can veto anything as un-Islamic. Currently dominated by hardliners after 2020 mass disqualifications
IRGC (Revolutionary Guards)
Controls 20-40% of Iran's economy (construction, telecoms, oil). Commands Basij militia that enforces social codes and suppresses protests. Financially independent of elected government — reports to Supreme Leader. Powerful constituency for maintaining the current system
Expediency Council
Resolves disputes between Majles and Guardian Council; advises Supreme Leader on policy. Appointed by Supreme Leader. Another layer of non-elected oversight that can override parliamentary decisions
📌 AP EXAM TIP
The Guardian Council is Iran's most important institution for the AP exam. Know both its powers: candidate vetting (prevents genuine opposition from competing) AND legislative review (prevents elected parliament from passing laws the Supreme Leader opposes). Together these create circular control — the Supreme Leader controls the Council, the Council controls who can be elected and what they can do.
Factionalism: Real Competition Within Regime Limits
Despite theocratic constraints, Iran has genuine political competition between reformist and hardliner factions. This is one of Iran's most distinctive features compared to China or North Korea — intra-regime conflict is real and produces different policy outcomes.
1997
KHATAMI
Reformist elected with 69% — genuine popular surge for political opening. Briefly expanded press freedom; attempted diplomatic engagement. Guardian Council blocked most legislative reforms; hardliners used judiciary to shut newspapers. Showed the ceiling of reformist power: can win elections, cannot change fundamental rules
2009
GREEN MVT
Disputed Ahmadinejad re-election (officially 63% vs Mousavi's 34%) triggered the largest protests since 1979 — millions in streets. Record 85% turnout made the official result implausible. Mousavi placed under house arrest. Basij and IRGC suppressed protests. Key AP lesson: elections can become sites of genuine contestation when manipulation is too visible
2015
JCPOA
Rouhani (pragmatist) negotiated nuclear deal with US/EU. Shows reformists can achieve major policy outcomes within the system. US withdrew 2018 (Trump); Iran resumed nuclear enrichment. JCPOA illustrates how factional competition produces real foreign policy differences
2022
MAHSA
"Woman, Life, Freedom" protests after Mahsa Amini (22) dies in morality police custody for alleged improper hijab. Nationwide protests — the deepest challenge to the Islamic Republic since 1979. Suppressed with lethal force. Exposed the generational chasm: younger urban Iranians reject the revolution's restrictions entirely
Common AP Exam Mistakes — Iran
⚠️
Saying Iran has "no elections" or is "just a dictatorship." Iran holds genuine elections with real competition between factions. The key is the Guardian Council vetting that prevents anti-regime candidates from running — not the absence of elections.
⚠️
Confusing the Supreme Leader and the President. The president is elected and handles domestic/foreign policy. The Supreme Leader (Khamenei) holds ultimate authority over all institutions including the military and judiciary — and is not elected by general population.
⚠️
Treating Iran as equivalent to China or Russia. Iran is uniquely theocratic — its unelected authority is religious, not party-based (China) or personalist-nationalist (Russia). The Guardian Council is Iran-specific; no equivalent exists in the other course countries.
⚠️
Saying the IRGC is "just a military." The IRGC controls a major share of Iran's economy, commands the Basij militia, and operates independently of the elected government. It is a state-within-a-state with economic and political interests in maintaining the current system.
Exam Strategies — Iran Questions
🎯
Iran as the hybrid/theocracy model: For any question about hybrid regimes, religious authority in politics, or elections that don't produce democracy — Iran is your primary example. Its structure (real elections + Guardian Council veto) is unique among the six countries.
🎯
Always name the institution: "Iran limits elections" = 0 pts. "Iran's Guardian Council disqualifies candidates before ballots are printed, ensuring only regime-approved options appear" = 1 pt. The Guardian Council is your go-to specific mechanism for almost every Iran question.
🎯
Turnout as legitimacy signal: Iran's presidential turnout data (79%→40%) is your quantitative evidence for how Guardian Council filtering correlates with declining electoral legitimacy. The 2021 and 2024 elections — where reformists were largely disqualified — both produced record-low turnout.
🎯
Argument Essay anchor: For religion and politics questions, Iran is your evidence that religious legitimacy and democratic legitimacy are fundamentally incompatible when religious authority holds veto power over electoral outcomes. The Guardian Council embodies this incompatibility.
FRQ Connections — Iran Topics Most Likely to Appear
✍️
Conceptual Analysis: Explain how Iran combines theocratic and democratic elements — requires naming BOTH sides and the specific Guardian Council mechanism that ensures theocratic elements override democratic ones when they conflict.
✍️
Quantitative Analysis: Iran's turnout data (79%→40%) shows correlation between Guardian Council filtering and declining legitimacy. The 2009 spike (85%) shows that genuine competition produces high participation; 2021/2024 lows show what happens when reformists are disqualified en masse.
✍️
Comparative Analysis: Iran vs. China on authoritarian governance — both authoritarian, but different mechanisms: Iran uses religious vetting + elections; China uses no elections + CCP monopoly. Iran's legitimacy is theocratic; China's is performance-based. Iran has more internal factional competition; China has more comprehensive information control.
✍️
Argument Essay: Religion and democratic legitimacy — Iran is your primary evidence that religious veto institutions are incompatible with genuine democratic accountability. The Guardian Council overrides electoral outcomes in the name of divine authority, placing clerical judgment above popular consent.

Key Institutions
☥️
Supreme Leader (Faqih)
Head of State · Unelected
Apex of all power. Ayatollah Khamenei since 1989. Commands armed forces including IRGC; appoints Guardian Council jurists, judiciary chief, state media. Serves for life. Authority derives from velayat-e faqih (guardianship of the Islamic jurist).
🏛️
President
Head of Government · Elected
Directly elected; 4-year term, max 2 consecutive. Must be Shia Muslim male approved by Guardian Council. Manages cabinet and implements laws. Currently Masoud Pezeshkian (2024, reformist). Real power limited by Supreme Leader's authority.
📜
Majles (Parliament)
290 seats · Unicameral
Directly elected; passes legislation, approves budget, can impeach president. All candidates vetted by Guardian Council. Laws must pass Guardian Council review. Increasingly conservative since 2020 mass disqualifications.
⚖️
Guardian Council
12 members · Unelected
Most powerful institution after Supreme Leader. Vets ALL election candidates. Reviews ALL legislation for Islamic compliance. 6 jurists appointed by Supreme Leader + 6 lawyers from judiciary (also Supreme Leader controlled).
⚔️
IRGC
Revolutionary Guard · State-Within-State
Parallel military created in 1979. Controls 20-40% of Iran's economy. Commands Basij militia. Designated terrorist organization by US (2019). Enforces domestic order and suppresses protests.
📚
Assembly of Experts
88 clerics · Filtered Election
Theoretically appoints and can remove Supreme Leader. In practice has never challenged one. Candidates vetted by Guardian Council — circular control: Supreme Leader controls who can evaluate him.
