AP COMPARATIVE GOVERNMENT · IRAN
Theocratic Hybrid Regime and Islamic Governance
Iran is the AP Comparative course's primary example of a theocracy — a regime where religious authority supersedes electoral legitimacy — and a hybrid system that combines genuine elections with systematic constraints that prevent democratic outcomes.
Regime Type: Theocratic Hybrid
Iran's system is built on velayat-e faqih (guardianship of the Islamic jurist) — Khomeini's doctrine that a senior cleric has the divine right and duty to govern until the return of the Hidden Imam. This gives the Supreme Leader ultimate authority over all branches of government, military forces, and judicial appointments. The elected president and Majles operate within this theocratic framework — they have real power over policy implementation but cannot challenge the fundamental basis of Islamic governance.
Iran is classified as a hybrid regime because elections genuinely happen — turnout can be high (85% in 2009), genuine factional competition exists, and electoral outcomes sometimes produce reformists. But the Guardian Council vets all candidates before they appear on ballots, preventing anyone who genuinely challenges theocratic rule from competing.
AP Exam tip: The key distinction is between Iran's FORMAL democracy (elected president, parliament, direct vote) and its ACTUAL theocracy (Guardian Council filters who can run; Supreme Leader holds ultimate power over all institutions). Elections produce real outcomes within regime-defined parameters.
The Guardian Council: The Chokepoint
The Guardian Council (12 members — 6 Islamic jurists appointed by Supreme Leader, 6 lawyers from judiciary also controlled by Supreme Leader) exercises two critical powers: (1) candidate vetting — disqualifying thousands of candidates before every election, including most reformists; (2) legislative review — any bill can be rejected as un-Islamic regardless of parliamentary vote. This creates a circular control: the Supreme Leader controls the Council, the Council controls who can stand for election and what laws can pass.
IRGC: The State Within the State
The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) controls 20-40% of Iran's economy through business conglomerates in construction, telecoms, and oil — making it financially independent of and potentially more powerful than the conventional government. It commands the Basij militia that enforces social codes and suppresses protests. The IRGC's economic interests create a powerful constituency for maintaining the current system regardless of electoral outcomes.
Civil Society and Protest
Despite constraints, Iran has significant civil society — women's organizations, professional associations, reformist newspapers (when permitted), student movements. The Green Movement (2009) and Mahsa Amini protests (2022) showed the capacity for massive mobilization, especially among younger, urban Iranians. Both were suppressed with lethal force. The generational divide — between older Iranians who remember the revolution and younger Iranians who reject its restrictions — is the deepest social cleavage in contemporary Iran.

Key Institutions
☥️
Supreme Leader (Faqih)
Head of State · Unelected
Apex of all power. Ayatollah Khamenei since 1989. Commands armed forces including IRGC; appoints Guardian Council jurists, judiciary chief, state media. Serves for life. Authority derives from velayat-e faqih (guardianship of the Islamic jurist).
🏛️
President
Head of Government · Elected
Directly elected; 4-year term, max 2 consecutive. Must be Shia Muslim male approved by Guardian Council. Manages cabinet and implements laws. Currently Masoud Pezeshkian (2024, reformist). Real power limited by Supreme Leader's authority.
📜
Majles (Parliament)
290 seats · Unicameral
Directly elected; passes legislation, approves budget, can impeach president. All candidates vetted by Guardian Council. Laws must pass Guardian Council review. Increasingly conservative since 2020 mass disqualifications.
⚖️
Guardian Council
12 members · Unelected
Most powerful institution after Supreme Leader. Vets ALL election candidates. Reviews ALL legislation for Islamic compliance. 6 jurists appointed by Supreme Leader + 6 lawyers from judiciary (also Supreme Leader controlled).
⚔️
IRGC
Revolutionary Guard · State-Within-State
Parallel military created in 1979. Controls 20-40% of Iran's economy. Commands Basij militia. Designated terrorist organization by US (2019). Enforces domestic order and suppresses protests.
📚
Assembly of Experts
88 clerics · Filtered Election
Theoretically appoints and can remove Supreme Leader. In practice has never challenged one. Candidates vetted by Guardian Council — circular control: Supreme Leader controls who can evaluate him.
