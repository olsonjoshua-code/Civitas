Lesson 31: Hoover to Roosevelt

A student study file on how the deepening Depression exposed the limits of Hoover's response, pushed voters toward Franklin Roosevelt, and opened the door to broader federal experimentation in the first phase of the New Deal.

    Study Guide

    Essential Question

    Why did the public shift from Hoover to Roosevelt during the Depression, and how did that change reflect new expectations about what the federal government should do in a national economic emergency?

    Key Terms

    Herbert Hoover
    voluntary cooperation
    Reconstruction Finance Corporation
    Boulder Dam
    Bonus Army
    Franklin D. Roosevelt
    New Deal
    First Hundred Days

    What to Watch For

    1. How Hoover's belief in limited federal relief and voluntary cooperation shaped his response to the Depression.
    2. How worsening unemployment and hardship made measures like public works and institutional loans seem too small to many Americans.
    3. How the Bonus Army became a powerful symbol of national suffering and political damage for Hoover.
    4. How Roosevelt's campaign and First Hundred Days signaled a broader federal role and a willingness to experiment.

    Study Tip

    Break the lesson into three stages: Hoover's limits, the political turning point of 1932, and Roosevelt's early burst of action. That sequence makes it easier to track why public expectations about government changed so sharply.
