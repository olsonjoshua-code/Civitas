Unit 6: Affluence, Inequality, and Civil Rights

Use these briefing slides to study the contradictions of postwar America. This unit follows the prosperity of the 1950s, the persistence of poverty and segregation, the rise of the civil rights movement, and the widening struggles for equality that reshaped the nation.

Overview

What this unit is about

This unit explores how postwar America could appear prosperous and stable while still marked by poverty, segregation, and exclusion. It traces the struggle for civil rights from early challenges to Jim Crow through mass protest, Black Power, affirmative action, and broader movements for equality among women, Latinos, Indigenous Americans, Asian Americans, LGBT Americans, older Americans, and Americans with disabilities.

Study Guide

Unit Focus

How could the United States experience postwar prosperity while still facing poverty, segregation, and powerful movements demanding equality?

What to Notice

  • How affluence expanded in the 1950s, but did not reach all Americans equally.
  • How poverty and segregation challenged the image of postwar prosperity.
  • How civil rights activists used protest, nonviolence, organizing, and legal change to force reform.
  • How the struggle for equality widened in the late 1960s and 1970s to include many more groups and goals.

Study Tip

Think of this unit as a tension between image and reality. The image is peace, prosperity, and progress. The reality is that many Americans were still excluded. Follow how those contradictions produced protest movements that pushed the nation closer to its ideals, even as new debates over equality continued.
