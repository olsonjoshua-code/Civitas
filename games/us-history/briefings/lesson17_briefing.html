Lesson 17: The Progressives Respond

A student study file on how Progressives moved from exposing problems to acting on them, tracing settlement houses, labor protection, child welfare, direct democracy, women’s suffrage, state reform, and civil rights activism.

    Study Guide

    Essential Question

    How did Progressives try to use community action, government reform, and new political rights to respond to the problems of industrial America?

    Key Terms

    settlement house
    factory inspection
    initiative
    referendum
    recall
    direct primary
    suffrage
    NAACP

    What to Watch For

    1. How settlement houses and neighborhood aid tried to answer the daily hardships of immigrant urban life.
    2. How labor and child-welfare reformers pushed for safer workplaces, school attendance, and public protection.
    3. How direct democracy reforms challenged party bosses and opened more routes for voter influence.
    4. How women’s suffrage and the NAACP broadened reform while also exposing the limits of the Progressive Era.

    Study Tip

    Pair each reform with the problem it answered: settlement work with tenements, inspectors with unsafe factories, primaries with machine politics, suffrage with broader reform, and the NAACP with racial injustice. That pairing will keep the lesson organized.
